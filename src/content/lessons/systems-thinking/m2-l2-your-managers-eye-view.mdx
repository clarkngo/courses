## A Second Lens: Borrow Your Manager's View

The one-click-out technique from the last lesson works on a single task. This lesson gives you a second, complementary lens: evaluate your work by imagining you're the person one level above you, looking at it.

That doesn't mean second-guessing your manager's decisions. It means noticing what changes when you stop looking only at your own team, your own metric, or your own piece of the problem — and start looking at how product, engineering, and the other functions around you (finance, content, support, whatever touches your work) actually have to fit together for the outcome to land. Your manager's job, structurally, is to hold that wider view. Borrowing it — even briefly — is a shortcut to seeing your own work the way the rest of the system will experience it.

This is the same idea Russell Ackoff pointed at from the [first lesson](/systems-thinking/lessons/m1-l1-what-systems-thinking-actually-means): the whole is the product of how the parts interact, not the sum of how well each part performs alone. Your manager's-eye view is a practical way to check for that interaction before someone else has to catch it for you.

<CodeLab title="Run Your Task Through It">

Take the same task from your running example (or the export-button example from the last lesson). Ask:

1. If my manager saw only the *outcome* of this work — not my effort, not my reasoning — would they see something that fits cleanly with what the rest of the team is doing this quarter, or something that has to be explained and reconciled later?
2. Which other function's work does this touch, even indirectly — and would that function be surprised by what I built?

If either answer makes you uneasy, that's useful information now, while the work is still cheap to adjust — not after it ships.

</CodeLab>

## Prioritize Your Colleagues and the Future

There's a second half to this lens: systems thinking isn't just about seeing the current picture more broadly — it's about leaving the system better than you found it. Two follow-up questions do most of the work:

- **Will this be useful to my colleagues**, or does it only serve my immediate, local need?
- **Am I leaving behind a stronger system** — something that makes the *next* piece of work easier — or a slightly more tangled one?

Neither question demands a heroic answer. A well-named function, a documented assumption, a small piece of logic extracted into something reusable — these are modest, and they compound. The habit of asking is the entire skill; the individual answers are usually small.

<Pitfall title="Waiting for Permission to Zoom Out">

It's easy to assume this level of thinking is reserved for people with "senior," "staff," or "lead" in their title — that asking these questions above your pay grade will read as overstepping. In practice, the opposite is usually true: a person early in their career who consistently asks "does this fit the bigger picture, and does it help the next person" reads as *more* trustworthy with scope, not less. You don't need permission to ask a question that makes your own work better.

</Pitfall>

## The Rule of Thumb: Don't Get Stuck Questioning

Here's the caveat this course keeps repeating, because it's the failure mode that actually happens once people learn these techniques: **do not spend too long in the questioning state.**

Zooming out is meant to take a quick beat — a few minutes of honest reflection — not an extended detour that delays the work. If you find yourself still turning a task over in your head after that first pass, still generating new hypothetical use cases, still waiting for more clarity before you'll commit to a direction, you've crossed from systems thinking into stalling. The goal was never to resolve every uncertainty about the broader system. It was to take one honest look, adjust what's cheap to adjust now, and get back to building.

| Signal you're doing it right | Signal you've gone too far |
|---|---|
| You asked one or two sharp questions and moved | You're still asking new questions an hour later |
| You changed one small thing about your approach | You're waiting for a decision from someone else before you'll start |
| You can say specifically what assumption you checked | You can't point to what, specifically, you're still unsure about |

<Quiz question="You've been zooming out on a task for 45 minutes, generating new questions about how it might connect to other teams' work, and you still haven't started building. According to this lesson, what should you do?">
<div>

**Stop questioning, commit to your best current answer, and start building — this is exactly the paralysis the rule of thumb warns against.**

The techniques in this module are explicitly designed to take minutes, not the better part of an hour. Forty-five minutes of open-ended questioning without a decision is a signal you've left "one click out" and entered indefinite strategic deliberation — which delivers none of the benefit (a shipped, well-fitted piece of work) and all of the cost (lost time). The correct move is to take your best answer from the first few minutes of reflection, adjust what's cheap to adjust, and move forward — you can always revisit if new information genuinely changes things.

</div>
