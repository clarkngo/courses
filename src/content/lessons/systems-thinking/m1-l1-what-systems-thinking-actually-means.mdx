## Who This Course Is For

This course is written for two roles that, increasingly, do the same job under different titles: **product managers** who scope and prioritize work, and **engineers** who build it. If your day-to-day is mostly the ticket in front of you — the feature request, the bug, the specific thing you're responsible for shipping — this course is written for exactly that situation, not as an abstract management theory survey.

You don't need a title with "architect," "staff," or "director" in it to use any of this. The techniques here are things you can apply to the next task on your list, today.

---

## Why This Course Exists Right Now

AI tools have made it faster than ever for one person to go from idea to working prototype — a product manager can now spin up a UI, an engineer can now draft a spec, and the traditional line between those two jobs is blurring. Elizabeth Stone, Netflix's Chief Product and Technology Officer, made this exact point on [Lenny's Podcast](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/netflix-cpto-on-ai-and-the-future) in July 2026: as AI collapses the distance between having an idea and having something built, the skill that matters more — not less — is the ability to see how that one idea fits into everything around it.

That's the gap this course fills. Speed without systems thinking produces more output, faster — and more of it disconnected, duplicated, and incompatible. Netflix's own response wasn't to slow down adoption of AI tools; it was to double down on hiring and developing people who default to zooming out, specifically **because** everyone can now build quickly.

## What "Systems Thinking" Actually Means

Strip away the management-theory language, and systems thinking is one habit: **evaluating a piece of work by how it interacts with everything around it, not just by whether it works in isolation.**

The organizational theorist Russell Ackoff put it more precisely:

> "A system is never the sum of its parts; it's the product of their interaction."

A feature that works perfectly on its own but duplicates what three other teams already built, or that solves today's request while making the next request harder, is a *part* that works — and a *system* that doesn't. Systems thinking is the discipline of checking for that gap before you ship, not after.

## Course Objectives

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

1. **Apply the "one click out" technique** — a fast, repeatable way to question your assumptions about a task without needing to map the entire company strategy first.
2. **Ask the three strategic questions** that separate a one-off fix from a reusable capability, and know when each answer changes your approach.
3. **Evaluate a decision through your manager's-eye view** — seeing how product, engineering, and other functions need to fit together, not just your own piece.
4. **Recognize feedback loops and leverage points** in your own systems, using the classic frameworks (Donella Meadows, Peter Senge) underneath the practical techniques.
5. **Diagnose common system archetypes** — Fixes That Fail, Shifting the Burden — before you become the cautionary example of one.
6. **Explain how Netflix operationalizes systems thinking** at scale, in both engineering (paved paths) and product design (a unified design language), and adapt that logic to your own team's size.

## The Line We Keep Returning To

Every module in this course circles back to one sentence:

> **Your job is never just the ticket in front of you — it's the system that ticket sits inside.**

That doesn't mean every task deserves a strategy offsite. It means every task deserves one honest question about what it connects to before you start building. The rest of this course is about making that question fast, specific, and habitual — not slow, abstract, and occasional.

<Pitfall title="Systems Thinking Is Not Overthinking Every Task">

It's tempting to read "zoom out before you build" as license to spend a day in meetings questioning the strategic merit of a one-line bug fix. That's not the skill this course teaches — it's the failure mode this course warns against repeatedly. The techniques ahead are built to take minutes, not days. If a technique is slowing you down more than it's sharpening your work, you're doing it wrong, not doing it thoroughly.

</Pitfall>

<CodeLab title="Pick Your Running Example">

Before Module 2, choose one real, current task from your actual work — a feature you're scoping, a fix you're about to ship, a decision you're about to make. Write down, in one or two sentences:

1. What you're being asked to build or decide
2. What you're currently assuming about why it matters and who else it affects

Keep this. Every module from here forward gives you a new lens to run this same example through, and Module 5's capstone asks you to apply the full framework to it at once.

**Systems thinking is a habit applied at the scope of whatever you're responsible for — it doesn't require seniority, only the two-minute question of what your specific piece connects to.**

The course explicitly frames this as something you apply to "the next task on your list," not as an executive-only strategic exercise. Someone implementing a single ticket can still ask whether their specific implementation duplicates existing work, or whether it'll need to be redone the moment a second use case shows up.
