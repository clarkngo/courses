## The Same Problem, on the Product Side

Module 4's first lesson covered engineering's version of a familiar problem: many teams solving the same underlying need in incompatible, one-off ways. Product design has its own version of that problem, and it's arrived faster than most teams expected — AI tools now let far more people, including engineers and product managers with no formal design training, prototype and ship a user interface in an afternoon.

That's a genuine gain in speed. It's also a genuine risk to coherence: a product built by many people, each making locally reasonable design decisions with no shared reference point, tends to accumulate into what's sometimes called a "Frankenstein" interface — individually functional pieces stitched together in a way that never reads as one product.

---

## Design as a Framework, Not a Feature List

Netflix's response mirrors the engineering one structurally, even though the artifact is different. Rather than the experience design team reviewing and approving each individual feature one at a time — a bottleneck that gets worse, not better, as more people are building — the team's actual job is to define the overarching framework: what a great Netflix experience *looks and behaves like*, as a system, independent of any one feature.

That framework — templates, a cohesive visual and interaction language, defined patterns for common needs — becomes the paved path for design the same way shared infrastructure is the paved path for engineering. A non-designer prototyping a new feature with AI assistance isn't starting from a blank canvas and hoping their instincts happen to match everyone else's; they're building inside a system that already encodes what "on-brand, on-pattern" looks like, which means the fast path and the coherent path are the same path — exactly the design goal Module 4's first lesson described for infrastructure.

<CodeLab title="The Three Questions, Design Edition">

Netflix's design team encourages the same "step out one click" instinct from Module 2, adapted for product decisions specifically:

1. **What is the larger consumer problem being solved** — not just the feature as specified?
2. **Will this scale across the different things the product actually spans** — for Netflix, that's film, TV, cloud games, and live events; for your product, it's whatever range of contexts your users actually touch?
3. **Am I leaving a stronger, more coherent version of the system behind** — or a one-off pattern the next designer will have to either copy inconsistently or ignore?

Notice these are the same three questions from Module 2, applied to a design decision instead of an engineering one. The technique doesn't change with the artifact — only the specifics of what "scale" and "shared building block" mean.

</CodeLab>

## Empowering Non-Designers Without Losing Coherence

The point of this system isn't to keep non-designers out of design work — AI tools have already made that gate difficult to enforce, and Netflix's stated approach isn't to fight that trend. It's to make sure that when a PM or engineer uses AI to prototype something quickly, the result fits into the end-to-end member experience by default, because the framework they're building inside of already encodes the patterns, rather than relying on that person's individual design judgment alone.

This is the product-design equivalent of the security-and-identity point from the last lesson: you don't want coherence to depend on every single contributor independently having good design instincts, any more than you want security to depend on every engineer remembering to run a review under deadline pressure. You want the default path to already be the good one.

<Pitfall title="Mistaking a Consistent Style Guide for Systems Thinking">

It's tempting to treat "we all use the same button color and font" as evidence the design-systems problem is solved. A style guide is necessary, but it's the parameter-level version of the fix from Module 3's leverage-point hierarchy — it standardizes surface appearance without necessarily standardizing the deeper thing: consistent information architecture, consistent interaction patterns for similar tasks, a shared point of view on what the product is actually *for*. Two features can share every visual token and still feel like they belong to different products, because the deeper design language — not just the visual one — was never actually shared.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="A product manager with no design background uses an AI tool to prototype a new in-app notification feature in an afternoon. It works well on its own, uses the company's standard color palette and fonts, but introduces a new interaction pattern — a swipe-to-dismiss gesture — that doesn't exist anywhere else in the product. Using this lesson's framework, is this feature 'on-system,' and why?">
<div>

**Not fully — it's on-brand (correct colors and fonts) but not necessarily on-pattern, and the interaction-pattern gap is the part a style guide alone won't catch.**

The Pitfall in this lesson makes exactly this distinction: shared visual tokens (color, font) are the parameter-level, surface layer of consistency, while a truly coherent design system also standardizes interaction patterns for similar tasks. A new swipe-to-dismiss gesture that doesn't exist elsewhere in the product is a new pattern being introduced ad hoc — even with correct colors, it risks becoming exactly the "Frankenstein" fragmentation this lesson describes, where each feature is individually reasonable but the product as a whole doesn't hold together. The fix isn't to block the PM from prototyping quickly; it's to check the new gesture against the existing interaction-pattern framework (question 3 from the CodeLab: is this reusing an existing pattern, or quietly introducing a new one that the next designer will now have to reconcile) before it ships as the default experience.
