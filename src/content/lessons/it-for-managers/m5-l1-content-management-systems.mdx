## What Is a CMS?

A **Content Management System (CMS)** is software that allows non-technical users to create, edit, organize, and publish digital content — typically web pages — without writing code.

Before CMS platforms, updating a website required a developer to manually edit HTML files. A CMS separates *content* (text, images, pages) from *presentation* (design and layout), so your marketing team can publish a blog post without involving IT.

## Popular CMS Platforms

| Platform | Market Share | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **WordPress** | ~43% of all websites | Blogs, business sites, eCommerce (with WooCommerce) |
| **Shopify** | Leading eCommerce CMS | Online stores |
| **Drupal** | Enterprise/government | Complex, high-security sites |
| **Joomla** | General use | Mid-size organizations |
| **Squarespace / Wix** | SMB / solopreneurs | Simple sites with no IT team |
| **Contentful / Strapi** | Headless CMS | Developers building custom apps |

---

## Traditional CMS vs. Headless CMS

| Type | How It Works | Manager's Takeaway |
|---|---|---|
| **Traditional (coupled)** | CMS manages both content and how it is displayed | Easier to set up, less flexible |
| **Headless** | CMS stores content; a separate front-end displays it | More flexible, requires developer resources |

Most small and mid-size organizations start with a traditional CMS. Headless CMS is increasingly chosen by organizations that deliver content across multiple channels (website, app, digital signage, etc.).

## Key CMS Capabilities to Evaluate

When selecting a CMS for your organization, evaluate:

- **Ease of use** — Can non-technical staff publish content without help?
- **Access control** — Can you limit who can publish vs. who can only draft?
- **SEO tools** — Does it support metadata, sitemaps, and clean URLs?
- **Plugin/extension ecosystem** — Can you add functionality (forms, analytics, eCommerce)?
- **Security update cadence** — How quickly does the vendor release patches?
- **Hosting options** — Managed hosting (vendor handles servers) vs. self-hosted (you manage infrastructure)

<Pitfall title="Choosing a CMS Based on Popularity Alone">

WordPress powers over 40% of the web, but it is also the most targeted platform for hackers — precisely because of its popularity. A misconfigured or unpatched WordPress site is a common entry point for attacks. If you choose WordPress, commit to a maintenance plan: regular plugin updates, security scanning, and managed hosting with built-in protections.

</Pitfall>

## CMS Governance for Organizations

A CMS without governance creates content chaos. Establish:

- **Roles and permissions** — Who can create drafts? Who approves? Who publishes?
- **Content calendar** — Scheduled publishing keeps the site fresh without ad-hoc urgency.
- **Style guide** — Consistent tone, terminology, and formatting across all authors.
- **Archiving policy** — When does outdated content get removed or updated?

<Quiz question="Your organization's website is built on an aging custom platform that requires a developer for every content change. You're evaluating moving to a CMS. What are the three most important criteria you would use to make your platform selection?">

<div>

The right answer depends on organizational context, but strong criteria include: (1) **Non-technical authoring** — the primary goal is enabling staff to publish without developer involvement, so the editing interface must be intuitive enough for your actual users to adopt it. (2) **Total cost of ownership** — compare licensing, hosting, maintenance, and developer hours needed for customization. A "free" open-source CMS may cost more in developer time than a paid managed platform. (3) **Security and update model** — who is responsible for keeping the platform patched? Managed SaaS (Squarespace, Webflow) handles this for you; self-hosted platforms (WordPress, Drupal) require internal ownership. Secondary considerations: SEO capabilities, integration with your existing tools (CRM, analytics), and migration complexity from the current platform.

</div>
