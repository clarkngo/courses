## Why WordPress + WooCommerce?

**WordPress** is the world's most widely used CMS. **WooCommerce** is a free, open-source eCommerce plugin that transforms a WordPress site into a fully functional online store. Together, they power approximately 28% of all online stores globally.

For managers, this combination offers:
- **No platform licensing fee** (both are free; you pay for hosting, themes, and extensions)
- **Complete ownership** of your store and data
- **Massive ecosystem** of plugins, themes, and developers
- **Flexibility** to build nearly anything — from a simple product catalog to a complex multi-vendor marketplace

---

## WordPress + WooCommerce vs. Shopify

| Factor | WordPress + WooCommerce | Shopify |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Hosting + extensions (variable) | Monthly subscription + transaction fees |
| **Control** | Full — you own everything | Limited to platform capabilities |
| **Ease of setup** | Moderate — requires configuration | High — guided setup |
| **Maintenance** | You manage updates, security, backups | Platform manages infrastructure |
| **Customization** | Unlimited | Limited by plan and app ecosystem |
| **Developer availability** | Very large talent pool | Large but specialized |

WooCommerce is best for organizations that want control and have IT resources. Shopify is better for teams that prioritize speed and simplicity.

## Setting Up WooCommerce: Manager's Overview

### Step 1 — Hosting
WooCommerce requires a WordPress-capable web host. Managed WordPress hosts (WP Engine, Kinsta, SiteGround) handle server optimization and security updates. This is strongly recommended over generic shared hosting for any serious store.

### Step 2 — WooCommerce Installation
WooCommerce installs like any WordPress plugin. The setup wizard walks through:
- Store location and currency
- Industry and product type
- Payment and shipping configuration
- Choosing a theme

### Step 3 — Products
WooCommerce supports:
- **Simple products** — Single item, one price
- **Variable products** — Multiple variants (size, color) with separate SKUs and prices
- **Grouped products** — Related products sold together
- **Virtual/downloadable products** — Software, ebooks, courses
- **Subscriptions** — Recurring billing (requires a paid extension)

### Step 4 — Payments
WooCommerce supports dozens of payment gateways. The most common:
- **WooPayments** — WooCommerce's native gateway (powered by Stripe)
- **Stripe** — Direct integration, widely recommended
- **PayPal** — Broad consumer familiarity
- **Square** — Good if you also have a physical point-of-sale

### Step 5 — Shipping
Configure shipping zones, rates (flat rate, free, calculated), and carrier integrations (USPS, UPS, FedEx) via plugins.

<Pitfall title="Underestimating Plugin Conflicts">

WooCommerce's power comes from its plugin ecosystem — but every plugin added increases complexity and the risk of conflicts. A plugin update can break checkout or product pages unexpectedly. Best practice: use staging environments to test updates before applying to production, and limit plugins to those that are actively maintained and well-reviewed.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="Your organization currently runs a WordPress website and wants to add eCommerce. Leadership is debating between adding WooCommerce to the existing site versus migrating everything to Shopify. What factors would you weigh in this decision?">

<div>

Keep WooCommerce on the existing WordPress site if: (1) the site has significant SEO equity and content that would be complex to migrate; (2) your team has WordPress/WooCommerce experience or budget for a developer; (3) you need deep customization (B2B pricing, complex product types, custom workflows); (4) data ownership is a priority. Migrate to Shopify if: (1) your team has no technical resources and needs a managed solution; (2) the existing WordPress site has performance or security problems that would compound with eCommerce; (3) you need to launch quickly and the store is your primary product (not an add-on to content). The migration cost — redirects, SEO impact, content reentry — is often underestimated. If the WordPress site is healthy and well-maintained, adding WooCommerce is almost always lower-risk and lower-cost than a full platform migration.

</div>
