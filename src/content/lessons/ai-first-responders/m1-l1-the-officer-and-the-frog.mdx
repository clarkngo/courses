## Heber City, Utah — 2024

A patrol officer in Heber City, UT responded to a disturbance call. Like always, his body-worn camera recorded everything — the shouting, the radio noise, the chaos of the scene.

Afterward, he used an AI tool called **Code Four** to draft his incident report. The tool listens to the body-cam audio and writes the narrative for you. It does in under a minute what used to take two hours.

The draft came back. It was mostly right.

Except for one detail: the AI had picked up background noise from a television nearby playing *The Princess and the Frog*. It processed that audio as part of the incident.

**The AI wrote that the officer had shape-shifted into a green bullfrog.**

---

## Why This Story Matters to You

Read that again slowly — because this isn't a joke about bad software.

An officer was about to sign a **legal document** containing a fact the camera never recorded. If that report had been filed and later surfaced in court:

- It's **Brady material** — evidence favorable to the defense that must be disclosed
- It's grounds for a **wrongful charge** getting thrown out
- It destroys the officer's **credibility on the stand** for years

One signature. Career risk. Because an AI misheard a cartoon.

---

## What Is a Hallucination?

In AI, a **hallucination** is when the system confidently generates something that isn't true.

The AI doesn't know it's wrong. It doesn't flag uncertainty. It just produces text that sounds plausible — and in a legal document, that's exactly the problem.

This isn't a fringe bug. It's a **built-in characteristic** of how these tools work. Every AI report-writing tool on the market today has this risk.

The question isn't whether hallucinations happen. They do. The question is: **do you know how to catch them before you sign?**

---

**The hallucination:** "Officer Torres, who briefly transformed into an amphibian" — a fabricated detail that has no basis in the body-cam footage or any witness account.

**Why it's dangerous:** An officer who signs this report has legally attested to a document containing an invented fact. Even if obviously absurd, it creates a chain-of-custody question about the integrity of the entire report.

**The lesson:** AI hallucinations aren't always absurd. Most are subtle — a wrong address, a misattributed statement, a detail from background noise. The frog story is memorable *because* it's extreme. The real threat is the 20% you almost miss.

<Pitfall title="'The AI Is Usually Right, So I'll Skim It'">

80% accuracy sounds reassuring. It isn't.

If you process 10 reports a shift, an 80% accuracy rate means **2 reports per shift** contain errors. Across a year, that's hundreds of legal documents with potential hallucinations bearing your signature.

**The human-in-the-loop requirement isn't a suggestion — it's your legal shield.** Treat every AI draft like a rookie partner's first write-up: trust but verify, line by line.

</Pitfall>
