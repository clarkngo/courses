## A Framework You Can Actually Use

After three modules of risks, hallucinations, and legal landmines, you might be wondering: *Is any of this safe to use?*

Yes. A lot of it is.

The Traffic Light Framework is the mental model we use to teach first responders how to evaluate any AI tool quickly and practically. You don't need to read a policy document every time a new tool appears. You need a framework that fits in your head.

**Green: Use it.** The task is low legal risk, high value, and the consequences of an AI error are manageable.

**Yellow: Use it carefully.** The task has value but requires mandatory human review before the output is acted on or signed.

**Red: Don't use it.** The legal, privacy, or safety risk is too high regardless of efficiency gains.

This lesson covers Green.

---

## Green Light Use Cases

These are tasks where AI assistance is safe, supported by policy, and delivering real value right now.

### Fleet Routing and Shift Scheduling

AI tools that optimize patrol routes, predict maintenance needs, or build shift schedules based on historical demand patterns carry minimal legal risk. If the AI recommends the wrong route, a human dispatcher adjusts it. No one signs a legal document. No evidence is affected.

**Why it's green:** The output is a recommendation. Errors are easily caught and corrected. No chain of custody implications.

### Translated Public Safety Announcements

Using AI to translate community notifications, emergency alerts, or public safety messaging into multiple languages is a clear green-light application. The output is reviewed by a human before publication, and the stakes of a translation error in a press release are far lower than in a 911 dispatch call.

**Why it's green:** Human review before public release. No legal document. Translation errors are discoverable and fixable.

### Synthetic VR Training Scenarios

AI-generated training simulations — virtual reality scenarios for de-escalation, tactical decision-making, or medical response — are green light. The AI creates training content, not evidence. Officers can practice in scenarios generated by AI without any risk to a live case.

**Why it's green:** Training environment, not operational. No real-world legal consequences to simulation errors.

### Bulk Evidence Processing — Human Verified

When AI (like Longeye) is used to process large evidence sets and the output is always verified against original source files before being used in a case, this is green. The human is in the loop at every step where it matters.

**Why it's green:** The AI accelerates the search. The human verifies the finding. The original source is the evidence, not the AI summary.

### Internal Administrative Tasks

Using AI to draft non-legal internal communications, summarize meeting notes, build training schedules, or generate reports that are reviewed before submission — all green.

**Why it's green:** Low legal stakes, human review before any consequence, no chain of custody involved.

<Pitfall title="Green Light Doesn't Mean No Review">

Green light means the legal risk is low enough that AI assistance is appropriate. It doesn't mean you can skip review entirely.

A green-light task like a translated community announcement still requires a human to read it before it goes out. A scheduling recommendation still needs a supervisor to confirm it works for the team.

**Green = appropriate to use. Not green = no human needed.** Human review is always part of the process — the question is how much scrutiny and what the stakes are if something slips through.

</Pitfall>

**Green application — the concern is valid but manageable at the review stage.**

Synthetic VR training is green because: errors in training scenarios don't affect real cases, no one signs a legal document, and the output is reviewed by training staff before officers use it.

The union rep's concern — that scenarios might be unrealistic — is legitimate feedback about the *quality* of the tool, not a reason to classify it as Yellow or Red. The fix is a content review process: training staff evaluate AI-generated scenarios before they enter the training library, and subject-matter experts (experienced officers, FTOs) validate that the scenarios reflect real field conditions.

This is exactly how green-light AI should work: the AI drafts, a qualified human reviews and approves.
