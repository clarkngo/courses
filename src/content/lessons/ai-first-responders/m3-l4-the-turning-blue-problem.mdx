## The Call

A woman calls 911. She's panicked, speaking Spanish. Her husband has collapsed. She is describing what she sees.

**"¡Se está poniendo azul!"**

*He is turning blue.*

Cyanosis — blue or gray discoloration of the skin, lips, or fingernails caused by oxygen deprivation — is a cardinal sign of cardiac arrest. It means tissues aren't getting oxygen. It means the window to save this person is closing fast.

This phrase should have triggered an immediate ALS response: advanced life support, cardiac-capable unit, priority dispatch.

**The AI dropped it.**

The translation missed the phrase. Without "turning blue," the call's clinical picture changed entirely. The system categorized the incident as a **minor fall**.

A basic life support unit was sent instead of an advanced cardiac team.

---

## Why This Happens

Understanding the mechanism helps you know where to watch for it.

### AI Dispatch Tools Are Only as Good as Their Training Data

Dispatch AI tools are trained on enormous datasets of 911 calls — the vast majority of which are in English. When a caller uses a non-English phrase, the AI must rely on translation models that were trained separately, often with less robust data for specific languages, dialects, and regional expressions.

### Medical Language Doesn't Always Translate Directly

Clinical symptoms are often described in culturally specific ways. Consider how differently people describe chest pain:

- "Pressure" (clinical English)
- "Like an elephant sitting on it" (common idiom)
- "Se me aprieta el pecho" (Spanish: "my chest is squeezing")
- "Tim chim mạnh" (Vietnamese: "my heart is beating hard")

An AI trained primarily on English clinical vocabulary may process these idioms as vague complaints rather than cardiac indicators.

### "Turning Blue" Has Multiple Meanings

The phrase "turning blue" (or its equivalents in other languages) doesn't have a single clinical meaning in all contexts:

- Medically: cyanosis — a life-threatening emergency
- Colloquially: feeling very cold ("turning blue from the cold")
- Emotionally: feeling sad or shocked
- Military: a training context

An AI must resolve this ambiguity correctly, in real time, under conditions of stress and background noise. In the case described above, it didn't.

## The Dispatcher's Role Hasn't Changed

AI tools for dispatch are decision support — they are not the dispatcher.

When an AI fails to correctly translate or classify a symptom, the human dispatcher is the last line of defense. That means:

- **Listen to the caller** — even when you don't speak their language, panic, urgency, and specific words can communicate severity
- **Ask follow-up questions** — even through translation delays, asking "Is he breathing? What color is his face?" can surface critical information the AI missed
- **When in doubt, dispatch up** — it is always better to send an ALS unit and stand them down than to send a BLS unit to a cardiac arrest

The AI gives you more information. Your training and judgment determine what to do with it — especially when the AI has given you incomplete information.

**Answers:**

1. **Possible:** Unconsciousness, head injury, stroke, cardiac. **AI risk:** "Can't wake her up" may be classified as welfare check. **Follow-up:** "Is she breathing? Can you see her chest moving?"

2. **Likely:** Agonal breathing — a sign of cardiac arrest. **AI risk:** "Weird sound with his throat" won't trigger cardiac flagging in most models. **Follow-up:** "What does the sound sound like — gasping? Gurgling? Is he responding to you at all?"

3. **Likely:** Overdose, possibly intentional. **AI risk:** "Wanted to sleep" masks suicidal intent; "all her pills" may not register as overdose language. **Follow-up:** "What pills did she take? How many? Is she breathing normally?"

4. **Uncertain:** Possibly cardiac event. **AI risk:** Incomplete phrase — the caller may not know the English words for what's happening. **Follow-up:** "What is happening to him right now? Is he awake? Is he breathing?"

<Pitfall title="Assuming the AI Translation Is Complete">

AI translation tools do not always flag when they've dropped content. The transcript you see may look complete while missing critical phrases.

**Never treat an AI-generated transcript as the verbatim record of what a caller said.** It is a processed interpretation. In high-stakes medical calls especially, the dispatcher's ears — and their follow-up questions — are the real safety net.

</Pitfall>

**Ask direct, yes/no follow-up questions that can be answered even across a language barrier — and if severity is uncertain, dispatch at the higher tier.**

When language is a barrier and the AI translation is incomplete:

1. **Use simple, direct questions:** "Is he breathing?" A caller who understands some English can usually respond to this. "Yes/no" crosses most language barriers.
2. **Listen to what you can hear:** Labored breathing, crying, physical sounds in the background often communicate urgency without words
3. **Default to ALS when uncertain:** The cost of sending an advanced unit to a non-critical call is resource waste — recoverable. The cost of sending a basic unit to an undetected cardiac arrest is a life — not recoverable.
4. **Request Language Line** if time permits and the situation isn't immediately critical — but don't wait for a live interpreter before dispatching

The AI failed on the "turning blue" call. The dispatcher is the safety net that catches that failure.
