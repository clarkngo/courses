## The Rule You're Already Bound By

Most first responders have heard of CJIS. Fewer can explain exactly what it covers — and almost none were trained on how AI changes the compliance picture.

CJIS stands for **Criminal Justice Information Services**. It's the FBI division that manages the largest criminal justice database infrastructure in the world: fingerprints, criminal histories, wanted persons, stolen vehicles, incident reports, and more. The **CJIS Security Policy** is the document that governs how all of that information must be handled.

If your job involves accessing, creating, or transmitting criminal justice information — and it does — you are bound by the CJIS Security Policy.

AI tools don't change that obligation. They create new ways to violate it.

---

## What Counts as Criminal Justice Information (CJI)

CJI is broader than most officers assume. It includes:

- **Biometric data:** Fingerprints, palm prints, iris scans, facial recognition data
- **Criminal history records:** Arrests, convictions, incarcerations, supervision status
- **Identity data:** Social Security numbers, driver's license numbers, immigration identifiers linked to a criminal record
- **Incident and case data:** Police reports, investigation files, evidence logs tied to an active case
- **Wanted and missing person records:** NCIC entries, local warrants
- **Protection order data:** Domestic violence restraining orders
- **Threat assessment and gang data:** Files compiled under CJIS-compliant systems
- **Body-worn camera footage:** When tied to an identifiable individual or active investigation

If you can look it up in NCIC or your CAD system and it pertains to a specific person, incident, or investigation — it's almost certainly CJI.

---

## The CJIS Security Policy — Key Requirements

The CJIS Security Policy sets specific technical and procedural controls:

### Encryption in Transit
All CJI transmitted across networks must be encrypted using FIPS 140-2 validated encryption. This means CJI cannot travel over unencrypted channels — including standard email and public messaging apps.

### Access Controls
Personnel must be authenticated and authorized before accessing CJI systems. Two-factor authentication is required for remote access.

### Audit Logging
Every access to CJI systems must be logged. Who accessed it, when, and what they did. Logs must be retained and reviewable.

### Physical Security
Workstations accessing CJI must be in physically secure locations. This is why you can't log into NCIC from your personal device at a coffee shop.

### Data Retention and Destruction
CJI that is no longer needed must be destroyed in a documented way — not just deleted from a folder.

---

## How Consumer AI Breaks Every One of These Requirements

When you paste criminal justice information into ChatGPT, Google Bard, or any similar consumer AI tool, here is what happens:

| CJIS Requirement | What Consumer AI Does |
|---|---|
| Encrypted transmission | Data sent to commercial servers over standard HTTPS — not FIPS 140-2 validated |
| Access control and authentication | No authentication — anyone can use ChatGPT |
| Audit logging | No audit log accessible to your department or the FBI |
| Physical security | Data stored on commercial servers with no law enforcement oversight |
| Data retention/destruction | Data may be retained and used for model training; you cannot guarantee destruction |

The CJIS Security Policy doesn't have a carve-out for "it was just for a quick summary." It doesn't distinguish between intentional violations and convenient shortcuts. The moment CJI leaves your compliant environment and enters a non-compliant one, the violation has occurred.

---

## What a CJIS Violation Costs

CJIS violations are investigated by the FBI. The consequences cascade:

**For the individual:**
- Criminal exposure under 18 U.S.C. § 1030 (Computer Fraud and Abuse Act)
- Termination and loss of law enforcement certification
- Loss of access to CJI systems — effectively ending a career in public safety

**For the department:**
- Loss of access to NCIC and state criminal history repositories
- A department that loses NCIC access cannot run warrants, check criminal histories, or enter stolen vehicle reports. It is effectively unable to do its job.
- Mandatory FBI audit and remediation process
- Civil liability if the breach exposed a protected individual (e.g., a domestic violence victim in a protection order file)

**For prosecutions:**
- Evidence derived from improperly handled CJI can be suppressed
- Defense attorneys will seek sanctions against departments with documented CJIS violations

<Pitfall title="'It's Just One Time' Is How Audits Start">

FBI CJIS audits are triggered by complaints, anomalous access patterns, and data breach reports — not just by deliberate misconduct.

If a department's data shows up in a commercial AI training dataset, if a user's account is flagged for unusual access patterns, or if a department member reports a suspected violation, an audit follows. By that point, "I only did it once" does not help.

**The audit will look at logs.** It will look at what was accessed, when, and from where. If you sent case data to a non-compliant system, there is a record — on the compliant system's side even if not on the consumer tool's side.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="An officer's department is piloting an AI report drafting tool provided by a vendor. The vendor assures the department that their system is 'secure.' What does the department need to verify before authorizing officers to input case data?">

<div>

**The vendor's self-assurance is not sufficient. The department needs documented, specific compliance verification:**

1. **CJIS Security Policy compliance certification** — Is the vendor an authorized CJIS-compliant cloud provider? This is a specific designation, not a general "secure" claim.

2. **FIPS 140-2 validated encryption** — Is data encrypted at rest and in transit using validated encryption? Ask for the specific FIPS certificate.

3. **Access control documentation** — How is access to the system controlled? What authentication is required? Who at the vendor can see the data?

4. **Audit logging** — Does the system maintain logs accessible to the department? How long are logs retained? Are they FBI-audit-ready?

5. **Data retention policy** — Where is data stored? How long? Can the department request deletion? Is data used to train any AI models?

6. **Criminal Justice Agency agreement** — Has the vendor signed a CJIS-compliant information security agreement with the department?

A vendor who cannot answer all six questions with specific, documented answers should not receive case data.
