## The Pitch vs. The Full Picture

When a vendor presents their AI tool to your department, you'll hear the upside numbers:

- Sub-minute report drafting
- 95% cardiac arrest detection accuracy
- 25-minute early wildfire warning
- 60 hours of evidence processed in minutes

These numbers are real. They represent genuine value.

What the pitch deck rarely includes is the corresponding risk column — what each gain puts at risk if not properly managed.

This lesson gives you the complete picture.

---

## The Risk Matrix

| Area | What AI Gains | What AI Risks |
|---|---|---|
| **Report writing** | 1–2 hours of paperwork → under 60 seconds | AI hallucinations in legal documents; Brady violations if unverified |
| **Report accuracy** | Consistent formatting and structure | Ghost Officer errors; defense impeachment if draft isn't reviewed |
| **Cardiac arrest dispatch** | Near-instant detection at 95% accuracy | The 5% miss rate on cardiac arrests is fatal; language gaps change triage |
| **Evidence processing** | Weeks of work → hours | AI summaries cited as evidence instead of source files; CJIS violations |
| **Wildfire detection** | Up to 25-min earlier warning | False positives cause unnecessary resource deployment; over-reliance delays human size-up |
| **Predictive tools** | Faster resource allocation | Historical bias in training data amplifies racial and geographic enforcement disparities |

## The Risk That Requires Its Own Conversation: Predictive Tools

Every other risk in this matrix is about a tool making a factual error. Predictive tools carry a different kind of risk: **they can make systematically biased decisions that look like data.**

Predictive patrol tools analyze historical crime data to recommend where officers should focus patrol presence.

The problem: historical crime data reflects historical enforcement patterns — which were not neutral. Areas that were over-policed in the past have more documented incidents, which means the AI recommends more policing there, which generates more incidents, which reinforces the recommendation.

This feedback loop is called **algorithmic amplification of historical bias.** It's not the AI doing something wrong — it's the AI doing exactly what it was trained to do with data that contains human biases baked in.

**Why this matters for you:** If you're assigned patrol areas partly determined by an AI recommendation, you should know how that recommendation was generated and what its limitations are. "The algorithm said so" is not a satisfying answer to a community asking why enforcement looks the way it does.

## How to Read the Risk Matrix for Any New Tool

When your department evaluates a new AI tool, use these five questions to build your own risk row:

1. **What specific task does it automate?**
2. **What is its documented accuracy rate — and in what conditions?**
3. **What happens in the failure case? Who is accountable?**
4. **Does it comply with CJIS, HIPAA, and Seattle IT data standards?**
5. **What does the human-in-the-loop review look like — and is it built into the workflow?**

A vendor who can't answer questions 3 and 5 clearly is selling you efficiency without accountability.

<Pitfall title="Accepting Efficiency Numbers Without Asking About Failure Rates">

"95% accuracy" sounds excellent. But in public safety contexts, you need to ask about the 5%.

- A 5% cardiac arrest miss rate, at scale, is lives lost
- A 5% hallucination rate across all report drafts is hundreds of legal errors per year per department
- A 5% false positive rate in predictive patrol tools means thousands of unnecessary enforcement contacts per year

**Always ask the vendor:** *What happens in your failure cases? How frequent are they? Who is accountable?*

If the vendor's answer focuses on their accuracy rate and not their failure mode, that's a red flag.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="A predictive patrol AI recommends increased presence in a specific neighborhood based on historical incident data. An officer who knows the neighborhood well believes the recommendation reflects old enforcement patterns rather than current actual crime. What should they do?">

<div>

**Raise the concern through their chain of command and document their reasoning.**

Officers have professional judgment that AI tools don't have. Historical data reflects historical conditions — neighborhoods change, enforcement patterns shift, and an officer with current community knowledge may have better situational awareness than a model trained on years-old data.

This is a legitimate use of human-in-the-loop authority: the officer isn't dismissing the tool, they're providing the local context that the tool can't access.

**Practically:** Document that the recommendation was reviewed, that the officer noted a potential data currency issue, and what decision was made. This creates accountability for the process — which protects the officer, the department, and the community.
