## You May Already Be Using AI

Not in a lab. Not in a pilot program somewhere else.

Departments in Utah, Redmond WA, Seattle, and King County have already signed contracts and started deployments. If your department hasn't yet, it's evaluating. The procurement conversations are happening right now — with or without your input.

That's why this lesson matters: **you should understand what's being bought before someone hands it to you on shift.**

---

## Four Reasons This Is Moving Fast

### 1. The Tools Are Already Here

Code Four, Axon Draft One, Longeye, Prepared, Corti, Pano AI — these aren't prototypes. They're commercially deployed, actively sold to public safety agencies, and already in the field.

Utah and Redmond went first. Seattle departments have started procurement. The market isn't waiting for training to catch up.

### 2. The City Already Asked for This

Seattle's **2025–2026 AI Plan, Pillar 3** mandates workforce upskilling and capacity building specifically around AI. The city recognized that buying tools is useless if the people using them don't understand what they're doing.

> *"With so many AI technologies available and innumerable vendors pushing to sell to the City, we face an urgent need to equip its leaders, managers, and employees with foundational knowledge."*
> — City of Seattle, Pillar 3

That mandate exists. No one has operationalized it for public safety yet. This course is part of changing that.

### 3. Vendors Move Faster Than Policy

Software companies are not waiting for regulations. They are actively pitching chiefs, sheriffs, and fire commissioners right now. The sales cycle for these tools is months — the policy cycle is years.

That gap is where officers, firefighters, and paramedics get caught with tools they weren't trained to use.

### 4. The Window Is Narrow

Washington's AI Task Force delivers its formal recommendations by **July 1, 2026**. Once those drop, mandatory transparency requirements and officer attestation rules become law. Departments that haven't built AI competency will scramble to comply.

Getting trained now means you shape how AI gets used — instead of having a policy handed to you.

## The Tools Across Your Departments

Here's a quick map of what's being deployed across SPD, SFD, and EMS:

| Tool | Who Uses It | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Code Four** | Police | Drafts incident reports from body-cam audio in under 60 seconds |
| **Axon Draft One** | Police | Narrative generation from body-worn camera footage |
| **Longeye** | Investigators | Processes hours of digital evidence (calls, video) in minutes |
| **Pano AI** | Fire | Early wildfire detection — up to 25 minutes before first 911 call |
| **Corti** | Dispatch / EMS | Detects cardiac arrest signatures in 911 audio at 95% accuracy |
| **Prepared** | 911 Dispatch | Real-time call transcription, translation, and keyword extraction |
| **ImageTrend Elite** | EMS | Voice-to-text for patient care reports in the field |
| **Truleo** | Police | Analyzes 100% of body-cam footage for compliance and behavior patterns |
| **SoundThinking** | Police | Acoustic gunshot detection — 45-second alert before first 911 call |

We'll go deep on each of these in Module 2.

<Pitfall title="Assuming 'Our Department Would Never Use That'">

Every department that now uses body-worn cameras once said they didn't need them.

The pattern with public safety technology is consistent: early adopters face the learning curve, everyone else inherits both the tools and the established (sometimes flawed) practices.

Being an informed voice in your department's procurement conversation is far better than being handed a tool on day one with a 10-minute training video.

</Pitfall>
