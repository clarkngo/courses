## From Software AI to Physical AI

Most AI you interact with daily — chatbots, recommendation engines, image classifiers — lives entirely in software. It processes data and returns data. Nothing physically moves.

**Physical AI** is different. It closes the loop between the digital and physical worlds:

1. **Sense** — cameras, radar, sonar, GPS, and other sensors feed real-world data into the system
2. **Perceive** — AI models interpret that raw data (obstacle ahead, sea state rough, engine temperature rising)
3. **Decide** — the system plans the best response given constraints (safety rules, mission goals, regulations)
4. **Act** — motors turn, valves open, alarms sound, course corrections execute

This sense-perceive-decide-act cycle runs continuously — often hundreds of times per second — while the physical world changes around it.

## Why Maritime Is a Prime Domain for Physical AI

The maritime industry is one of the most demanding environments for Physical AI for several reasons:

| Challenge | Why It's Hard |
|---|---|
| **Vast, unstructured environment** | Open ocean has no road markings, no traffic lights, no fixed lanes |
| **Extreme sensor conditions** | Saltwater, fog, spray, and darkness degrade cameras and lidar |
| **Safety stakes** | A mistake in a cargo corridor can sink a vessel or cause an oil spill |
| **Regulatory complexity** | COLREGs, SOLAS, flag-state rules, and port authority requirements all apply |
| **Crew limitations** | Fatigue, small crews on large vessels, and remote operations demand automation |

Despite — or because of — these challenges, shipping carries **90% of world trade**, and the industry is investing heavily in AI to improve safety, efficiency, and sustainability.

## The Physical AI Stack in Maritime

Think of maritime Physical AI as five stacked layers, each depending on the one below it:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│  5. Mission Layer                   │  voyage planning, fleet management
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  4. Decision Layer                  │  collision avoidance, route optimization
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  3. Perception Layer                │  object detection, sea-state estimation
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. Sensor Layer                    │  radar, AIS, cameras, sonar, IMU
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  1. Physical Layer                  │  hull, propulsion, rudder, actuators
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each layer feeds information upward and receives commands downward. A breakdown at any layer affects every layer above it. In Module 2 we explore the sensor layer in depth; in Module 3 we cover the decision layer.

## Real-World Physical AI in Maritime Today

These systems are already operating commercially:

**Autonomous Surface Vessels (ASVs)**
Rolls-Royce and Kongsberg have demonstrated remotely operated and semi-autonomous ferries in Norwegian fjords. The Yara Birkeland — a zero-emission container ship — sailed autonomously between Norwegian ports in 2022.

**Predictive Maintenance**
Wärtsilä's AI platform monitors engine vibration, exhaust temperatures, and fuel flow in real time. It predicts failures days before they happen, allowing planned maintenance instead of emergency repairs at sea.

**Port Automation**
The Port of Rotterdam uses AI to coordinate berth scheduling, crane sequencing, and truck flow. Computer vision tracks every container in the yard.

**Weather Routing AI**
Systems like StormGeo and Weathernews analyze satellite data and model forecasts to find fuel-optimal routes that avoid heavy weather — saving 5–15% on fuel per voyage.

<Quiz question="What distinguishes Physical AI from traditional software AI?">

<div>

Physical AI closes the sense-perceive-decide-act loop in the real world. Unlike pure software AI that takes data in and returns data out, Physical AI has actuators that change the physical environment — turning a rudder, sounding an alarm, or stopping an engine. The consequences of errors are physical and often irreversible, which is why safety and reliability requirements are far stricter than for software-only systems.
