## The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

Perception is hard. But it's only half the problem.

A self-driving car can perfectly detect every pedestrian, lane marking, and traffic sign on the road — and still crash if it doesn't know what to do with that information.

The decision-making and control pipeline is what turns a rich perception of the world into a safe, appropriate physical action.

---

## The Decision Stack

Physical AI systems typically organize their decision-making into layers, each operating at a different timescale:

**Mission planning (minutes to hours)**
The highest level. What is the overall goal? A delivery drone plans its route from warehouse to destination. A surgical robot is assigned a procedure. A Mars rover is given a science target 500 meters away.

**Behavioral planning (seconds)**
Given the current environment, what should the system be doing right now? A self-driving car decides: should I follow the car in front, change lanes, or yield to a merging vehicle?

**Motion planning (milliseconds to seconds)**
Given a behavioral decision, compute a specific trajectory through space. The car decides to change lanes: now plan the exact path to move smoothly from one lane to the other while avoiding all obstacles.

**Control (milliseconds)**
Execute the planned motion by sending commands to actuators. Turn the steering wheel this many degrees. Apply this much brake force. Rotate this joint to this angle.

---

## Path Planning

Path planning is the process of finding a route from the current state to a goal state that avoids obstacles and satisfies constraints (don't go too fast, don't tilt more than 15 degrees, etc.).

**Grid-based planning**
The environment is divided into a grid. Each cell is marked as free or occupied. Algorithms like **A*** search find the shortest path through free cells.

Simple and reliable, but computationally expensive for high-resolution grids and doesn't handle continuous motion well.

**Sampling-based planning**
Algorithms like **RRT (Rapidly-exploring Random Trees)** work by randomly sampling configurations in the space and connecting them into a tree. They're effective for high-dimensional problems (a robot arm with 7 joints has a 7-dimensional configuration space).

**Potential fields**
The goal exerts an attractive force; obstacles exert repulsive forces. The robot follows the net gradient toward the goal. Elegant and fast, but prone to getting stuck in local minima.

---

## Trajectory Optimization

Path planning finds a feasible route. **Trajectory optimization** refines it: given the planned path, find the smoothest, most energy-efficient way to execute it while respecting the physical limits of the system (maximum acceleration, joint torque limits, etc.).

A planned path might say "move from point A to point B." Trajectory optimization says "move from A to B in 2.3 seconds, accelerating smoothly, keeping joint velocities below their limits, and minimizing jerk so the motion doesn't damage the mechanism."

## Reactive vs. Deliberative Control

<CodeLab title="Two Modes of Decision-Making">

**Deliberative control** reasons about the future. It builds a model of the world, simulates possible actions, and chooses the best one. This produces smart, efficient behavior — but takes time.

**Reactive control** responds directly to sensor inputs without building a model. Fast, robust, and hard to fool — but can produce unintelligent behavior in complex situations.

Real physical AI systems use both:
- Deliberative planning for long-horizon decisions (route, task sequence)
- Reactive control for immediate hazard avoidance (collision detection, balance recovery)

The challenge is integrating them: when should the reactive system override the deliberative plan? A robot executing a planned motion should stop immediately if a human steps into its path — even if the planner didn't anticipate it.

</CodeLab>

## Feedback and Feedforward Control

**Feedforward control** computes the actuator commands needed to execute a motion before measuring the result. It relies entirely on an accurate model of the system.

**Feedback control** (closed-loop) measures the actual result and corrects errors. If the robot arm is 2 degrees off target, apply a corrective command.

Most physical AI systems use both:
1. Feedforward to get approximately right commands quickly
2. Feedback to correct the inevitable errors in real time

The most important feedback control algorithm is the **PID controller** (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) — a simple, tunable formula that has been used in everything from thermostats to aircraft for over a century.

<Pitfall title="Model Mismatch — The Hidden Danger">

Every control system is built on a model of the physical system it controls. When that model is wrong — because the robot is carrying an unexpectedly heavy load, because a joint is worn, because the terrain is different from the training environment — the controller makes wrong predictions and produces wrong commands.

Model mismatch is one of the most common causes of physical AI failures in deployment. A system that works perfectly in the lab fails in the field because the real world doesn't match the model.

Robust control design specifically accounts for model uncertainty — assuming the model is slightly wrong and ensuring the system remains stable anyway. Adaptive control goes further, updating the model in real time from sensor data.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="What is the difference between path planning and trajectory optimization?">

<div>

Path planning finds a feasible route through the environment — a sequence of configurations that gets from start to goal while avoiding obstacles. It doesn't necessarily specify timing or how smoothly the motion should be executed.

Trajectory optimization takes that path and refines it into a specific time-parameterized motion that respects physical constraints: joint speed limits, maximum acceleration, smoothness requirements, and energy efficiency. The result is a trajectory — not just where to go, but exactly how to get there in a physically realizable way.
