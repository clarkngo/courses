## One Loop to Rule Them All

A self-driving car. A surgical robot. A warehouse drone. A Mars rover.

These systems look nothing alike, but they all run the same fundamental loop:

1. **Sense** — gather data from the environment
2. **Think** — process that data and decide what to do
3. **Act** — execute the decision in the physical world

Then repeat — thousands of times per second.

This is the **Sense-Think-Act loop**, and it's the foundation of every physical AI system ever built.

---

## Sense

Sensing means collecting raw information about the world. Physical AI systems use a variety of sensors depending on what they need to know:

- **Cameras** — capture visual information (color, shape, depth)
- **LiDAR** — measure distance by firing laser pulses
- **Radar** — detect objects and measure speed using radio waves
- **Microphones** — capture audio
- **IMUs (Inertial Measurement Units)** — measure acceleration and rotation
- **GPS** — determine location
- **Touch and force sensors** — detect contact and pressure

No single sensor is sufficient. Real systems combine many inputs — a process called **sensor fusion** — to build a complete picture of the environment.

---

## Think

The "think" step is where AI lives. Given the sensor data, the system must:

1. **Perceive** — interpret the raw data (what objects are present? where are they? what are they doing?)
2. **Plan** — determine what action to take given the current state and the system's goal
3. **Predict** — anticipate how the environment will change

This is where deep learning, computer vision, and reinforcement learning are applied. It's also where most of the hard problems in physical AI live.

<CodeLab title="Thinking Fast and Slow in Physical AI">

Physical AI systems often have two thinking modes running simultaneously:

**Fast path (reactive):** millisecond responses to immediate hazards. A robot arm detects unexpected contact and stops instantly — no planning required.

**Slow path (deliberative):** seconds-to-minutes planning for complex tasks. A robot navigating a warehouse maps its route, avoids dynamic obstacles, and schedules its next pickup.

Getting these two modes to coexist — fast enough to be safe, smart enough to be useful — is one of the core engineering challenges in physical AI.

</CodeLab>

---

## Act

Acting means sending commands to physical actuators — the components that create motion or force:

- **Electric motors** — spin wheels, joints, fans
- **Hydraulic actuators** — move heavy loads with fluid pressure
- **Pneumatic actuators** — use compressed air for fast, light movements
- **Speakers** — produce sound
- **Grippers and hands** — manipulate objects

The action closes the loop: the system changes the world, which changes what the sensors detect, which triggers the next cycle.

---

## Why the Loop Is Hard

Each step introduces error:

- Sensors give noisy, incomplete readings
- AI models make wrong predictions
- Actuators don't execute perfectly

In software, errors compound slowly. In physical AI, errors compound at the speed of the loop — which means small mistakes can cascade quickly into dangerous situations.

<Pitfall title="The Latency Problem">

The speed of the loop matters enormously. A self-driving car traveling at 60 mph covers **88 feet per second**. If the perception-to-action loop takes 200 milliseconds (a fifth of a second), the car travels 17 feet before it can respond to a detected obstacle.

Every millisecond of latency in the sense-think-act loop has a direct physical consequence. This is why physical AI hardware is optimized obsessively for speed.

</Pitfall>

<div>

No single sensor captures the full picture. Cameras can't measure distance accurately. LiDAR struggles in rain and fog. GPS fails indoors. Radar can't distinguish object shapes.

By combining multiple sensors, a system can compensate for the weaknesses of each individual sensor — getting a more complete, accurate, and reliable picture of the environment than any one sensor could provide alone.
