## Two Philosophies of Human-Robot Interaction

Industrial robots and collaborative robots (cobots) represent two fundamentally different answers to the same question: how should robots and humans share a workspace?

Industrial robots say: keep humans away. Cobots say: work alongside them.

The difference isn't just engineering — it shapes the AI, the safety systems, the deployment model, and the economics.

---

## Industrial Robots

Industrial robots are the workhorses of modern manufacturing. They've been welding car frames, painting surfaces, and assembling electronics since the 1960s.

**Key characteristics:**

**Speed and power**
Industrial robots are fast and powerful. A heavy-payload welding robot can move a 500 kg load at 2 m/s. That kind of kinetic energy is lethal to humans in the path.

**Caged and segregated**
Because they're dangerous, traditional industrial robots operate inside physical cages with interlocked safety doors. When a human enters the cage, the robot must stop. Human and robot never share the workspace simultaneously.

**Repeatability over adaptability**
Industrial robots are typically programmed for a specific, fixed task — weld these points, every time, in this order. The task doesn't change; the environment is controlled; variation is minimized. AI is optional — many are still pure motion controllers running scripted programs.

**High upfront cost, high throughput**
A high-end industrial robot cell costs $200,000-$500,000 installed. The economics only work at high production volumes where the cost is amortized over millions of cycles.

---

## Collaborative Robots (Cobots)

Cobots emerged in the 2000s with a different design philosophy: make robots that can work safely beside humans, without cages, in flexible environments.

**Key characteristics:**

**Force limiting**
Cobots continuously monitor the forces their joints are experiencing. If they detect unexpected contact — a human arm in the path — they stop immediately. ISO/TS 15066 defines the force and pressure limits for human-robot contact that cobots must not exceed.

**Lighter, slower, safer**
Cobots sacrifice some speed and payload capacity for safety. A typical cobot has a payload of 3-16 kg and moves much slower than an industrial robot. The reduced kinetic energy means contact injuries, if they occur, are minor.

**Easy programming**
Cobots can often be programmed by hand-guiding: an operator physically moves the robot through the desired motion, and the robot records the trajectory. No coding required. This democratizes robot deployment to small manufacturers without robotics engineers.

**AI integration**
Cobots increasingly use AI for perception and adaptation:
- Computer vision to locate parts that aren't in exactly the right position
- Force feedback to detect when assembly steps are complete
- Anomaly detection to flag parts that look wrong before assembly

<CodeLab title="The Cobot Market">

The global collaborative robot market is growing at over 30% annually. Key players include Universal Robots (UR), FANUC's CRX series, KUKA's iiwa, and ABB's YuMi.

The typical cobot customer is a small-to-medium manufacturer who can't afford a full industrial robot cell and needs flexibility — the robot might do assembly on Monday and packaging on Friday. Cobots that can be redeployed in hours (versus days for an industrial cell) command a premium for that flexibility.

## AI in Industrial Robotics

Even traditional industrial robots are getting AI upgrades:

**Vision-guided robotics**
Cameras and AI perception allow robots to pick parts that aren't precisely positioned — "bin picking," the ability to reach into a bin of randomly oriented parts and grasp one. This was extremely hard with classical computer vision and has only become practical with deep learning.

**Quality inspection**
AI vision systems inspect parts for defects at camera speed — faster and more consistent than human inspectors. Mounted beside or on the robot arm, they can reject defective parts immediately.

**Predictive maintenance**
AI monitors motor currents, vibration signatures, and thermal profiles to predict bearing failures and gear wear before they cause downtime.

**Adaptive welding**
AI adjusts weld parameters in real time based on sensor feedback — compensating for material variation, joint fit-up errors, and thermal distortion.

## The Safety Engineering Stack

<Pitfall title="Cobot Safety Is Not Optional">

Cobots are safer than industrial robots but not inherently safe. A cobot carrying a sharp tool or a heavy payload can still injure a human, even if the robot itself stops when it detects contact.

ISO 10218 (robot safety) and ISO/TS 15066 (cobot safety) require a **risk assessment** for every cobot deployment. The assessment considers: payload, end-effector, tool, workspace geometry, and human exposure to determine what safety measures are required.

Simply buying a cobot and deploying it without a risk assessment is both dangerous and non-compliant. Safety is the responsibility of the system integrator and the end user, not just the robot manufacturer.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="What is the primary safety mechanism that distinguishes a collaborative robot from an industrial robot?">

<div>

Collaborative robots use force limiting — they continuously monitor the forces in their joints and stop immediately if they detect unexpected contact above a safe threshold. This allows them to work alongside humans without physical barriers.

Industrial robots rely on physical segregation: cages, safety fences, and interlocked doors that prevent humans from entering the robot's workspace during operation. The robot itself has no mechanism to detect or respond to human contact safely.
