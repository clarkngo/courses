## From Architecture to Standard

The Purdue Model tells you *how to think* about layering IT and OT systems. The **ISA/IEC 62443** standard tells you *what security controls* are required at each layer. Together they give you both a mental map and an auditable checklist — which is why ISA/IEC 62443 has become the dominant compliance framework for industrial cybersecurity globally.

---

## ISA/IEC 62443 in One Page

ISA/IEC 62443 is a family of documents (not a single standard) published jointly by the International Society of Automation (ISA) and the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC). It covers three stakeholder groups:

| Series | Who It's For | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **62443-1.x** | Everyone | Concepts, terminology, security lifecycle |
| **62443-2.x** | Asset owners & operators | Policies, patch management, supplier requirements |
| **62443-3.x** | System integrators | Architecture, zones & conduits, security requirements |
| **62443-4.x** | Product suppliers | Secure development, component requirements |

The most operationally relevant document is **62443-3-3**: it defines the 51 foundational requirements (FRs) organized into 7 categories, and introduces **Security Levels (SL)** as the mechanism for specifying how strong those requirements must be in a given zone.

---

## Zones and Conduits

The central architectural concept in 62443-3-3 is **zones and conduits**:

- A **zone** is a grouping of assets with similar security requirements and the same level of trust. Think of it as a logical perimeter around a set of devices.
- A **conduit** is the communication channel between zones. Every conduit must be explicitly defined and protected — it is the enforcement point for traffic crossing a zone boundary.

This is zero trust expressed as architecture: traffic between zones is denied by default and permitted only through an explicitly defined, monitored conduit.

### Mapping Purdue Levels to ISA/IEC 62443 Zones

| Purdue Level | 62443 Zone (typical) | Example Assets |
|---|---|---|
| L5 Enterprise | Enterprise Zone | ERP, email, business BI |
| L4 Site Business | IT/OT DMZ (conduit zone) | Historian mirror, data diode |
| L3 Site Operations | Operations Zone | SCADA server, engineering workstation |
| L2 Area Supervisory | Control Zone | DCS operator station, batch server |
| L1 Basic Control | Field Control Zone | PLCs, RTUs, smart instruments |
| L0 Physical Process | (not a network zone) | Sensors, actuators |

The OT DMZ between L3 and L4 becomes a dedicated conduit zone in 62443 terms — not just a firewall rule, but a fully managed zone with its own security level specification.

## Security Levels Explained

Every zone is assigned a **target Security Level (SL-T)** from 1 to 4, based on the consequences of a breach:

| Security Level | Threat Actor | Consequence of Breach |
|---|---|---|
| **SL 1** | Unintentional or incidental | Minor operational disruption |
| **SL 2** | Intentional, simple means, low motivation | Moderate plant disruption |
| **SL 3** | Intentional, sophisticated means, nation-state capable | Serious injury or major environmental damage |
| **SL 4** | State-sponsored, unlimited resources | Catastrophic consequence (mass casualties, national infrastructure) |

Most industrial sites target SL 2 for general OT networks and SL 3 for safety-critical systems. Nuclear, water treatment, and power grid operators often have regulatory requirements for SL 3 or above.

The **achieved Security Level (SL-A)** is what your controls actually deliver — the gap between SL-T and SL-A is your risk exposure.

## The 7 Foundational Requirement Categories

62443-3-3 organizes its 51 requirements into these categories (FRs):

| FR | Category | Core Idea |
|---|---|---|
| FR 1 | Identification & Authentication Control | Every entity (human, device, process) must prove identity before access |
| FR 2 | Use Control | Authorized entities get only the access they need (least privilege) |
| FR 3 | System Integrity | Hardware and software are protected against unauthorized modification |
| FR 4 | Data Confidentiality | Data in transit and at rest is protected against unauthorized disclosure |
| FR 5 | Restricted Data Flow | Communication between zones flows only through defined conduits |
| FR 6 | Timely Response to Events | Detect, log, and respond to security events within defined time windows |
| FR 7 | Resource Availability | Systems maintain required functions under stress, including attack |

Each FR has multiple system requirements (SRs) with enhancements (RE) that apply at higher security levels. For example, FR 1 SR 1.1 requires user identification and authentication; RE 1 adds multi-factor authentication for SL 2+.

<CodeLab title="Zone Boundary Checklist">

Use this checklist when defining a zone boundary in your architecture review:

```
Zone Boundary Definition Checklist (per conduit)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
[ ] Source zone identified and documented
[ ] Destination zone identified and documented
[ ] Security level assigned to each zone (SL 1–4)
[ ] All protocols allowed through this conduit explicitly listed
[ ] Default-deny enforced (no implicit allow)
[ ] Inspection method defined (stateful firewall / DPI / data diode)
[ ] Logging destination configured (SIEM or local historian)
[ ] Authorized users/systems enumerated (not just "IT team")
[ ] Conduit owner assigned (a named person, not a team)
[ ] Review cadence documented (annually at minimum for SL 2+)
```

Print this and walk every conduit in your current architecture. Any unchecked box is a gap that belongs in your risk register.

</CodeLab>

## Designing the OT DMZ

The OT DMZ is the most critical conduit in a typical industrial deployment — it is the only path between the corporate network and the control system. Designing it correctly means:

**1. No direct IT-to-OT routing**
Traffic must pass through an inspecting host in the DMZ; no router rule should forward packets directly from the enterprise VLAN to the OT VLAN.

**2. Historian replication, not direct database access**
The corporate data warehouse pulls from a read-only historian replica in the DMZ — not from the SCADA historian directly. If the enterprise side is compromised, the attacker reaches only the replica, not the live process data store.

**3. Jump server for engineering access**
OT engineers access PLCs through a hardened jump server in the DMZ, not through direct VPN tunnels from their laptops. The jump server enforces MFA, session recording, and access control.

**4. Data diodes for highest-sensitivity zones**
A hardware data diode enforces one-way flow at the physical layer — data can exit the OT network (to the historian replica) but nothing from IT can enter. No software misconfiguration can reverse this.

```
Enterprise Network (L4/L5)
        │
   [Firewall 1]   ← permits only specific IP:port pairs inbound
        │
   ┌────┴────────────────────────────┐
   │         OT DMZ                  │
   │  [Historian Replica]            │
   │  [Jump Server + MFA]            │
   │  [Patch Management Proxy]       │
   └────┬────────────────────────────┘
        │
   [Firewall 2]   ← default-deny; DPI-enabled; Zeek tap here
        │
   OT Network (L1–L3)
```

Both firewalls must be different brands/vendors — a single vendor vulnerability shouldn't bypass both layers simultaneously.

<Pitfall title="The 'Air Gap' That Isn't">

Many organizations believe their OT network is air-gapped when it isn't. Common hidden connections include:

- **Vendor modems**: a dial-up or cellular modem installed by a control system vendor for remote support, never documented in the network diagram
- **Wireless access points**: installed by operators for convenience, bridging directly into the OT VLAN
- **USB bridges**: a laptop that connects to both the corporate Wi-Fi and a PLC programming port simultaneously
- **Historian sync**: a "one-way" ODBC connection that actually allows bidirectional queries

Before assuming air-gap integrity, conduct a physical cable trace and RF scan. Tools like Nmap passive mode and Zeek's `conn.log` will surface unexpected connections within minutes of deployment.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="A zone contains a SCADA server that, if compromised, could allow an attacker to misoperate a chemical plant — causing potential worker injuries. What Security Level should this zone target?">

<div>

**SL 3** — Intentional attack with sophisticated means, with potential for serious physical harm.

SL 2 covers simple intentional attacks with moderate consequence. Potential worker injuries in a chemical plant cross the threshold into "serious injury," which is the SL 3 consequence category.

If the scenario involved mass-casualty potential or national infrastructure (e.g., a water treatment plant serving a major city), SL 4 would apply. In practice, SL 3 is the target for most safety-critical industrial sites.

Note: the target SL also drives the required controls. At SL 3, FR 1 (authentication) requires multi-factor authentication (SR 1.1 + RE 1), and FR 5 (data flow restriction) requires encrypted conduits (SR 5.2 + RE 1) — requirements that are optional at SL 2.
