## The STAMPED Method

When selecting or replacing a hydraulic hose, use the acronym **STAMPED** as your checklist. It ensures you consider every critical factor before choosing a hose assembly.

![STAMPED acronym checklist: S=Size, T=Temperature, A=Application, M=Material, P=Pressure, E=Ends of couplings, D=Delivery](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m2/stamped-acronym.png)

---

## S — Size (Dash Numbers)

Hose size is identified using **Dash Numbers** — the industry standard for describing hose and coupling size.

- Dash numbers represent the hose **Inside Diameter (ID) in sixteenths of an inch**
- A **-4** hose = 4/16" = **¼" ID**
- A **-8** hose = 8/16" = **½" ID**

> ⚠️ **Do not use the hose OD to identify its size.** Different hose constructions vary in wall thickness and OD — always measure the ID.

### Dash Numbers Reference Table

| Dash No. | ID (Inches) | ID (mm) |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| -2 | 1/8 | 3.2 |
| -3 | 3/16 | 4.8 |
| -4 | 1/4 | 6.4 |
| -5 | 5/16 | 7.9 |
| -6 | 3/8 | 9.5 |
| -8 | 1/2 | 12.7 |
| -10 | 5/8 | 15.9 |
| -12 | 3/4 | 19.0 |

| -20 | 1-1/4 | 31.8 |
| -24 | 1-1/2 | 38.1 |
| -32 | 2 | 50.8 |

### Replacing a Hose — Determining the Right Size

1. **Easiest method:** Read the layline printed on the original hose
2. **If layline is unreadable:** Cut the original hose and measure the inside diameter
3. **Also note:** Count and identify the number of reinforcement layers

## T — Temperature

You must consider **three temperature factors**:

- **a) Fluid temperature** — the temperature of the fluid being conveyed
- **b) Ambient temperature** — the temperature of the surrounding environment (e.g., near a hot manifold)
- **c) System temperature** — heat generated by the hydraulic system itself (affected by hose length, friction, and hose size)

### Important Temperature Rules

> ⚠️ **Never exceed the manufacturer's maximum operating temperature.** Doing so risks equipment breakdown and injury.

- Intermittent temperature surges (up to 10% of operating time) are acceptable
- **Do NOT** expose a hose to maximum temperature and maximum working pressure **at the same time** — this drastically shortens hose life
- When routing near hot manifolds, protect the hose with a **heat shield**

---

## A — Application

Most of the time you simply duplicate the original hose. When that's not possible, answer these questions:

- Did the original hose give adequate service life?
- Where will the hose be used? What equipment type?
- What are the working and surge pressures?
- Is it a suction application?
- What are the fluid and ambient temperatures?
- Is the fluid compatible with the hose?
- What are the routing requirements and minimum bend radius?
- Are there government/industry standards to meet (USCG, DOT/FMSS, MSHA)?
- Is non-conductive hose required? *(Critical for aerial equipment)*
- Is excessive abrasion expected?
- What is the expected service life?

> For **gaseous applications** (where permeation can occur), exercise additional caution in selecting the proper hose.

---

## M — Material to Be Conveyed

The hose **tube, cover, and couplings** (including O-rings) must all be **chemically compatible** with the fluid.

### Permeation
Permeation is the seepage of fluid through the hose wall — it results in fluid loss and can occur with any fluid. If permeation through the tube and reinforcement is likely, you can **pinprick the hose cover** to prevent fluid buildup and blistering (commonly done in pressure washer applications to release steam).

### Volatile Fluids — Handle with Extra Care

These fluids require special attention due to their volatility. Improper hose selection creates liability and environmental risk:

- Liquid and gas fuels
- Refrigerants (134A)
- Fuel oil
- Natural gas

> **Note:** All block-type couplings contain Nitrile O-Rings — these must also be compatible with the system fluid.

---

## P — Pressure

You must know the **system pressure, including pressure spikes**. The hose working pressure must be **equal to or greater than** the system pressure.

> Gates does NOT recommend using hose on applications where pressure spikes exceed the published working pressure of the hose.

**Burst pressure** is for destructive testing only — not for operational use. The minimum burst pressure is typically **4× the maximum working pressure**.

### Two Types of Pressure Systems

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Static** | Confined liquids perform work; force multiplied between containers | Hydraulic jack |
| **Dynamic** | Fluid-in-motion transfers energy to a driven element | Backhoe |

### Impulse vs. Spike
- **Impulse** — a pressure surge usually caused by the hydraulic pump
- **Spike** — a sudden dramatic increase in pressure for a short period (e.g., a backhoe hitting a rock while digging)

### Pressure Drop

Pressure drop is the difference between fluid pressure entering one end of a hose assembly and exiting the other. It occurs naturally but must be managed.

**Causes of pressure drop:**

| Factor | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **Friction** | Fluid rubbing against hose walls and couplings |
| **Viscosity** | Thicker fluids drop more pressure |
| **Ambient temperature** | Cold = higher viscosity = more drop |
| **Fluid temperature** | Warmer fluid = thinner = less drop |
| **Hose length** | Longer = more friction = more drop |
| **Hose ID (size)** | Smaller ID = higher velocity = greater drop |
| **Couplings/adapters** | Bends and bore changes increase drop |
| **Flow rate** | Higher flow = greater drop for same size hose |

**Example:** If your system needs 4,000 psi output and the pressure drop is 150 psi, your input pressure must be **4,150 psi**.

---

## E — Ends of Couplings

The coupling ends must match the ports your hose assembly connects to. Consult the **Gates Crimp Data Chart** for recommended hose and coupling combinations. (Couplings are covered in detail in Module 4.)

---

## D — Delivery (Quantity and Volume)

The hose must be sized to transport the required volume of fluid efficiently.

- **Under-sizing** a hose → increased pressure loss and heat
- **Over-sizing** a hose → unnecessary cost, weight, and bulk

If your replacement hose has the same ID as the original, the system is most likely properly sized.

## Gates SAE Hose Nomenclature

Gates hose part numbers follow a standardized naming convention that tells you the hose size, spec, and cover type at a glance.

### Standard SAE Nomenclature — Example: **8G2**

![Gates SAE hose nomenclature diagram showing 8G2: 8 = dash size (8/16 = ½"), G = SAE 100R Spec (SAE 100R2 two-wire), 2 = thin cover no skiving required](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m2/sae-nomenclature.png)

| Position | Value | Meaning |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **8** | Dash size | 8/16" = ½" ID |
| **G** | SAE 100R Spec | SAE 100R2 (2-wire braid) |
| **2** | Cover type | Thin cover, no skiving required |

---

## Gates MegaSys® Nomenclature

MegaSys® hoses have **half the bend radius** of their SAE counterpart — making them significantly more flexible.

![MegaSys flexibility diagram showing a MegaSys hose requiring only 12" of length vs 22" for a standard SAE 100R4 hose between two ports](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m2/megasys-flex-diagram.png)

### Benefits of MegaSys®
- Reduces hose length requirements **up to 47%**
- Decreases inventory requirements
- Routes easily in small spaces
- Fewer bent tube couplings needed
- Easier to plumb and bend than conventional hoses

### MegaSys® Nomenclature — Example: **8M3K**

![MegaSys nomenclature diagram showing 8M3K: 8 = dash size (8/16 = ½"), M = MegaSys designation (½ SAE bend radius), 3K = 3,000 psi working pressure](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m2/megasys-nomenclature.png)

| Position | Value | Meaning |
|----------|-------|---------|
| **8** | Dash size | 8/16" = ½" ID |
| **M** | MegaSys® | ½ SAE bend radius |
| **3K** | Pressure rating | 3,000 psi (K = Roman numeral for 1,000) |
