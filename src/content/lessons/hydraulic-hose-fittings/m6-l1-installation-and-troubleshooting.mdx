## Installing Hose Assemblies

Male-fitting-to-port connections come in four configurations:
1. **Solid male** (MP, MB, MBSPT, etc.)
2. **Male swivels** (MPX, MBX, MIX)
3. **Block-style adapters with lock nuts**
4. **Flanges** (FL, FLH, FLC, FLK)

---

### Solid Male Connections
- Use **Teflon® tape** on tapered threads for easy installation and better seal
- Apply tape **only on upper threads** — NOT over the first thread (could contaminate the system)
- Hand tighten first, then torque properly with a wrench on the hex
- **Never use two solid males on the same hose assembly** — hose rotation is necessary for installation

### Male Swivel Connections
- Simply thread the male into the port — no hose rotation needed
- Use a wrench to torque properly
- Swivel couplings (except MIX) have **internal O-rings** that must be compatible with the fluid

### Block-Style Adapters with Lock Nuts
- Rotate the block and thread the fitting into the port
- When nearly tight, hold the block in the desired position and tighten the lock nut against the port

### Flange Connections (FL, FLH, FLC, FLK)
1. Apply a small amount of oil to the O-ring and place it in the fitting groove
2. Install the flange fitting over the port
3. Install the clamp halves over the flange head and thread in the bolts by hand
4. Tighten with a wrench using a **crossing (X) pattern**
5. Torque properly

### Female Swivel Connections
- Rotate the swivel nut over the solid male threads — **never use swivel female with swivel male**
- Once hand tight, hold the backup hex while tightening the swivel nut to prevent stem rotation and hose twist

### Using Adapters During Installation
Install the adapter first, then the hose assembly. Use adapters to:
1. Avoid fitting orientation on both ends
2. Make a size jump when jump-size fittings are not available
3. Ease port connection and hose installation
4. Change to a different thread configuration (including international)
5. Provide better flow and reduce pressure drop

## Hose Assembly Routing Tips

Proper routing is as critical as proper assembly. Hose can **elongate up to 2%** or **contract up to 4%** under pressure — always account for this.

(5) Use elbows and adapters to relieve strain. (6) Route hose to avoid rubbing and abrasion.](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m6/hose-routing-1.png)

![More Wrong vs Right diagrams: (7) Do not twist hose during installation. (8) Allow slack for straight runs. (9) Distribute movement on flexing applications. (10) Clamp at plane changes. (11) Use angle adapter when radius is below minimum. (12) Use angle adapters to avoid tight bends. (13) Prevent twisting by bending in same plane as port motion. (14) Route directly with 45° or 90° adapters to avoid excess length.](/courses/images/hydraulic-hose-fittings/m6/hose-routing-2.png)

### Key Routing Rules

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Keep bend radii **as large as possible** | Tight bends stress reinforcement and restrict flow |
| **Never twist** hose during installation | Twisting misaligns reinforcement; reduces pressure resistance by up to 90% |
| Allow **slack in straight runs** | Hose shortens under pressure; slack prevents tugging at fittings |
| Do **not clamp at bends** | Curves must absorb length changes when pressurized |
| Keep hose **away from heat sources** | Ambient heat shortens hose life; use heat shields or insulation if unavoidable |
| Route to **avoid abrasion** | Use clamps of the correct size — too large lets hose move and abrade |
| Use **45° or 90° adapters** for direct routing | Reduces hose length, improves appearance and flow |
| Do **not clamp high and low pressure lines together** | Different pressure responses cause hose-to-hose abrasion |

## Troubleshooting: Hose Failure

Your goal is to identify the **cause**, then take **corrective action**. Here are the most common failures:

---

### 🔴 Problem: Cover Erosion
The cover is damaged, exposing the reinforcement.

**Causes:** Abrasion (hose rubbing equipment, other hoses, or objects); non-compatible fluids (toxic chemicals, acids, detergents)

**Solution:** Use nylon or urethane **sleeving** or **spring guards** to protect the cover.

---

### 🔴 Problem: Hose Burst Mid-Length
The hose has burst some distance from the coupling ends.

**Causes:**
1. **Excessive pressure surges** exceeding maximum operating pressure
2. **Excessive flexing, kinking, or crushing** — causes reinforcement fatigue
3. **Exceeding minimum bend radius** — opens large gaps in reinforcement

**Solutions:**
1. Measure operating pressure with a gauge; select a hose rated for the surge pressure
2. Bundle hoses together so they all flex in the same direction; clamp to equipment
3. Re-route or change the fitting to eliminate excessive stress

---

### 🔴 Problem: Hose Burst at Coupling End
**Causes:** Insufficient hose slack; excessive bending and flexing; **over-crimped** hose end (crushes reinforcement)

**Solutions:**
1. Increase hose length to accommodate pressure-induced contraction
2. Increase bend radius at the coupling exit
3. Reduce bending stress using **bend restrictors**
4. Replace with a properly crimped assembly per crimp data chart
5. Check for heat or fluid incompatibility damage

---

### 🔴 Problem: Coupling Leaks at Thread or Seat
**Causes:**
1. Missing or damaged O-ring
2. Damaged threads or seat angle
3. Thread misalignment
4. Incompatible thread ends or seat angles
5. Over- or under-torquing

**Solutions:**
1. Install a **new O-ring** — never reuse; must be fluid-compatible
2. Inspect and replace damaged threads or seat
3. Replace misaligned coupling carefully
4. Use the **Gates Thread Identification Kit (Part No. 86580)** to confirm compatibility
5. Torque to the proper specification — over-torquing stretches threads; under-torquing fails to seal

---

### 🔴 Problem: Fluid Seeping from Ferrule End
**Causes:**
1. Hose not fully inserted during assembly
2. Under-crimped coupling
3. Excessive vibration, flexing, and tugging weakening the interface

**Solution:** Replace the hose assembly — there is no repair for an under-crimped or under-inserted assembly.

---

### 🔴 Problem: Coupling "Blow-Off"
The coupling has completely released from the hose.

**Causes:**
1. Under- or over-torquing
2. Improper die selection
3. Improper skiving
4. Incorrect fitting/hose combination
5. For two-piece couplings: ferrule not engaged in stem locking collar
6. Mixing manufacturers' hose and couplings

**Solutions:**
- Replace the assembly with adequate slack to prevent hose pulling away under pressure
- **Never mix manufacturers' hose, couplings, or crimpers**

---

### 🔴 Problem: Cover Cracked / Hardened
The cover has cracks and appears stiff or hardened.

**Causes:** Excessive heat; ozone exposure; using undersized hose (increases velocity and heat)

**Solution:** Select a hose meeting the temperature and flow requirements; re-route away from heat sources; consider **Gates HeatGuard™ Protective Sheathing**

---

### 🔴 Problem: Hose Assembly Is Twisted
The spiraling layline is the telltale sign — the hose bends in two planes.

**Causes:** Twisting during installation; incorrect assembly procedure

**Impact:** Twisting misaligns reinforcement; can reduce service life by **up to 90%**

**Solution:** Re-route so hose bends in only one plane; use bent tube or block-style couplings and adapters; when installing male and female on the same assembly, install the male (non-swivel) end first

---

### 🔴 Problem: Equipment Sluggish and Unresponsive
**Causes:**
- Fluid incompatibility — tube swells and deteriorates
- Excessively high fluid temperatures — tube bulges near coupling
- Improper hose assembly (wrong size or coupling configuration)

**Solution:** Replace with a hose compatible with the fluid (see Chemical Resistance Table in Gates Catalog); restore original hose size and coupling ends

---

### 🔴 Problem: Blistered Cover
Blisters form on the outside of the hose.

**Causes:** Incompatible fluids or compressed gases permeating through the tube and collecting under the cover; trapped air

**Solutions:**
- Replace with a compatible hose
- For compressed gas: **pin-prick** the cover to allow gas to escape
- Textile covers eliminate blistering
- Bleed the system to remove trapped air

---

### 🔴 Problem: Crushed Hose
A section is flattened; reinforcement is weakened; flow is restricted.

**Solution:** Determine the damage source; re-route or guard the hose; replace the damaged section

---

### 🔴 Problem: Pinholes in C14 (PTFE) Tube
Fluid escapes through the cover; dissecting the tube reveals black burn areas and pinholes from **electrostatic discharge**.

**Solution:** Replace with a **conductive PTFE hose (Gates C14CT)** — electrostatic charge conducts to end fittings instead of discharging through the tube wall
