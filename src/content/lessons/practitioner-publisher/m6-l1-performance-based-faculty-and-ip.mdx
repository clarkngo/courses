## The Context You're Actually Walking Into

Before evaluating any specific practitioner-publisher offer, you need the baseline data on the academic labor market you're entering. It's less favorable to instructors than most industry practitioners assume.

**AAUP (2025). "Data Snapshot: Tenure and Contingency in US Higher Education, Fall 2023." *Academe*, Spring 2025.**

- **Access:** [aaup.org](https://www.aaup.org/academe/issues/spring-2025/data-snapshot-tenure-and-contingency-us-higher-education-fall-2023)

The headline figures: **68.2% of faculty members nationally held contingent appointments** — part-time or full-time non-tenure-track — in fall 2023, up from about 47% in 1987. Nearly half of all faculty (49%) were employed part time, up from 33% in 1987. Contingency rates by institution type range from about 51% at Research I universities to roughly 83% at associate's colleges.

This is the market a practitioner-publisher offer is being made inside of. That matters for one specific reason: **the practitioner-publisher model is easy to mis-sell as a special opportunity when it's structurally identical to ordinary adjunct hiring with better production values.** Your job before signing anything is to identify which one you're actually being offered.

---

## The Test: Does the Compensation Structure Reflect What You're Bringing?

The legacy adjunct model pays for teaching hours and nothing else — a rate per course or per credit hour, regardless of whether the instructor also built the department's most-viewed public content and brought 8,000 subscribers with them. If a practitioner-publisher offer pays exactly that same flat, teaching-hours-only rate, the institution is capturing the value of your audience, your repo, and your workshop revenue history for free, while still classifying you inside the 68.2% contingent-appointment statistic above.

A genuine performance-based model should include compensation components a flat adjunct rate doesn't:

| Component | What it should reflect | Red flag if missing |
|---|---|---|
| Base teaching rate | Standard classroom/course delivery | — (this alone is the legacy model) |
| Enrollment attribution bonus | Students who arrived via your funnel (Module 5's segmented data) | Institution keeps 100% of tuition upside from your audience |
| Workshop revenue share | Your paid workshops (Module 4) feeding the Open edX pipeline | Institution takes workshop revenue with no split back to you |
| Content/royalty terms | Ongoing value of your videos/repo continuing to attract learners after a semester ends | One-time payment for content that keeps generating enrollment for years |

<Pitfall title={`"Exposure" and "Co-Branding" as a Substitute for Compensation`}>

A common version of this mismatch: an institution offers "co-branding" — your name and content associated with a respected university — as if it substitutes for the missing performance-based components above. Co-branding has real value (it's explicitly one of this model's institutional benefits), but it is not compensation, and offers that lean heavily on it instead of a written enrollment-attribution or revenue-share formula are worth scrutinizing closely. Ask specifically: **what is the formula, in writing, for how my funnel's enrollment numbers translate into pay?** If there isn't one, there's no performance-based model — there's a standard adjunct contract with better marketing.

</Pitfall>

## The Other Half of the Deal: Who Owns Your Content

Compensation is only half the negotiation. The other half — often skipped, and consequential for years — is who owns the videos, the repo, and the course materials after the relationship with the institution ends or changes.

**Straumsheim, C. (2014, March 17). "If a MOOC Instructor Moves, Who Keeps the Intellectual Property Rights?" *Inside Higher Ed*.**

- **Access:** [insidehighered.com](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/2014/03/18/if-mooc-instructor-moves-who-keeps-intellectual-property-rights)

This reporting on early MOOC-era IP disputes documents genuinely different institutional approaches, with no single industry norm: **MIT's** policy let faculty keep teaching rights elsewhere but not the produced recordings themselves — the institution retained the footage and a license to continue running the course. **Harvard's** approach called for shared rights when the university's involvement was "more than incidental." **Duke** treated MOOC content the same as a traditional course, granting faculty a license for "all traditional, customary or reasonable academic uses." **Princeton** sidestepped the dispute by framing the MOOC as a "21st-century textbook" rather than resolving ownership directly.

The practical lesson from a decade of unresolved variation across even the most resourced institutions: **do not assume your institution's IP policy defaults in your favor, and do not assume it matches what a different department, or a different school, told a colleague.** Read your specific agreement, for this specific content, before you publish anything under it.

<CodeLab title="Four Questions to Get Answered in Writing Before You Publish">

Before your first piece of content goes out under any institutional agreement:

1. If you leave this institution, can you keep teaching from your own repo and re-uploading your own videos elsewhere — or does the institution retain exclusive rights to the recordings, the way MIT's early policy did?
2. Does your enrollment-attribution bonus (if any) apply to students who found the institution through content you published *before* you were formally affiliated, or only content published after?
3. If your workshop revenue funds a cohort that later becomes an Open edX course, what's the revenue-share formula for that specific transition — not a general statement about "co-branding value"?
4. Is any of this in a signed document, or only a verbal understanding from a department chair who may not be in that role in three years?

Get all four in writing. A verbal "of course you'll keep your channel" is not a substitute for a clause.

</CodeLab>
