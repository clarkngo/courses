## The Destination, and Its Credibility Problem

Open edX is where a validated workshop module gets wrapped in assessment, formal credit, and a credential — a certificate, a badge, or a stackable pathway toward a degree. It's the funnel's revenue and legitimacy destination. It's also entering a marketplace with a real, documented credibility problem, and you need to know the scale of that problem before you design your own credential into it.

---

## The Evidence: Most Credentials Don't Move Wages

**Burning Glass Institute & American Enterprise Institute (2025). "Holding Credentials Accountable to Outcomes."**

- **Access:** [burningglassinstitute.org](https://www.burningglassinstitute.org/research/holding-credentials-accountable-to-outcomes)
- **Related tool:** [Credential Value Index (CVI)](https://www.burningglassinstitute.org/cvi) — a free, open-access index scoring the real labor-market value of over 23,000 non-degree credentials

This analysis, built on 65 million career records, catalogued more than 23,000 credentials with meaningful labor-market representation out of a marketplace of over 1.1 million credentials total. Its central finding: **only about 1 in 8 credentials correlate with material wage gains** for the people who hold them. The strongest programs produce wage gains approaching $5,000 and real upward mobility. The rest — the large majority — don't move outcomes in any measurable way, regardless of how the credential is marketed.

That statistic should reframe how you think about the Open edX stage. The goal isn't to issue a credential — issuing one is nearly free and nearly meaningless on its own. The goal is to be one of the roughly 12% whose credential shows up in a hiring manager's actual decision, or a raise, or a promotion.

<Pitfall title="Confusing Completion With Outcome">

A completion certificate answers one question: did the learner finish the material? It does not answer the question the Burning Glass Institute/AEI data says actually matters: **did holding this credential change anything about the learner's career?**

A credential built around your Module 4 workshop's checkable, real-world outcome (a working repo, a specific deployed skill, a portfolio artifact an employer can actually inspect) is a fundamentally different signal than a certificate that only attests to attendance. If your Open edX offering can't point to what a graduate can concretely *do* that they couldn't do before — something an employer could verify, not just take on faith — you're building toward the 7-out-of-8 outcome, not the 1-out-of-8.

</Pitfall>

## Designing for the 1-in-8, Not the 8-in-8

Three design principles follow directly from the Burning Glass Institute/AEI finding:

1. **Anchor the credential to a specific, verifiable skill**, not a topic area. "Certificate in Cloud Computing" is a topic. "Certificate in deploying a production RAG pipeline with cost monitoring" — backed by the actual repo from Module 2 as portfolio evidence — is a verifiable skill.
2. **Make the underlying work inspectable.** A credential that links to a public, real artifact (a repo, a deployed project, a workshop deliverable) gives an employer something to check beyond your institution's name — closing exactly the gap between "completed" and "capable" that the wage-gain data is measuring.
3. **Track your own outcomes, don't just issue and move on.** The institutions best positioned to defend a credential's value five years from now are the ones already collecting data on what their graduates did next — the same discipline the Burning Glass Institute/AEI report is asking the whole market to adopt.

<CodeLab title="Write Your Credential's Verifiability Statement">

Before your first cohort reaches Open edX, draft one paragraph, written for a skeptical hiring manager, not a prospective student:

1. Name the single specific, checkable thing a graduate can do that they could not do before
2. Name the artifact that proves it (a repo, a project, a deployed system) — something the employer can actually look at, not just a transcript line
3. State how you'd know, a year later, whether this credential correlated with any real career outcome for that graduate — and commit to actually checking

If you can't fill in all three, the credential isn't ready for Open edX yet — it's still a Module 4 workshop.

</CodeLab>

<Quiz question="A department wants to launch ten new micro-credentials quickly, one per popular workshop topic, to build out the Open edX catalog fast. Based on this lesson, what's the strongest, evidence-grounded objection to that plan as stated?">

<div>

**Speed and topic coverage are not what the Burning Glass Institute/AEI data says predicts a credential's value — verifiable, employer-checkable outcomes are, and roughly 7 out of 8 credentials in the current market fail on exactly that dimension.** The objection isn't "don't launch ten credentials" — it's that launching quickly on topic coverage alone, without first defining the specific checkable skill and inspectable artifact for each one (per the CodeLab above), reproduces the exact failure mode the 65-million-career analysis documented at scale. The stronger version of the same plan ties each of the ten credentials to a workshop that already has a real, checkable deliverable from Module 4 — turning "ten credentials fast" into "ten credentials with a defensible claim to being in the useful minority."
