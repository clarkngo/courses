## The Holding Layer

Between a video someone watched once and a workshop they pay for, most learners need a place to exist without committing to either. A Slack workspace, a Discord, a forum, or a mailing list functions as that holding layer — where past viewers, active repo contributors, and program alumni coexist and interact without a transaction attached to any of it.

This isn't a novel idea packaged for content marketing. It has a specific, well-established academic foundation.

---

## The Actual Theory: Situated Learning and Communities of Practice

**Lave, J., & Wenger, E. (1991). *Situated Learning: Legitimate Peripheral Participation.* Cambridge University Press.**

**Wenger, E. (1998). *Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity.* Cambridge University Press.**

Lave and Wenger's original contribution was the idea of **legitimate peripheral participation**: newcomers to a practice learn not primarily by direct instruction, but by participating — even in a minor, peripheral way — in the real activity of a community that already does the thing they want to learn. A new contributor answering easy questions in your Discord, or a lurker who reads three months of discussion before posting, is engaged in exactly this kind of participation, and it's genuine learning, not a lesser substitute for it.

Wenger's 1998 elaboration names three conditions that make a group a functioning community of practice rather than just a group of people who happen to be in the same channel:

1. **Mutual engagement** — members actually interact, not just co-exist in the same space
2. **A joint enterprise** — a shared goal or problem the group is negotiating together, not a topic imposed from outside
3. **A shared repertoire** — a common vocabulary, in-jokes, tools, and reference points that build up over time

<Pitfall title="A Slack With 500 Members Isn't a Community of Practice">

Membership count is not the same as mutual engagement. A channel with 500 people who joined after a YouTube video and never posted again satisfies none of Wenger's three conditions — there's no mutual engagement, no negotiated joint enterprise, and no shared repertoire has had time to form.

The practical implication: **retention design matters more than acquisition design at this stage.** A smaller, actively-engaged 50-person hub with a real joint enterprise (say, a shared project, a recurring office-hours session, or a running Q&A thread) is functioning as a community of practice in a way a passive 500-person list is not — regardless of which number looks better in a pitch deck.

</Pitfall>

## Now, the Number You'll See Everywhere — and Why to Distrust It As Stated

If you research "community-led growth," you will immediately encounter a specific claim, repeated across dozens of marketing blogs: **communities reduce customer acquisition cost (CAC) by roughly 32%**, sometimes inflated to "5–10x lower" or "$6.40 return per dollar invested."

This is worth stopping on, because it's a useful case study in exactly the evidence-quality problem this course asks you to watch for in your *own* funnel claims later. Tracing that number back through the sources that cite it does not lead to a peer-reviewed study, a disclosed methodology, or even a single consistently-cited original source — it leads to a chain of marketing blogs and vendor content citing each other, with the number changing (32%, 5x, 10x, $6.40) depending on which site you land on.

That doesn't mean community-led growth doesn't reduce CAC. It plausibly does, for reasons the Lave & Wenger and Wenger citations above actually support: word-of-mouth within a genuine community of practice is a real, low-cost acquisition channel. What it means is that **the specific number is not something you should put in a funnel plan you're presenting to a dean, a department chair, or a corporate training partner** — because the first person who asks "where does that statistic come from?" will find the same trail of uncited blog posts you just found.

The one source in this space with an actual disclosed methodology is an annual practitioner survey, not a peer-reviewed study — cite it as that, not as academic evidence:

**CMX (2024/2025). *Community Industry Trends Report.* cmxhub.com.**

- **Access:** [cmxhub.com/reports](https://www.cmxhub.com/reports)

This is a self-reported industry survey of community professionals, useful for directional benchmarking against peers in your field, but it is not a controlled study and should never be cited with the same confidence as the Lave & Wenger or Wenger findings above.

<CodeLab title="Audit a Statistic Before It Goes in Your Funnel Plan">

Practice the check you'll need for Module 6's capstone. Take any specific number you're tempted to cite in your own funnel plan (a conversion rate, a CAC figure, a completion percentage) and answer:

1. Can you trace it to a named, dated, original source — not a blog post that cites another blog post?
2. Is the underlying data a controlled study, a large observational dataset, or a self-reported practitioner survey? Rank those three in that order of strength.
3. Does the number match your own domain, audience size, and platform — or is it from a different industry, company size, or era entirely?
4. If you can't answer #1, cite the number as "commonly reported, unverified" or drop it — don't present it as established fact.

</CodeLab>
