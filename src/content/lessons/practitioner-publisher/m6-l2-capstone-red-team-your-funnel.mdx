## Why Red-Team Your Own Plan

Every funnel stage in this course has a documented failure mode: skipping the active-experimentation stage in your repo (Module 2), mistaking a passive Slack for a community of practice (Module 3), citing an untraceable statistic (Module 3), running a seminar as a disguised sales pitch (Module 4), designing a workshop without an honest completion target (Module 4), issuing a credential with no verifiable outcome (Module 5), or signing an agreement with no written revenue formula (Module 6).

A funnel plan that hasn't been checked against each of these failure modes isn't a weak plan — it's an untested one. This capstone builds your plan and then attacks it, the same way the compliance and security courses on this platform run a final red-team assessment against a policy before it goes live.

<Pitfall title="The Three Failure Patterns That Take Down Otherwise-Good Plans">

Across every module, the failures that actually sink a funnel plan aren't random — they cluster into three repeatable patterns:

**1. Skipping a stage to move faster.** Jumping straight from video to enrollment, or from repo to credential, without the community and workshop stages in between, doesn't just weaken conversion — it removes the self-selection and accountability mechanisms (Modules 3–5) that make the *later* stages work. A shortened funnel isn't a faster version of the same funnel; it's a different, weaker one.

**2. Citing an unverifiable number as if it were established fact.** This course caught one real example of this mid-construction — the "32% CAC reduction" claim in Module 3 traced back to nothing but other marketing blogs. Any number in your plan that you can't trace to a named, dated source with a disclosed methodology should be labeled as unverified or removed, not presented with false confidence.

**3. Signing before the compensation and IP terms are in writing.** Module 6, Lesson 1's four questions exist because "we'll figure it out later" is the single most common way a practitioner-publisher ends up contributing real audience value and receiving standard adjunct compensation for it.

</Pitfall>

<CodeLab title="Part 2 — Red-Team Each Stage of Your Own Plan">

For every row you filled in above, answer honestly:

1. **Could this stage be skipped, and would anyone downstream notice?** If your workshop could disappear and your Open edX enrollment wouldn't change, the workshop isn't actually doing conversion work — it's decoration.
2. **What's the weakest evidence claim in this row?** Rate it: peer-reviewed study, large observational dataset, self-reported industry survey, or unverified/anecdotal. Any row resting on the last category needs either better evidence or an honest caveat.
3. **What happens to this stage if you stop actively maintaining it for six months?** A community hub or repo that decays without you isn't a compounding asset (this course's core promise, per Module 1) — it's a project with a maintenance cost you haven't priced in.
4. **If a hiring committee or dean asked you to defend one number in this row, which number would you struggle to defend — and what would you say instead?**

</CodeLab>

<Quiz question="After completing the red-team exercise, you find that your 'Community' row is the weakest: you have a Discord with 200 members but almost no mutual engagement, no recurring joint activity, and no shared repertoire. What does this course's framework say you should do?">

<div>

**Treat this as the priority gap to fix before investing further in the Seminar and Workshop stages, because a passive 200-member Discord doesn't satisfy Wenger's (1998) three conditions for a community of practice — mutual engagement, a joint enterprise, and a shared repertoire — and Module 3 established that retention design, not member count, is what this stage is actually for.** The fix isn't necessarily "grow the Discord bigger" — a larger passive list makes the underlying problem worse, not better. It's introducing an actual joint enterprise (a recurring activity members do together, not just a channel they're subscribed to) so the group starts satisfying Wenger's conditions before you build a Seminar and Workshop funnel on top of a retention stage that isn't retaining anyone.

## Where This Leaves You

You now have a six-stage plan, tied to specific research rather than a marketing template, with its weakest link identified rather than hidden. That's a materially different starting position than either the legacy "industry professional" hire (Module 1's left-hand column, with no content built until after a contract is signed) or a practitioner who adopts this funnel model without checking any of it against evidence.

The model in this course is a loop, not a ladder: your first cohort of Open edX graduates becomes your next round of workshop mentors, seminar guest speakers, and testimonials — feeding back into Stage 1 and starting the cycle again, this time with real outcome data behind it instead of a plan. That's the point where a practitioner-publisher stops building a funnel and starts running one.
