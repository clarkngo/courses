## The Workshop's Job

The workshop is where a seminar attendee's stated interest becomes a transaction: a scheduled, hands-on, paid session — commonly a 60–90 minute or half-day format, run with cohorts small enough for real feedback, built directly around the GitHub repo from Module 2. This is the first funnel stage with real revenue attached, and it's also the stage most often designed on optimistic, unexamined assumptions about how many registrants will actually finish.

Two pieces of peer-reviewed evidence are directly relevant to getting that design right — one showing what happens at the scale you're *not* trying to replicate, and one that should inform your own outcome measurement.

---

## What Happens at Scale: The MOOC Completion Record

**Jordan, K. (2015). "Massive open online course completion rates revisited: Assessment, length and attrition." *International Review of Research in Open and Distributed Learning*, 16(3).**

- **Full text:** [IRRODL](https://www.irrodl.org/index.php/irrodl/article/view/2112)

Jordan's analysis of 221 MOOCs found completion rates ranging from 0.7% to 52.1%, with a **median of 12.6%** — and found that longer courses and courses without auto-graded assessment saw systematically lower completion.

**Reich, J., & Ruipérez-Valiente, J. A. (2019). "The MOOC pivot." *Science*, 363(6423), 130–131.**

- **DOI:** [10.1126/science.aav7958](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30630920/)

Reich and Ruipérez-Valiente's follow-up analysis of MIT and Harvard edX courses through 2017–18 found the picture had gotten worse, not better, as the format matured: completion had fallen to **roughly 3.13%**, down from around 6% in 2014–15, and 52% of people who registered never even started the course.

Neither paper is an argument against online education. They're a specific, well-evidenced argument against one particular design: large, open, self-paced, low-accountability enrollment at scale. That's the format your workshop is explicitly *not* using — and knowing exactly why that format underperforms tells you what to protect in your own design.

---

## Why Small, Paid, and Scheduled Changes the Picture

The gap between MOOC-scale completion (single digits) and reported completion for small, cohort-based, paid formats (commonly discussed in the 40–70%+ range by cohort-course platforms) isn't magic — it maps onto the specific factors Jordan (2015) already identified as predicting completion: shorter format, built-in assessment, and — the piece unique to a paid cohort — real accountability to a scheduled group and a sunk financial cost.

<Pitfall title="Citing Platform Completion Statistics as If They Were Peer-Reviewed">

You will encounter specific completion-rate comparisons from cohort-course platforms (Maven, Circle, Ruzuku, and similar) citing numbers like "72% cohort completion vs. 48% self-paced." These are **real operating platforms reporting on their own real user data** — which makes them more useful than an untraceable marketing blog statistic (see Module 3's lesson on the "32% CAC" claim), but they are still self-reported, uncontrolled, platform-specific figures, not peer-reviewed research with a disclosed methodology.

The correct way to use them: cite Jordan (2015) and Reich & Ruipérez-Valiente (2019) as your peer-reviewed evidence for *why* accountability and format length affect completion. Cite platform data, explicitly labeled as such, only as directional industry benchmarking for what your own workshop might realistically achieve — never blend the two into a single unqualified statistic.

</Pitfall>

## Designing Your Workshop Against This Evidence

Three design choices, each traceable to a specific finding above:

1. **Keep it short.** Jordan (2015) found length negatively predicts completion. A 90-minute or single-day workshop protects against the attrition curve that kills long, open-ended formats.
2. **Build in a checkable outcome.** Jordan (2015) found courses with auto-graded or clearly checkable assessment complete at higher rates than those without. Your Module 2 repo's "deliberate failure point, checkable success" design does this work directly.
3. **Charge something, and cap the cohort.** Reich & Ruipérez-Valiente (2019) is a study of a *free*, open, effectively unlimited-enrollment format — the opposite of a paid, capped cohort. A financial commitment and a scheduled group of peers are accountability mechanisms MOOCs at scale don't have by design.

<CodeLab title="Set an Honest Completion Target Before You Launch">

Before opening registration for your first workshop:

1. State your target completion rate as a number, in writing, before the event — not after, when it's tempting to grade on a curve
2. Ground that number in your own format's proximity to the accountability factors above (paid, capped, short, checkable) rather than in an unqualified platform statistic
3. Design one measurable checkpoint during the workshop itself (not just at the end) so you can see attrition happening in real time, the way Jordan (2015) tracked attrition *during* a course, not only at its conclusion
4. After the workshop, record your actual completion rate against your stated target — this becomes real data for your own funnel plan, stronger than any external citation

</CodeLab>

<Quiz question="A colleague proposes turning your paid, 50-person, single-day workshop into a free, unlimited-enrollment, self-paced version 'to reach more people.' Using the evidence in this lesson, what's the most likely consequence, and why?">

<div>

**Completion rates would likely fall sharply — plausibly toward the single-digit range Jordan (2015) and Reich & Ruipérez-Valiente (2019) documented for large, open, self-paced formats — because the change removes exactly the factors this lesson identifies as protective: a financial commitment, a capped and scheduled cohort, and time-bounded structure.** "Reaching more people" through enrollment count and "teaching more people" through completion are different metrics, and the peer-reviewed record on MOOC-scale formats is specifically a record of the first metric rising while the second collapses. The response grounded in evidence isn't "never go open and free" — it's that doing so requires deliberately rebuilding the accountability mechanisms (checkable milestones, cohort structure, some form of commitment) that a paid, capped format gets for free.

</div>
</Quiz>
