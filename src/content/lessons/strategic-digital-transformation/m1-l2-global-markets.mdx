## 00–30 min | Core Business Theory

### Four Questions Before Going Global

Before a company expands internationally, it must answer:

1. Is there a real market here?
2. Who exactly is our customer?
3. How do we reach them?
4. What do we change — and what do we protect?

### The Four Market Entry Modes

| Mode | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Exporting** | Sell domestically-made products abroad | A Korean cosmetics brand selling through US retail |
| **Licensing / Franchising** | Let local partners use your model for a fee | McDonald's global franchise system |
| **Joint Venture** | Co-own a local operation with a local partner | Starbucks entering China with local real estate experts |
| **Wholly Owned Subsidiary** | Build or acquire your own operation entirely | Amazon building its own fulfillment centers globally |

Each mode trades **control** for **speed and local knowledge.** The right choice depends on your risk tolerance, local regulatory environment, and how much your model depends on brand consistency.

### Segmentation: There Is No "Global Customer"

There are only local clusters of similar needs. The four segmentation lenses:

- **Geographic** — country, region, city size, climate
- **Demographic** — age, income, gender, education, occupation
- **Psychographic** — values, lifestyle, personality, social identity
- **Behavioral** — usage frequency, brand loyalty, purchase occasion, benefits sought

### Three Companies That Got This Right

**Starbucks in China** — Did not export the American coffee shop. Redesigned stores as prestige social gathering spaces for a tea-drinking culture. Entered via joint venture. The target customer was not a coffee drinker — it was someone aspiring to a Western professional lifestyle.

**Netflix** — Built a global content library plus local-language originals (Money Heist, Squid Game, Sacred Games). Proved that *behavioral* segmentation — binge-watchers who prefer character-driven drama — cuts across national borders in ways demographic segmentation does not.

**Zara (Inditex)** — Operates in 96 countries but keeps a centralized supply chain in Spain. The product adapts minimally; the logistics model does not change. Speed-to-market is the global value proposition, not local customization.

<Pitfall title="Confusing 'International' With 'Global'">

An international strategy adapts the product for each market — think local menus at McDonald's. A global strategy sells the same thing everywhere — think iPhone pricing logic. Most companies need to choose one as their dominant approach. Trying to do both simultaneously usually produces a product that satisfies no one completely and costs more than either pure strategy.

</Pitfall>

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting

### The Scenario

A strategy analyst must decide whether her company's fitness app should enter Brazil or Indonesia first. She has two hours before a leadership meeting.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Market Entry Comparison">

```
I am a strategy analyst at a fitness technology company. We need to
decide whether to prioritize market entry into Brazil or Indonesia in
the next fiscal year. Compare these two markets across five dimensions:
smartphone penetration, middle-class growth, competitive landscape for
fitness apps, key cultural attitudes toward health and wellness, and
regulatory environment for foreign tech companies. Present the
comparison as a simple table, then write a two-paragraph recommendation
with your top choice and the key risk to monitor.
```

**After reviewing the output, run this follow-up:**

```
Rewrite the recommendation paragraph assuming our company has zero
local brand recognition and a limited budget for influencer marketing.
```

**Teach the constraint principle:** Claude's first output gives you the general case. Adding a constraint (no brand recognition, limited budget) shifts the recommendation. Real strategy is always constrained. Practice prompting *with* constraints.

**Note on AI limitations:** Claude does not have real-time data. Its market analysis is a framework populated with general knowledge — still enormously useful for structuring a real analysis, but always verify specific statistics before presenting to leadership.

</CodeLab>

## 50–80 min | Team Portal Deployment

### Build Module 2: Global Market Profile

**Module 2 must contain:**

1. **Target Country Selection** — one country where your company is not yet the market leader (or a new country you are "entering" for this exercise), with a one-sentence justification
2. **Market Segmentation Map** — a table or structured breakdown of your primary, secondary, and tertiary customer segments in that country
3. **Market Entry Mode Recommendation** — which of the four modes you would use, and why (two to three sentences)
4. **Top Three Risks** — specific to your company + country combination, generated with Claude and edited for accuracy
5. **Opportunity Statement** — one bold, specific claim about why this market is worth entering *right now*

Every claim in your portal must be followed by *"because..."* That is the class standard from this session forward.

<Quiz question="A luxury French perfume brand wants to enter the Indian market. Why might a wholly owned subsidiary be riskier than a joint venture for this specific entry?">

<div>

**Local knowledge is the constraint.**

India's retail landscape is hyper-local — regulatory requirements for foreign-owned retail operations differ by state, consumer scent preferences vary significantly by region, and the distribution network for premium goods is not uniform. A joint venture partner brings regulatory navigation, regional distribution relationships, and consumer insight that a wholly owned subsidiary would need years to build.

The tradeoff: the joint venture partner will also capture margin and may gain IP access. For a brand where the formula *is* the product, protecting IP terms in the JV agreement becomes the critical negotiation.
