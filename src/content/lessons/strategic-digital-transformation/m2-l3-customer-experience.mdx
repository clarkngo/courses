### The Customer Journey

Customers do not appear at checkout. They travel a journey from not knowing a brand exists to recommending it to friends. The five stages:

| Stage | The Customer's Question |
|---|---|
| **Awareness** | "Does this thing exist?" |
| **Consideration** | "Is it right for someone like me?" |
| **Decision** | "Should I buy it now?" |
| **Retention** | "Was that worth it? Will I come back?" |
| **Advocacy** | "Should I tell other people about this?" |

Each stage requires a different message, a different channel, and a different definition of success.

### Three Companies That Mastered Different Stages

**Glossier — Hacking Advocacy First**
Built a $1.8B beauty brand almost entirely without traditional advertising by entering the customer journey at the *advocacy* stage. The strategy: turn existing customers into content creators. User-generated Instagram photos were the marketing. Glossier's own community forum answered product questions better than any sales team. They built advocacy first, then let it pull people backward into awareness. The lesson: you do not have to acquire customers at the top of the funnel if you can engineer enough advocacy at the bottom that it generates its own top.

**Sephora — Omnichannel Journey Design**
A customer sees a TikTok review (awareness) → searches the Sephora app (consideration) → uses the virtual try-on AR tool (decision) → earns Beauty Insider loyalty points (retention) → shares a haul video (advocacy). Every stage is a designed touchpoint. The physical store is now the *experiential* stage of a digital journey — not the primary stage. Sephora's advantage is that they designed the journey as a system, not as a set of disconnected marketing campaigns.

**Zappos — Retention as Strategy**
Became famous by over-investing at the *retention* stage: free overnight shipping, 365-day returns, call center agents authorized to spend up to $100 to resolve any issue without manager approval. Result: 75% of purchases came from repeat customers. The product (shoes) was not differentiating; the experience was. Zappos proved that retention investment can reduce acquisition cost — if your retention rate is high enough, you need fewer new customers to maintain the same revenue.

### Digital Channel Map

| Stage | Priority Channels |
|---|---|
| Awareness | SEO, paid social, influencer, PR |
| Consideration | Content marketing, email, YouTube, comparison sites |
| Decision | Reviews, testimonials, retargeting ads, demos |
| Retention | Loyalty programs, email sequences, push notifications |
| Advocacy | Referral programs, UGC incentives, community |

<Pitfall title="The 'Post Everywhere' Trap">

Every platform requires different content formats, different posting cadences, different audience expectations, and different analytics. A team of two people posting mediocre content on five platforms will always lose to a team of two people posting exceptional content on one platform. For early-stage companies and this course project: pick two channels, do them excellently, and resist the pressure to be everywhere.

</Pitfall>

### The Scenario

A marketing director at a fintech startup must build a customer journey strategy for a new product launch — in 48 hours.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Customer Journey Map">

```
I am the marketing director at a fintech company launching a mobile app
that helps young adults aged 22–35 track their spending and automate
savings. Write a complete customer journey map for this product. For
each of the five stages — Awareness, Consideration, Decision,
Retention, and Advocacy — describe the customer's emotional state,
their primary question at that stage, the one digital channel we should
prioritize, and one specific content idea. Do not give me channel
types — give me the actual content concept. Keep it focused and
actionable.
```

**The power of "not just the channel type — the actual content":** Generic prompts produce generic marketing plans. The constraint "give me the actual content concept" forces specificity. Compare the output with and without that phrase.

**Discussion:** Which of the five stages in the output did Claude do best? Which was weakest — and why might that be? (Hint: Claude has strong pattern-matching for awareness and consideration tactics, but retention and advocacy strategies are more company-specific and harder to generalize.)

## 50–80 min | Team Portal Deployment

### Build Module 6: Customer Journey & Marketing Strategy

**Module 6 must contain:**

1. **Customer Journey Map** — five stages, each covering:
   - Customer emotional state at this stage
   - The primary question they are asking
   - Your priority channel
   - One specific content idea (not a channel type — an actual concept)

2. **Channel Priority Rationale** — a paragraph explaining which two channels you are prioritizing and why those two are the right fit for your company *and* your specific target customer in your target market

3. **One Campaign Concept** — a brief description of a single integrated marketing campaign. Not a channel list — a story: What is the hook? Who is it aimed at? What do you want them to feel and do?

4. **Anti-Pattern Warning** — one marketing mistake your company should avoid, based on your target customer and market. Reference a real brand that made this mistake and what it cost them.

**Cross-module consistency check:** Does your priority channel match the platform behavior of your target customer from Module 2? A B2B software company targeting procurement managers should not list TikTok as its primary awareness channel.

## 80–90 min | Live Push & Review

Two portals displayed. Review focus: Are the five journey stages actually differentiated?

The customer's emotional state in *Awareness* is not the same as in *Decision.* Awareness might be casual curiosity; Decision is often anxiety about making the wrong choice. If the content in each stage feels the same, the journey map is not doing its job.

<Quiz question="A luxury hotel chain wants more five-star reviews on travel sites. Which customer journey stage should they invest in — and what would a smart investment at that stage look like?">

<div>

**Retention and Advocacy — and they are connected.**

Five-star reviews are advocacy. But advocacy does not emerge from nowhere — it is produced by exceptional retention experiences. A smart investment:

- **Retention stage:** Personalized welcome notes referencing past stays, room preference memory across visits, a seamless check-out experience that ends the stay on a high note rather than a queue.
- **Advocacy trigger:** A "private" post-stay email (24 hours after checkout) that thanks the guest personally and includes a direct link to leave a review — *after* the glow of a great stay, not a week later when the feeling has faded.

The mistake most hotels make: they ask for reviews at checkout, when the guest is tired and focused on logistics. The window of peak satisfaction is the day after the trip, when the guest is back home and misses the experience. That is when to ask.
