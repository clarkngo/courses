## 00–30 min | Core Business Theory

### What Is a Business?

Every company in the world — from a street food cart to Apple — can be mapped onto nine building blocks called the **Business Model Canvas** (Osterwalder):

> Value Proposition · Customer Segments · Channels · Customer Relationships · Revenue Streams · Key Resources · Key Activities · Key Partners · Cost Structure

The one concept to internalize today: **a value proposition is not a product.** It is the answer to one question:

> *"Why would a rational person choose you over every alternative — including doing nothing?"*

### Three Companies, Three Lessons

**IKEA** — The value proposition is not furniture. It is *affordable design dignity for people who just moved out.* Flat-pack solves global logistics cost; the showroom experience solves aspiration; low price solves the wallet. Every building block serves that one sentence.

**Airbnb** — The value proposition is not rooms. It is *belonging anywhere.* Hosts are a key partner resource Airbnb does not own but fully depends on. The asymmetry between what they control and what they earn is the business model insight.

**McDonald's** — The value proposition to the *customer* is speed and familiarity. But McDonald's real value proposition to *franchisees* is a proven, low-risk operating system. There are two customers. That is a business model insight most people have never considered.

<Pitfall title="A Feature Is Not a Value Proposition">

*"Our app is fast and easy to use"* describes a feature. *"The only expense tracker that takes less time than the coffee you're trying to afford"* describes a value. The test: can a competitor copy your sentence and still be telling the truth? If yes, you have described a feature, not a value.

</Pitfall>

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting

### The Scenario

A new product manager at a global beverage company has been asked to draft a value proposition for a mineral water brand entering Southeast Asia — in 72 hours. She opens Claude.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Value Proposition Generator">

Copy, run, and refine this prompt in Claude or Gemini:

```
I am a product manager at a global beverage company. We are launching
a premium mineral water brand in Southeast Asia targeting urban
professional women aged 25–40. Draft three distinct value propositions
for this product — one focused on health, one on sustainability, and
one on status signaling. For each, write one sentence that a billboard
could use and two sentences explaining the underlying customer
psychology. Use plain business language, no jargon.
```

**Anatomy of a strong prompt:**
- **Role** — "I am a product manager"
- **Context** — product, market, and segment
- **Deliverable** — three value props, not just "help me"
- **Format** — billboard sentence + psychology
- **Tone constraint** — plain language, no jargon

Remove any one of these elements and run the prompt again. Watch the output degrade. That degradation is your lesson.

**Discussion:** Which output would you put on a billboard in Bangkok? In Manila? Why does the answer differ by city?

</CodeLab>

## 50–80 min | Team Portal Deployment

### Build Module 1: Company Charter

Your team selects one real or fictional global company you will study for all 13 sessions. Today you publish **Module 1** to your live portal.

**Module 1 must contain:**

1. **Company Name & Sector** — the company you will study all summer
2. **Value Proposition** — one sentence, written in the voice of that company's CEO
3. **Target Customer Profile** — three bullet points: demographics, psychographics, core need
4. **Business Model Snapshot** — a short paragraph mapping at least four of the nine BMC blocks to your company
5. **Team Byline** — team name, member names, and country of origin for each member

Use Claude to generate a draft, then edit it for accuracy and voice. Your instructor will challenge any value proposition that describes a feature rather than a value.

<Quiz question="Apple's value proposition is often described as 'premium technology.' Is that a strong value proposition? Why or why not?">

<div>

**No — it describes a category and a price tier, not a value.**

A stronger version: *"The only technology that feels like it was designed specifically for you."* Or: *"Devices that disappear into your life instead of demanding attention from it."*

The test: could Samsung write the same sentence? If yes, it is not a differentiated value proposition — it is a market position description. Strong value propositions are specific enough that only one company can honestly claim them.

</div>
</Quiz>
