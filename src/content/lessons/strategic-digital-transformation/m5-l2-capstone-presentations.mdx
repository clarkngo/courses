## 00–30 min | Core Business Theory

### What Makes a Great Business Presentation (10 min)

Three principles that separate memorable executive presentations from forgettable ones:

**1. Lead with the so-what.**
Start with your conclusion, not your process. Executives know you did research — they want your judgment. Begin with: *"Here is what we recommend, and here is why it is the right bet."* Then provide the evidence. The BLUF rule — Bottom Line Up Front — is the operating standard in every boardroom, consulting firm, and military briefing in the world.

**2. Evidence, not assertion.**
Every strategic claim must be followed by specific evidence. *"We chose differentiation positioning because our primary customer segment in Japan consistently ranks product quality 2.3× higher than price in purchase decisions"* is evidence-backed. *"We chose differentiation because we think quality matters"* is assertion. Assertion invites challenge. Evidence invites dialogue.

**3. Anticipate resistance.**
The best presenters already know the three hardest questions the audience will ask — and have answered them before being asked. Preempting objections signals preparation and confidence. Getting blindsided by an obvious objection signals neither.

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting — Pressure Testing

### Final AI Demonstration: The Skeptical Board

Before presenting, every team runs this prompt against their own portal content:

<CodeLab title="Team Prompt: Generate the Hardest Questions">

```
You are a skeptical board member reviewing our strategic business plan.
We are [COMPANY NAME], a [SECTOR] company targeting [MARKET] with a
[REVENUE MODEL] business model and a [POSITIONING] competitive
strategy. Our three-year digital transformation roadmap prioritizes
[KEY INITIATIVE]. Our biggest self-identified risk is [YOUR RISK FROM
MODULE 12].

Generate the five hardest questions you would ask us as a skeptical
board member — questions that probe weak assumptions, logical gaps, or
underestimated risks in our plan. For each question, briefly note what
strategic weakness it is testing.
```

Review the five questions. Select the two hardest. Write your answers in your own words — not AI-generated. These become your Q&A preparation for the live presentation.

**The meta-lesson:** You used AI to find the holes in your own argument. That is one of the highest-value uses of AI in executive decision-making — not to generate conclusions, but to stress-test the ones you have already reached.

</CodeLab>

## 50–80 min | Final Portal Deployment

### Build Module 13: Lessons Learned + Team Bio (Final Polish)

**The final 30 minutes serve two purposes:**

**Module 13 — the human layer of the portal:**

1. **Lessons Learned** — one paragraph per team member (written in your own voice, not AI-generated). Answer: *What did you believe about business or strategy at the start of this summer that you now believe differently? What changed your mind — a specific lecture concept, a field trip observation, a site visit moment, a team disagreement?*

2. **Team Bio** — a polished team bio page: each member's name, country of origin, intended major or area of interest, and one sentence describing their professional curiosity going forward.

**Full portal final polish:**
- Navigation flows logically for a first-time visitor
- Tone and formatting are consistent across all thirteen modules
- Every module heading is clear and specific (not "Module 3" — a real section title)
- The executive summary in Module 12 accurately represents what is in the modules beneath it
- No broken links, placeholder text, or draft language visible

**The standard:** Would you send this URL to a professional contact without apologizing for it? If yes, push it live. If not, keep editing.

## 80–90 min | Live Portfolio Showcase

Every team's portal is displayed on the projector in sequence. Each team has **4 minutes:**

- Navigate the live portal — do not read from notes or slides
- State the core strategic argument in the first 30 seconds (BLUF)
- Walk through two or three modules that best represent the strategic logic
- Invite two to three questions from the class

After each presentation, the class identifies one strength and one question they would bring to a real board meeting.

**Close:** The instructor reads one insight from each team's portal aloud to the full class. Not the most polished sentence — the most *specific* one. The insight that could only have come from this team, this company, and this summer.

<Pitfall title="'We Did a Lot of Research' Is Not a Presentation">

Research is the preparation for the presentation. The presentation is the argument that emerges from the research. Teams that walk through their portal module by module — "and then in Module 5 we did our revenue model, and then in Module 6 we did our customer journey" — are presenting their work process, not their strategic conclusions.

The question a board is asking is: *"What should we do, and why?"* Answer that question in the first sentence. Use the modules as evidence, not as the agenda.

</Pitfall>

**Lead with the business, not the course.**

Wrong: *"We built a strategic business plan as part of a summer program where we had to create twelve modules covering value propositions, supply chains, financial statements..."*

Right: *"We developed a three-year market entry strategy for [Company] expanding into [Country]. The core argument is that [Company's] differentiation advantage — [one specific thing] — is uniquely suited to the [target customer segment] there, which is underserved by every current competitor. We built the full financial and operational plan, and the live site is [URL]."*

The difference: the first answer describes the process. The second answer describes the output — and makes the listener want to see it.

The URL matters. The portal is real. You built something that exists in the world. Lead with that.

## What Comes Next

The portal URL is yours. The frameworks are yours. The field trip observations, the site visit insights, the brand analyses, the financial narratives — all of it is documented, published, and accessible to anyone you choose to share it with.

Three things to take forward:

1. **The habit of structured prompting** — every professional problem has a better version of itself that emerges when you specify the role, context, deliverable, format, and constraints. That skill transfers to every job in every industry.

2. **The cross-functional lens** — you have now seen how value propositions connect to pricing, how supply chain connects to financial health, how digital transformation connects to competitive strategy. That integrated view is rare. Most professionals are specialists. You have spent the summer as generalists — on purpose.

3. **The portfolio** — you published real strategic work at age 18 or 19. That is unusual. Use it accordingly.
