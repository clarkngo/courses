## 00–30 min | Core Business Theory

### What Digital Transformation Actually Means

"Digital transformation" is one of the most misused phrases in business. Clarify once and for all:

> **Digital transformation is not buying new software, building an app, or hiring a Chief Technology Officer.**
> It is redesigning how a company creates, delivers, and captures value using digital capabilities — and it requires cultural and organizational change, not just technical change.

A company that digitizes its existing paper-based processes has automated the past. A company that uses digital capabilities to reinvent its business model has transformed.

### The Digital Maturity Model

| Level | Description | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Beginner** | Manual processes dominate | Spreadsheets over systems, limited data use |
| **2 — Literate** | Basic automation, siloed data | Some digital tools, departments not connected |
| **3 — Adopter** | Integrated systems, digital customer channels | ERP in place, e-commerce active |
| **4 — Champion** | Data-driven decisions enterprise-wide | AI in operations, real-time analytics |
| **5 — Native** | Born digital; data is the product | Google, Alibaba, Spotify |

Most established companies sit between Level 2 and Level 3. The journey from Level 3 to Level 4 is where most transformation programs fail — because it requires changing how people make decisions, not just what tools they use.

### Three Case Studies

**Domino's — The Pizza Tech Company**
In 2009, Domino's ran a campaign where its CEO admitted their pizza tasted like cardboard. Then they rebuilt — both the recipe and the company's operating model. Today, 80%+ of US orders are digital. They built their own ordering OS, an AI pizza tracker visible to customers in real time, voice-ordering integration, and drone delivery pilots. Domino's stock price: approximately $3 in 2008; over $500 at its peak. The product got better, but the transformation was digital. The company's competitive moat is now its technology infrastructure, not its pizza recipe.

**Walmart vs. Amazon**
Walmart spent decades building the world's most efficient physical retail supply chain — the original logistics technology company. When Amazon digitized everything Walmart had built and added an infinite digital shelf, Walmart had to rebuild the entire customer experience layer without dismantling the physical infrastructure they depended on for revenue. The result: a decade of painful, expensive catch-up. Walmart's $16B acquisition of Flipkart in India was their counter-move — buying a digital native rather than trying to build one. Still incomplete.

**BBVA — Bank as Technology Company**
Traditional Spanish bank that became a global digital banking benchmark by treating technology as core strategy, not an IT department. Built an API-first architecture that allows third-party apps to plug into BBVA's banking infrastructure. Sold physical branches and reallocated capital to digital. One of the first traditional banks to publish an explicit "we are a technology company that has a banking license" positioning. The result: a digital customer base growing significantly faster than peers, and a cost structure that physical branch competitors cannot match.

### The 3-Zone Framework

Every digital transformation can be organized into three time horizons:

1. **Quick Wins (0–12 months)** — Digitize existing processes, reduce manual work, generate early ROI to fund the larger effort. Risk is low; impact is moderate.
2. **Foundation Building (12–36 months)** — Redesign core business processes, integrate data systems, build digital capabilities that did not exist before. Risk is medium; impact is high.
3. **Competitive Differentiation (36+ months)** — New business models, AI-driven products, data monetization, capabilities that enable market expansion into adjacencies. Risk is high; impact is transformational.

<Pitfall title="Technology Is Not the Problem — Culture Is">

The #1 reason digital transformation programs fail is not the technology. It is organizational resistance to changing how decisions are made. When data says one thing and the regional sales director's gut says another, and the regional sales director wins every time — the transformation is not happening, regardless of how sophisticated the analytics platform is.

Transformation requires leadership commitment to actually using data to override intuition when they conflict. Without that commitment, companies end up with expensive systems that no one changes behavior for.

</Pitfall>

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting

### The Scenario

A newly appointed Chief Digital Officer must present a three-year transformation roadmap to the executive committee of a traditional retail company.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Digital Transformation Roadmap">

```
I am the Chief Digital Officer of a 50-year-old retail clothing chain
with 300 physical stores. Our e-commerce is only 12% of revenue. Our
inventory system runs on 15-year-old software. Our customer data is
stored in six different systems that do not talk to each other. Write
a three-year digital transformation roadmap organized into three
phases: Quick Wins (Year 1), Foundation Building (Year 2), and
Competitive Differentiation (Year 3). For each phase, list three
specific initiatives, the primary business outcome, and the biggest
implementation risk. Keep the language at executive level — no
technical jargon, pure business impact.
```

**After reviewing, address board skepticism:**

```
The board is skeptical. Several members say: "We've invested in
technology before and it never delivered the promised ROI. Why should
this roadmap be different?" Write a two-paragraph response that
acknowledges past failures honestly and explains what is structurally
different about this approach.
```

**The teaching moment:** The follow-up prompt is as important as the first. Anticipating and pre-answering the objection is what separates a good presentation from a great one. Claude helps you stress-test your argument before walking into the room.

</CodeLab>

## 50–80 min | Team Portal Deployment

### Build Module 10: Digital Transformation Roadmap

**Module 10 must contain:**

1. **Digital Maturity Assessment** — rate your company's current digital maturity (Level 1–5) with a three-sentence justification. Cite specific evidence: what systems exist, what is manual, where is data fragmented? Ratings without evidence will be challenged.

2. **Three-Phase Roadmap** — Quick Wins / Foundation / Differentiation. Two to three specific initiatives per phase, each described in one action-oriented sentence. ("Migrate inventory management to a cloud-based ERP" is a specific initiative. "Improve our technology" is not.)

3. **Priority Technology Bet** — one specific technology your company should invest in over the next three years (AI, IoT, cloud ERP, AR/VR, blockchain, etc.). A paragraph explaining the business case — not what the technology is, but what specific customer value or operational advantage it creates.

4. **Change Management Warning** — the one human or organizational barrier most likely to derail your transformation, and what leadership must do specifically to address it. Generic warnings ("resistance to change") are not acceptable — be specific to your company and industry.

## 80–90 min | Live Push & Review

Two portals displayed. Key question: Is the Digital Maturity Assessment honest?

Teams that rate themselves a Level 4 or 5 without specific evidence are not analyzing — they are rationalizing. The most credible assessments are the ones that honestly identify gaps. A Level 2 or 3 assessment with a clear roadmap is far stronger than an inflated Level 4 with no justification.

<Quiz question="Blockbuster famously had the opportunity to acquire Netflix for $50M in 2000 and declined. What does this decision reveal about the challenge of digital transformation for established businesses?">

<div>

**The innovator's dilemma in practice.**

Blockbuster's entire revenue model — late fees, physical inventory, store footprint — depended on the behavior that Netflix was about to eliminate. Acquiring Netflix would have meant cannibalizing Blockbuster's own business before anyone else forced them to.

This is the central paradox of digital transformation for established companies: the digital opportunity is often clearest to the incumbents, but acting on it requires destroying the revenue streams that fund the transformation itself. Blockbuster's leadership could see the threat. The question was whether they could convince shareholders to accept years of declining traditional revenue in exchange for an uncertain digital future.

They could not — or chose not to. Netflix built the future independently. The lesson for every incumbent: the question is not whether disruption is coming. It is whether you will be the one who disrupts your own business, or whether you will let a startup do it for you.
