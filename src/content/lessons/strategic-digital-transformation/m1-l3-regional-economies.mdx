## 00–30 min | Core Business Theory

### Cities Are Businesses

They compete for tourists, talent, and investment with the same tools companies use: branding, value propositions, and strategic positioning. The discipline is called **Place Branding**, and the strategic question is identical to what we asked in Session 1:

> *"Why would a rational person choose this place over every alternative?"*

The difference between a city that *manages decline* and one that *engineers a turnaround* is often a single strategic bet — on an anchor institution, an infrastructure investment, or a radical identity repositioning.

### Three Civic Turnarounds

**Bilbao, Spain — The Guggenheim Effect**
In the 1980s, Bilbao was a decaying industrial port city with 25% unemployment. The Basque regional government made a $100M bet on Frank Gehry's titanium museum. Year 1 results: 1 million visitors (the city's population was 350,000). Hotel tax revenues covered construction costs within three years. The city repositioned from industrial relic to European cultural capital.

The lesson: **one anchor institution can rewrite a region's identity.** The Guggenheim did not attract tourists because it was a great museum — it attracted tourists because it was an *impossible* thing to see in that place.

**Medellín, Colombia — Infrastructure as Equity**
Transformed from the world's most dangerous city (1990s) into a global model for urban innovation. The strategy: cable cars and escalators connecting hillside slums to the city center, paired with libraries and parks in the highest-violence neighborhoods. Result: homicide rate fell 95%. Medellín now attracts urban design conferences and policy tourists from around the world.

The lesson: **economic development and social equity can be the same investment.** The visitors are not coming despite the history — they are coming because of what was done with it.

**Detroit, USA — The Cautionary Counterpoint**
Decades of single-industry dependency (automotive), no economic diversification, and civic leadership that managed decline rather than enabling reinvention. Contrast with Pittsburgh, which lost steel and rebuilt around medicine, education, and robotics — a deliberate multi-decade diversification strategy.

The lesson: **cities with one dominant industry are one industry cycle away from crisis.** Diversification is not just good business strategy — it is urban survival strategy.

### Your Analytical Assignment for the Field Trip

This weekend you are not tourists. You are analysts. Each team is assigned one lens to apply during the trip:

- **Team A:** What is the region's value proposition to visitors? Is it communicated clearly in public spaces?
- **Team B:** What industry anchors does the local economy depend on? What evidence do you see of economic health or stress?
- **Team C:** What does public infrastructure tell you about investment priorities? Who is being served — and who is not?
- **Team D:** Who is being marketed to, and who is being excluded from the region's public brand?

Come back with specific observations. *"The downtown felt economically healthy"* is not an observation. *"12 of 20 storefronts on Main Street were occupied; 4 new businesses had opening-soon signs"* is an observation.

<Pitfall title="Tourism Is Not Economic Development">

A city that fills hotel rooms in July and struggles in November has a seasonality problem, not an economic development solution. Sustainable place branding attracts *resident talent* and *year-round business investment* — not just peak-season visitors. When you observe the field trip destination, distinguish between tourist-facing activities and evidence of year-round economic life.

</Pitfall>

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting

### The Scenario

A regional tourism board director must present a three-year revitalization strategy to the city council Monday morning.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Regional Revitalization Strategy">

```
I am the director of a regional tourism board for a mid-size industrial
city that built its economy around manufacturing. The factory that
employed 40% of workers closed five years ago. We have a historic
downtown, a river waterfront, two universities, and a minor league
baseball stadium. Write a three-year tourism and economic development
strategy with three strategic pillars. For each pillar, name a target
visitor segment, one flagship initiative, and one measurable success
metric. Keep the language clear enough to present to a city council
with no business background.
```

**After reviewing the output, run this refinement:**

```
Pillar 2 feels generic. Make it more specific to cities competing with
larger urban centers for weekend visitors. Add one competitive
differentiation point that a city with these exact assets could own
that a big city could not.
```

**The teaching moment:** Claude gave you a complete strategic framework in 90 seconds. A consulting firm would charge $80,000 for this document. Your value as a manager is knowing whether the strategy is actually *good* — and knowing how to refine it until it is.

</CodeLab>

### Build Module 3: Regional Market Opportunity (Pre-Trip)

**Module 3 must contain:**

1. **Field Trip Destination Overview** — a brief economic profile of the region you are about to visit: industry history, population, major employers, and any known economic transitions in the past 10 years
2. **Current Value Proposition** — how the region markets itself to visitors and businesses today (research the official tourism website and chamber of commerce)
3. **Three Analytical Questions** — specific, testable questions your team will answer through direct observation during the trip (not questions you can Google from your laptop)
4. **First Impression Hypothesis** — one paragraph predicting what you expect to find, which you will test and either confirm or revise in Session 4's debrief

**Good analytical question test:** *"What businesses are open vs. closed on Main Street during a weekday afternoon?"* passes. *"What is the economic health of the downtown?"* fails — it is too vague to answer through direct observation.

<Quiz question="A region's tourism board slogan is 'Where History Comes Alive.' What is the strategic weakness of this value proposition, and how would you improve it?">

<div>

**The weakness: every historic region can say the same thing.**

"Where History Comes Alive" is a category description, not a differentiator. Williamsburg, Edinburgh, Kyoto, Cusco, and hundreds of other destinations could use this exact slogan.

A stronger approach identifies *what is unique about this region's history* and *who specifically cares about it.* Example: *"The only place in the state where you can stand in a working 19th-century iron mill and eat dinner in the building where the strike was settled."* Specific, experiential, impossible to claim elsewhere.

</div>
</Quiz>
