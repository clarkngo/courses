### From Analysis to Strategy

Individual analyses — market segmentation, supply chain mapping, competitive positioning, digital roadmaps — only produce value when integrated into a coherent strategic position. An executive who presents ten well-researched analyses without a unifying strategic argument has not done strategy work. They have done research work.

**Strategy is the synthesis.** It answers: given everything we know, what is our highest-leverage move, and why now?

### Blue Ocean Strategy

Kim and Mauborgne's 2005 framework offers an alternative to Porter's Five Forces logic. Rather than analyzing how to compete better in an existing market (a "red ocean" — crowded, bloody with competition), Blue Ocean Strategy asks: **can we create a new market where no competition exists?**

The strategic move: **eliminate** what the industry takes for granted, **reduce** what the industry over-delivers, **raise** what the industry under-delivers, and **create** something the industry has never offered.

**Cirque du Soleil** — Stopped competing with Ringling Bros by eliminating animals (reducing cost dramatically), adding theatrical production and original music (creating value for an adult audience), removing the ring structure (allowing more intimate sightlines), and raising ticket prices 10× above a traditional circus. They did not win the circus market — they created a new market that circus competitors could not enter without destroying their own model. Revenue: $0 in 1984; $1B by 2006.

**The Nintendo Wii** — While Sony and Microsoft competed on processing power, graphics, and hardcore gamer loyalty, Nintendo eliminated hardware performance competition entirely, raised physical interactivity (motion controls), and created a market of non-gamers — families, elderly people, rehabilitation patients — who had never owned a gaming console. The Wii outsold the PlayStation 3 and Xbox 360 combined in its peak years.

**Yellow Tail Wine** — An Australian brand that eliminated complex wine education requirements, reduced price and variety to two accessible options, raised sweetness and approachability, and created a casual occasion wine for beer-drinkers who felt intimidated by the wine aisle. Became the #1 imported wine in the United States within two years of launch.

### Writing for Executives: The One-Page Standard

Every great strategy fits on one page — not because it is simple, but because it has been refined until it is clear.

**The Executive Summary structure:**
1. **Who we are** — one sentence on company identity and positioning
2. **Who we serve** — one sentence on primary customer segment
3. **What we uniquely offer** — value proposition, stated specifically
4. **Why we win** — competitive advantage, not just competitive difference
5. **How we grow** — the strategic moves that expand the business
6. **What we risk** — the one honest threat we are managing

**Rules for executive writing:**
- Lead with the conclusion, not the process
- Every claim must be followed by specific evidence
- Active voice throughout — passive voice signals uncertainty
- Anticipate the two hardest objections and answer them before being asked

<Pitfall title="The Summary Should Be Written Last, Read First">

The executive summary appears at the top of every business plan and strategic document. It is written last — after all the analysis is complete — because it is a distillation, not an introduction. Many students write it first and then write supporting content to match. The result is a summary that does not actually summarize — it previews. A genuine executive summary can stand alone. A reader who reads only the summary should have everything they need to make a decision.

</Pitfall>

Fill in each bracketed section from your actual portal content. Run the prompt, then evaluate the output against this checklist:

Edit until all four are yes. This is your Module 12 centerpiece.

<Quiz question="A management consultant presents 40 slides of rigorous analysis to a CEO. The CEO says: 'I don't have time for this. Tell me in one sentence what you're recommending and why.' What does this reveal about the relationship between analysis and strategy?">

<div>

**Analysis is not strategy. Strategy is the judgment that emerges from analysis.**

The consultant who cannot reduce 40 slides to one sentence has not finished the work — they have completed the research and stopped before reaching the conclusion. A recommendation is only useful if it can be communicated clearly enough to be acted on.

The one-sentence test is not a presentation technique. It is a diagnostic: if you cannot state your recommendation in one clear sentence, you probably do not yet know what your recommendation is. The summary forces clarity. Clarity reveals gaps. Filling gaps requires more thinking, not more slides.

The practical skill: write your one-sentence recommendation first, before the analysis, as a hypothesis. Then check whether the analysis supports or contradicts it. Revise accordingly. Work backward from the conclusion, not forward from the data.
