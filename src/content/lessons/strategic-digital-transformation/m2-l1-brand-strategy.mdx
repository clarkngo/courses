### From Place Brands to Company Brands

The field trip showed how *places* build brands. Now we study how *companies* build brands — specifically through sports, events, and emotional loyalty.

**Brand Equity** is the premium customers pay because of how a name makes them feel, not what the product objectively delivers. It is the reason people pay $5 for a Starbucks coffee and $1.50 for a gas station coffee — the liquid is similar; the identity signal is not.

### Two Brand Architecture Models

**Master Brand / Endorsed Brand** — one dominant brand name with sub-brands beneath it. *Virgin* (Virgin Atlantic, Virgin Mobile, Virgin Hotels) and *Samsung* (phones, TVs, semiconductors, ships) use their master brand as a quality and trust signal across radically different products.

**House of Brands** — each product has its own brand identity; the parent company is invisible to consumers. *Procter & Gamble* owns Tide, Pampers, Gillette, and Oral-B — each a #1 brand in its category, each marketed to a different customer without P&G's name visible on a single package.

### Three Sports Brand Case Studies

**FC Barcelona — "Més que un club" (More Than a Club)**
Over 500 million global fans. Kit sponsorship by Nike worth €155M/year. The club does not sell football — it sells Catalan cultural identity, underdog resilience, and Total Football aesthetics. Fans in Indonesia and Brazil who have never visited Spain are emotionally invested. This is brand equity in its most powerful form: identity attachment that transcends the product.

**Nike — The Athlete AS the Brand**
Nike does not sponsor athletes as spokespersons. It structures agreements so the athlete's personal brand amplifies Nike's, and vice versa. The Air Jordan brand — launched in 1984 — still generates over $5 billion per year for Nike, decades after Jordan's retirement. The athlete is not promoting a product; the athlete *is* a product line.

**The NBA's China Playbook**
The NBA became China's most-watched sports league through patient, decades-long brand building — Yao Ming as a cultural ambassador, not just a player. In 2019, a single tweet by a team executive about Hong Kong cost the league an estimated $400 million in Chinese revenue within weeks. The lesson: **brand equity is both asset and liability.** The same emotional attachment that creates loyalty creates vulnerability when brand values clash with market context.

<Pitfall title="Logos Are Not Brands">

A logo is a visual symbol. A brand is the sum of every experience a customer has ever had with your company — the product, the customer service call, the packaging, the social media post, the store environment, the tweet someone else wrote about you. Freshmen often say "we need to improve our brand" when they mean "we need a better logo." These are different problems requiring different solutions.

</Pitfall>

## 30–50 min | Executive AI Prompting

### The Scenario

A brand manager at a global athletic footwear company needs to pitch a regional sports sponsorship to the executive team by tomorrow morning.

<CodeLab title="Live Prompt: Sponsorship Proposal">

```
I am a brand manager at an athletic footwear company entering the
Southeast Asian market. I need to pitch sponsoring a professional
soccer club in the region to our executive team. Write a one-page
sponsorship proposal that includes: the strategic rationale (why
soccer, why this region), the brand alignment argument, three specific
activation ideas (not just logo placement — actual fan engagement
initiatives), projected audience reach, and one key risk with our
mitigation plan. Tone: confident, data-aware, executive-level.
```

**After reviewing the output, run this refinement:**

```
The brand alignment section is too generic. Rewrite it specifically
for a footwear brand whose core customer is 16-to-28-year-old urban
males in Indonesia, Vietnam, and the Philippines — and who aspires to
streetwear culture, not athletic performance.
```

**The teaching moment:** Adding audience specificity ("streetwear culture, not athletic performance") changes the entire strategic argument. Generic prompts produce generic strategy. The more specific your constraint, the more useful the output.

</CodeLab>

<Quiz question="Nike's brand archetype is widely identified as 'The Hero.' How does this archetype shape product marketing, athlete partnerships, and even customer service decisions?">

<div>

**The Hero archetype drives every customer touchpoint.**

In product marketing: campaigns consistently show ordinary people overcoming extraordinary challenges ("Just Do It" is a hero's call to action). Athlete partnerships: Nike signs athletes who embody competitive resilience and comeback narratives — not just winners, but dramatic competitors. Customer service: Nike's return policy is generous because a hero brand cannot be seen as petty.

The archetype is not a marketing filter. It is an operating principle. When a brand team asks "should we do this?", the archetype is the answer: *"Does this make our customer feel like a hero?"*
