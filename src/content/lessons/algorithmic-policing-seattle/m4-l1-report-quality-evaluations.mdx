## Independent Research, Not Vendor Data

Everything in this lesson comes from independent evaluation, not vendor-supplied performance claims — a distinction this course has flagged before (see [Operational Advantages](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m3-l1-operational-advantages) and [Tactile AI](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m2-l3-tactile-ai)) and one that matters most of all when the technology in question generates the actual language of an incident narrative. The American Civil Liberties Union's review of studies on AI-assisted police report tools surfaces three specific, measured findings that directly contradict the standard vendor pitch for these products.

If your department has a sister policy course on this exact subject — Seattle's own **Operational AI Compliance & Supervisory Accountability** course — this lesson is the evidence base that course's KCPAO-directive discussion draws on. The two are meant to be read together: that course teaches the compliance line ("AI may check, never author"); this lesson explains *why* the underlying research supports drawing that line where it's drawn.

---

## Three Findings

**Reduced factual accuracy.** AI-drafted narratives, evaluated against source material (body-worn camera audio/video and officer testimony), showed a measurable drop in factual accuracy compared to fully human-authored reports — the generative step introduces errors, omissions, or invented details that a purely human draft, working directly from the same source material, did not produce at the same rate.

**Increased syllable count and grade-level complexity.** Counterintuitively, AI-assisted reports were not simpler or more readable — independent linguistic analysis found *increased* syllable complexity and grade-reading-level scores compared to officer-authored baselines. A report that is harder to read is harder for a supervisor, a prosecutor, or a defense attorney to accurately parse — working against the transparency an incident report is supposed to provide.

**Zero statistically significant time savings.** The headline vendor claim for these tools is almost always speed — reports drafted in under a minute, dramatic reductions in time-on-task. Rigorous, controlled measurement (a pre-registered randomized design, evaluating end-to-end time including the human review and correction step, not just initial draft generation) found **no statistically significant time savings** at all once the full workflow was measured.

---

## Why the Time-Savings Finding Is the Most Important One

Of the three findings, the time-savings result deserves the most attention from leadership specifically because it's the one procurement decisions are usually made on. A tool that's slightly less accurate but dramatically faster might still be a defensible trade-off for some use cases. A tool that is **both less accurate and not actually faster once the full workflow is measured** has no remaining efficiency argument to justify the accuracy cost.

This is also a direct, concrete instance of the pattern named in [Operational Advantages](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m3-l1-operational-advantages): a headline efficiency number (draft generation in under a minute) measures a narrow process step, while the outcome that actually matters to a department (total time from incident to a reviewed, accurate, signed report) is a different — and in this case, unmoved — number.

<Pitfall title="A Fast Draft Is Not a Fast Report">

If the review and correction step is where accuracy problems get caught — and it has to be, given the reduced-accuracy finding above — then a fast initial draft that requires more careful, more time-consuming review doesn't actually save net time. It may even cost time relative to a human-authored draft that a reviewer trusts more by default. Any efficiency argument for an AI drafting tool needs to account for the full pipeline, not the generation step in isolation — exactly what the controlled research measured, and exactly what found no net savings.

</Pitfall>
