## Who This Course Is For

This course is written for **police department leadership, policy staff, and oversight personnel** who sit on the approving side of algorithmic policing decisions — the people who have to answer, in public, why a tool was acquired, how it was reviewed, what it does operationally, whether it works, and who watches it once it's live.

That is a different job than using the tools day-to-day. This course does not teach officers how to operate a specific product. It teaches command staff and policy leads how to evaluate, justify, and govern the whole category — using Seattle as the working example, because Seattle has more publicly documented history on this than almost any other city in the country.

---

## Why Seattle Is the Case Study

Three things collided in Seattle over the last decade, and the collision is why this city's paper trail is so useful to study:

1. **An unusually aggressive local ordinance regime.** Seattle passed one of the first municipal surveillance-technology ordinances in the nation (2013), then rewrote it into a much stronger disclosure-and-approval regime (SMC 14.18, 2017) — years before most cities had any equivalent rule.
2. **A federal consent decree that shaped department culture for over a decade.** Seattle Police operated under direct federal oversight for the use of force and bias-free policing, which means every new technology decision was made inside an environment already primed for public scrutiny.
3. **Rapid, real deployment of AI tools regardless of the above.** Real-time crime centers, bias-crime NLP classifiers, AI-assisted report review, and predictive/forecasting models have all been piloted or deployed in Seattle — sometimes ahead of the policy written to govern them, which is itself an important lesson.

That combination — strong ordinance, exiting federal oversight, live AI deployment — is what makes Seattle the sharpest available teaching case for how algorithmic policing governance actually plays out, warts and all.

## How This Course Is Organized

The course follows five modules, each built around one governing question:

| Module | Question | What You'll Be Able to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Regulatory Framework** | What law and policy actually govern this? | Trace Seattle's ordinance history and explain what a Surveillance Impact Report requires |
| **2. Technical Implementations** | What is actually deployed, and how does it work? | Distinguish operational, accountability, tactile, and integrated AI systems by function |
| **3. Impact Analysis** | Does it work, and at what cost? | Weigh documented operational gains against systemic drawbacks, using ShotSpotter as the central case |
| **4. Criminological Research** | What does the independent evidence say? | Cite specific findings on AI report quality and predictive forecasting models |
| **5. Oversight and Reform** | Who watches it now, and what should change? | Explain the post-consent-decree oversight structure and evaluate concrete policy recommendations |

Each module builds on the last. Module 1 gives you the legal vocabulary and process everything else refers back to; by Module 5 you'll be applying it to make an actual recommendation.

## Course Objectives

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

1. **Explain Seattle's surveillance ordinance regime** — Ordinance 124142 (2013), SMC 14.18 (2017), and the 2025 AI policy layered on top — and what triggers council review.
2. **Name and categorize the AI systems currently in use or piloted** by Seattle PD, sorted by what job they actually do (operational, accountability, tactile, integrated).
3. **State the documented operational advantages and systemic drawbacks** of algorithmic policing tools, with specific figures rather than vendor talking points.
4. **Summarize the independent research** on AI-assisted report quality and predictive forecasting accuracy, including where it contradicts vendor claims.
5. **Describe the current oversight structure** — post-consent-decree — and evaluate the strategic recommendations on the table for closing remaining gaps.

<Pitfall title="This Is Not a Product Endorsement or Takedown Course">

It would be easy to teach this material as either "AI is transforming policing for the better" or "AI in policing is a civil-liberties disaster." Neither framing survives contact with Seattle's actual record. Some tools show real operational value with contained risk (bias crime NLP classification, VR de-escalation training). Others show minimal or negative value despite heavy investment (ShotSpotter, some report-writing tools). This course teaches you to tell the difference on a tool-by-tool basis — that discrimination is the actual skill leadership needs, not a category-wide verdict.

</Pitfall>
