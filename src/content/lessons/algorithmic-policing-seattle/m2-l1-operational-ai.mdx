## What "Operational AI" Means in This Course

Module 2 sorts Seattle's AI deployments into four functional categories, not by vendor or product name. **Operational AI** is the category of tools that sit directly inside a case's day-to-day workflow — reading, classifying, or flagging content as part of how a case gets handled, not auditing it after the fact (that's Module 2's next category, Accountability AI) and not training officers (Tactile AI, Lesson 3).

The flagship example in Seattle is the **Bias Crime Identifier**.

---

## The Bias Crime Identifier

Bias crimes — offenses motivated by the victim's protected characteristic — are historically undercounted because identifying bias motivation requires reading and correctly interpreting free-text narrative, not just checking a structured offense code. Seattle's approach pairs its Real-Time Crime Center work with an **NLP (natural language processing) system trained to extract and classify potential bias-crime indicators from incident narratives** — flagging cases where language, context, or symbols in the reported narrative suggest a bias motivation that a structured code alone would miss.

This matters operationally in a specific way: it doesn't replace the detective's judgment about whether a crime is bias-motivated. It **surfaces candidates for review** that might otherwise be missed in the volume of daily incident narratives — a triage function, not a determination function.

---

## How the NLP Layer Actually Works

At a functional level, the classifier does two things:

1. **Extraction** — pulling structured signals (slurs, symbols, references to protected characteristics, patterns of targeting) out of unstructured narrative text.
2. **Classification** — scoring or flagging the extracted content against known bias-crime indicator patterns, then routing flagged cases to a human reviewer.

Neither step finalizes a bias-crime designation. That's a deliberate design choice, not an accident — and it's the most important thing to understand about this category of tool.

---

## Human-in-the-Loop Verification Is the Load-Bearing Design Choice

The reason the Bias Crime Identifier is a defensible use of AI — where other tools in this course (see [ShotSpotter Debate](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m3-l3-shotspotter-debate) and [Report Quality Evaluations](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m4-l1-report-quality-evaluations)) run into serious trouble — is that its output is **advisory, not dispositive**. A flagged narrative goes to a human reviewer who makes the actual determination. The AI's job is recall (don't miss candidates), not precision (don't be the final word).

This is the same distinction the sibling course on report-writing compliance teaches as "AI-as-Auditor, not AI-as-Author": the system checks and surfaces, a human decides and signs. Operational AI tools that keep this boundary intact are the tools that survive scrutiny. Tools that quietly let AI output become the final answer — without a human genuinely re-examining the underlying evidence — are the tools that generate the accountability failures covered later in this course.

<Pitfall title="Human-in-the-Loop Only Works If the Human Actually Looks">

A human-in-the-loop step is not automatically a safeguard. If the reviewing officer treats a low AI confidence score as "probably nothing" and moves on without reading the underlying narrative themselves, the human step becomes a rubber stamp — the exact automation-complacency failure mode documented in AI-assisted report review research (see [Report Quality Evaluations](/algorithmic-policing-seattle/lessons/m4-l1-report-quality-evaluations)). A human-in-the-loop design is only as strong as the review discipline actually applied at that checkpoint — that's a training and supervision commitment, not something the software guarantees on its own.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="A vendor proposes upgrading the Bias Crime Identifier so that narratives scoring above a high confidence threshold are automatically coded as bias crimes in the record system, skipping human review to 'save detective time on the obvious cases.' Should command staff approve this change?">

<div>

**No — this removes the human-in-the-loop verification step that is the actual basis for treating this tool as an operational aid rather than a determination-making system, and it should not be approved without a separate, full review process of its own.**

Confidence scores are a measure of pattern-match strength, not ground truth — a narrative can score high on language-pattern indicators while still requiring human context (relationship history, prior incidents, credible alternative explanations) to correctly determine bias motivation. Automating the "obvious" cases quietly converts an advisory tool into a dispositive one, which is precisely the shift that would require this change to go through its own Surveillance Impact Report and civil liberties review under the framework from Module 1 — not a quiet configuration change.

</div>

</Quiz>
