## Reading the Timeline as a Sequence, Not a Single Law

It's tempting to treat "Seattle's surveillance ordinance" as one fixed rule. It isn't. It's three distinct legislative acts, each one written to close a specific gap the previous one left open. Understanding the sequence — not just the current end state — matters because it tells you *how* this kind of policy actually gets built: reactively, incrementally, and usually after a specific controversy exposed the gap.

| Year | Instrument | What It Actually Did |
|---|---|---|
| **2013** | Ordinance 124142 | First requirement that city departments disclose surveillance technology acquisitions and get council authorization |
| **2017** | SMC 14.18 | Replaced the 2013 framework with a much more detailed regime: mandatory Surveillance Impact Reports, privacy and civil liberties assessments, and a formal community review step |
| **2025** | POL-211 AI Policy | Extended the surveillance-technology governance model to AI systems specifically, including generative AI, addressing gaps the surveillance ordinance didn't anticipate |

---

## 2013: Ordinance 124142 — Disclosure as the First Fix

Seattle's original surveillance ordinance was a direct response to city departments acquiring surveillance capability — mesh network infrastructure, drones — with functionally no public disclosure or council vote before the fact. The 2013 ordinance's core fix was procedural: **before a department can acquire surveillance technology, the City Council has to know about it and approve it.**

That was a meaningful first step, but it was thin. It set a disclosure gate without specifying what information had to go into that disclosure, without a standard privacy-impact methodology, and without an ongoing review mechanism once a technology was already in use.

---

## 2017: SMC 14.18 — From Disclosure to Structured Review

SMC 14.18 rewrote the regime from "tell the council" into a structured, repeatable evaluation process. This is the ordinance still doing the operational work in Seattle today, and its core innovation is the **Surveillance Impact Report (SIR)** — covered in full in the next lesson — a standardized document every surveillance technology has to produce before deployment, covering data collection, retention, sharing, and equity impact.

The practical shift from 2013 to 2017: a technology no longer just needs a *disclosed* acquisition — it needs a *documented case* that survives a defined review pathway involving privacy staff, civil liberties assessment, and a community body before Council will approve it.

---

## 2025: POL-211 — AI as Its Own Category

By the 2020s, the SMC 14.18 surveillance framework was doing real work, but it was written around a specific mental model: *sensors and data collection systems* — cameras, license plate readers, cell-site simulators. Generative and predictive AI don't fit that model cleanly. A large language model summarizing a report, or a classifier flagging a bias crime, isn't "collecting" data the way a camera is — but it raises its own distinct risks: hallucination, bias amplification, and accountability gaps when a model's reasoning can't be fully audited.

The 2025 AI Policy is Seattle's attempt to close that specific gap — governing the AI layer directly, rather than trying to stretch the surveillance-technology definition to cover it after the fact.

<Pitfall title="Don't Assume the Newest Rule Replaced the Old Ones">

POL-211 did not repeal SMC 14.18, and SMC 14.18 did not repeal the 2013 ordinance's underlying disclosure principle. These layer on top of each other. A tool that is both a data-collection surveillance system *and* an AI-driven analysis layer — a real-time crime center integrating camera feeds with an AI classifier, for example — may need to satisfy obligations under more than one instrument at once. Treating this as "the 2025 policy is the current rule" and ignoring the earlier layers is a common and costly misreading.

</Pitfall>

<Quiz question="A department is evaluating a new AI classifier that runs on top of an already-approved camera network. Is that correct?">

<div>

**No — the AI layer is a distinct governed instrument under the 2025 policy and generally requires its own review, even if the underlying data source was already approved.**

The legislative history matters here: POL-211 exists specifically because SMC 14.18's Surveillance Impact Report framework was built around data-collection systems, not the analytical/generative layer that can now sit on top of them. An AI classifier introduces its own distinct risks — bias amplification, hallucination, auditability — that a camera-network SIR was never designed to evaluate. Treating prior surveillance-technology approval as sufficient for a new AI layer skips exactly the gap the 2025 policy was written to close.
