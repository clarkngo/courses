## Who This Course Is For

This course is written for people already doing two jobs, not one:

- **CityU instructors who are also industry practitioners** — you're teaching a course while still doing the work you teach, and your LinkedIn says both things
- **Adjunct and affiliate faculty** who've never posted publicly about what they teach, and aren't sure it's allowed, appropriate, or worth the time
- **Full-time faculty with deep industry backgrounds** who get asked to speak, guest-lecture elsewhere, or consult, but have never turned that into a standing public presence
- **Anyone who has caught themselves explaining something well in office hours** and thought, briefly, "that would make a good video" — before getting back to grading

You are not starting from zero. You already do the hardest part of content creation — translating real expertise into something a non-expert can follow — every time you teach. This course is about taking that skill public, deliberately, on a schedule that fits around a full-time role.

## Why This Is Being Asked of You Now

For most of academic history, a faculty member's public reputation and their teaching were two separate accounts: one built through publications, conferences, and word of mouth; the other visible only to the students enrolled that term. That separation is breaking down, in both directions.

**Ernest Boyer's *Scholarship Reconsidered* (1990, Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching)** argued for a broader definition of scholarship than "discovery" (traditional research) alone — including the **scholarship of application** (using expertise to address real problems) and the **scholarship of teaching** (transmitting, transforming, and extending knowledge in ways that can themselves be scrutinized and built on by others). Boyer's point wasn't that public-facing work is *lesser* scholarship — it's that institutions had been undercounting forms of scholarly contribution that don't take the shape of a peer-reviewed paper. A well-made public lecture, a widely-used teaching repository, or a clearly explained industry case study fits Boyer's application and teaching categories directly.

At the same time, **danah boyd and Alice Marwick's research on "micro-celebrity" and networked publics (Marwick, A. E. (2013). *Status Update: Celebrity, Publicity, and Branding in the Social Media Age*. Yale University Press)** documents a real shift: professional credibility increasingly gets built and verified in public, ongoing, cumulative view — not solely through credentials disclosed once at hiring. For an institution like CityU, whose instructors are hired specifically *because* they're active industry practitioners, that shift is an asset waiting to be used, not a threat to be managed.

<Pitfall title={`"Isn't This Just Asking Faculty to Do Marketing for Free?"`}>

It's a fair worry, and this course does not wave it away — Module 6 deals directly with time budgets and what's reasonable to expect alongside a full teaching load. But there's a distinction worth holding onto from the start: **this course is not about promoting your course sections.** It's about doing the scholarship of application and teaching (Boyer, 1990) in a public register, which has a payoff to *you* — professional reputation, speaking and consulting opportunities, a durable body of work outside any one institution — independent of whatever secondary benefit it has for enrollment. Module 5 covers the institutional side honestly, including where the evidence for a content-to-enrollment link is solid and where it's just correlation.

## Course Objectives

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

1. **Choose a public format** (video, writing, audio, or a hybrid) that matches the kind of expertise you're actually trying to transmit — not the format that happens to be trendy.
2. **Turn material you already teach** — a lecture, a case study, a recurring office-hours question — into a standalone public artifact, without building new content from scratch.
3. **Hold three identities at once** — practitioner, instructor, public creator — without letting any one of them distort what you say in the other two.
4. **Know where your institutional and contractual boundaries actually are** — IP, confidentiality, academic freedom — well enough to ask your specific institution the right question instead of guessing.
5. **Build a realistic, time-bounded content habit** you can sustain for a full academic year without it competing with your teaching load.
6. **Produce a stress-tested 90-day content plan** as your capstone, with the most common failure modes already designed around.
