## The Constraint Every Other Module Deferred

Every module so far has assumed you have some protected time for this. This lesson is about being honest with yourself about how much, and why the failure mode isn't usually lack of ideas — it's an unexamined collision between three roles that all compete for the same finite hours.

---

## Role Conflict and Overload

**Kahn, R. L., Wolfe, D. M., Quinn, R. P., Snoek, J. D., & Rosenthal, R. A. (1964). *Organizational Stress: Studies in Role Conflict and Ambiguity*. Wiley.**

Kahn and colleagues' foundational organizational psychology research distinguished between **role conflict** (competing, incompatible demands from different roles a person holds simultaneously) and **role overload** (the simple, cumulative demand of too many role obligations exceeding the time and energy available to meet them well). Their research established that overload isn't solved by working harder within the existing structure — it's a structural mismatch between total demands and total available capacity, and it predicts real costs: declining performance across roles, and psychological strain, when the mismatch isn't addressed directly.

Applied here: practitioner, instructor, and creator are three distinct roles, each with its own real obligations — client or employer deliverables, course prep and grading, and a content cadence. Kahn et al.'s framework predicts, correctly, that simply adding "creator" as a fourth demand on top of an already full practitioner-instructor schedule, without reducing anything or explicitly bounding the new role, produces overload — not a thriving new habit.

<Pitfall title={`"I'll Just Find the Time Somewhere"`}>

This is the plan Kahn et al.'s research predicts will fail, and predicts *why*: "finding time somewhere" treats time as elastic, when the actual problem is a fixed supply competing against a rising number of role demands. The evidence-aligned alternative isn't more discipline — it's treating the content-creator role the way this course has structured it from Module 2 onward: a hard cadence cap, a hard per-piece time ceiling, and an explicit acknowledgment of what does *not* get more time as a result (a slightly shorter office-hours block, content pulled directly from existing lecture material rather than new research, and so on).

</Pitfall>

## An Honest Time Budget

Rather than an aspirational number, build a time budget from what you've already committed to in this course:

| Commitment | Where it was set | Weekly/biweekly cost |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed cue and cadence | Module 2, Lesson 2 | Your stated cadence (e.g., biweekly) |
| Per-piece time ceiling | Module 2, Lesson 2 | Your stated cap (e.g., 45 minutes) |
| Repurposing existing material | Module 3 | Near-zero *new* creation time — the content already exists |
| Return-stage tracking | Module 5, Lesson 2 | A few minutes per contact, logged as it happens |

Add these up honestly. If the total exceeds what Kahn et al.'s framework would predict you can sustain without overload symptoms (declining energy for teaching itself, resentment toward the habit, missed cues becoming permanent lapses), the fix is not more willpower — it's reducing cadence or scope, not abandoning the habit outright.

<CodeLab title="Stress-Test Your Own Time Budget">

Answer honestly, in writing:

1. Add up your Module 2 cadence and time-ceiling commitments as actual hours per month.
2. Compare that number to the number you wrote down in Module 1's inventory exercise for "time I can actually protect per week."
3. If the content commitment exceeds the protected time, cut cadence (not per-piece quality) until it fits — per Kahn et al., reducing total role demand is the intervention that works, not compressing each task further.
4. Write down one specific thing you will *not* do less of to make room (teaching prep, grading turnaround, family time) — naming the protected boundary makes the trade-off explicit instead of accidental.

</CodeLab>

<div>

**The problem is role overload — total demands across the practitioner, instructor, and creator roles now exceed available capacity — not a personal failure of time management, and the fix is reducing the content role's demand (lower the cadence, e.g., to biweekly) rather than trying to compress teaching duties further to make room.** Kahn et al.'s framework specifically warns against treating overload as solvable through more effort within an unchanged structure; the instructor's declining performance in teaching (rushed grading, skipped office hours) is exactly the kind of cross-role cost their research predicts when one role's demands quietly cannibalize another's without being explicitly renegotiated. The sustainable fix protects the roles that came first — teaching obligations — by scaling back the newer role's cadence.
