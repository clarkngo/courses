## The Discomfort Is Not a Sign of Failure

Instructors who start publishing publicly almost universally report the same discomfort: which version of themselves is speaking? The practitioner who has to be precise and defensible in front of industry peers? The instructor who has to simplify for students who don't yet have the background? Or this new, unfamiliar "creator" voice that feels performative and slightly embarrassing the first dozen times? This lesson uses one piece of research to explain why that discomfort is expected, temporary, and productive — not a signal you're doing this wrong.

---

## Provisional Selves

**Ibarra, H. (1999). "Provisional selves: Experimenting with image and identity in professional adaptation." *Administrative Science Quarterly*, 44(4), 764–791.**

Ibarra studied professionals (in her original research, associates transitioning into new roles at investment banks and consulting firms) navigating a gap between who they currently were professionally and who their new role required them to become. Her central finding: successful adaptation didn't happen by suddenly having a fully-formed new professional identity. It happened through **provisional selves** — deliberately trying on incomplete, imperfect versions of the new identity, observing how they landed, and adjusting, in repeated cycles, before anything stabilized.

Three of Ibarra's specific findings map directly onto starting as a public creator:

1. **People draw on multiple role models selectively** — copying different admired traits from different people, rather than fully imitating one — instead of inventing a voice from nothing. You don't need an original "creator persona"; borrowing what works from creators you admire and combining it with your existing teaching voice is the normal path, not a shortcut.
2. **Experimentation happens through action, not introspection.** Ibarra found people figured out their adapted identity by doing the new role's tasks and observing feedback, not by first reasoning their way to a finished self-concept and then performing it. Concretely: you find your creator voice by publishing something imperfect and noticing how it feels and lands, not by pre-deciding "my brand voice is X" before you've made anything.
3. **The transition is felt as uncomfortable precisely because it's incomplete by design** — provisional selves are, by definition, not-yet-settled, and Ibarra treats that discomfort as a normal signal of adaptation in progress, not a diagnostic that something has gone wrong.

<Pitfall title="Trying to Sound Like a Different Person Online">

The most common early mistake is manufacturing a "creator voice" that doesn't resemble how you actually talk — more energetic, more polished, more like other creators you've watched, and less like the person your students recognize from class. Ibarra's research suggests this backfires for a specific reason: provisional selves that work are usually **recognizable extensions** of an existing identity, refined through feedback, not wholesale replacements. Your practitioner judgment and your instructor's explanatory clarity are exactly what should carry over — the "creator" layer is a register change (more direct, more visual, shorter sentences), not a personality change.

</Pitfall>

## A Practical Frame for the Three Voices

Rather than treating practitioner, instructor, and creator as competing identities, treat them as three lenses you apply to the same underlying expertise, chosen based on the moment:

| Voice | When it leads | What it protects |
|---|---|---|
| **Practitioner** | Making a technical claim, discussing a real project or case | Accuracy and defensibility to industry peers |
| **Instructor** | Sequencing an explanation, choosing what to simplify and what not to | Clarity for someone new to the topic |
| **Creator** | Deciding the hook, pacing, and format of a specific piece | Whether anyone actually watches or reads to the end |

A single piece of content usually needs all three at different points — the creator voice decides how you open, the instructor voice decides how you sequence the explanation, and the practitioner voice keeps you from overclaiming or oversimplifying something that matters.

<CodeLab title="Draft, Publish Small, Observe">

Applying Ibarra's action-before-introspection finding directly:

1. Do not try to define your "voice" in the abstract before publishing anything.
2. Take the artifact from Module 3 and publish it in a genuinely imperfect first form.
3. After it's out, write down — specifically — what felt like "you" and what felt forced.
4. Adjust only those specific things for the next piece. Do not redesign your whole approach based on one data point.
5. Repeat for at least three pieces before drawing any conclusion about your "voice" — Ibarra's subjects needed repeated cycles, not one attempt, before anything stabilized.

</CodeLab>
