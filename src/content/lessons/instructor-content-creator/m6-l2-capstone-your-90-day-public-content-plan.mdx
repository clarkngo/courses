## The Assignment

Build a single-page, 90-day public content plan, using the decisions and evidence from every prior module. This is the deliverable this course has been building toward since Module 1's inventory exercise — not a new exercise, but the assembly of everything you've already decided.

## Red-Teaming the Plan

A plan that looks complete on paper still fails in practice for a small number of predictable reasons. Stress-test yours against each:

<Pitfall title="Failure Mode 1 — The Cue Quietly Disappears">

Your fixed cue (Module 2.2, grounded in Lally et al., 2010) was anchored to something like "after Thursday lecture prep." What happens the week that lecture is cancelled, or prep moves to Tuesday? If your plan doesn't name a backup cue, the habit has no anchor the first time your schedule shifts — which, across a 15-week semester, is close to guaranteed to happen at least once.

**Fix:** name one backup cue now, before it's needed.

</Pitfall>

<Pitfall title="Failure Mode 2 — The First Piece Doesn't Match the Channel">

Module 2.1's media richness and tacit/explicit analysis (Daft & Lengel, 1986; Polanyi, 1966) should have driven your channel choice. Check: does your actual first artifact (Module 3) match what you said your channel should carry? A tacit, demonstration-heavy idea forced into a short text post is a mismatch that will underperform regardless of how good the underlying expertise is.

**Fix:** re-run the Module 2.1 table against your actual first three artifacts, not just your original hypothetical ones.

</Pitfall>

<Pitfall title="Failure Mode 3 — A Boundary Question Was Assumed, Not Asked">

Module 4.2 was explicit: a specific written answer from your institution or employer outranks any general guidance, including this course's. If line 5 of your plan contains an assumption ("I'm sure this is fine") rather than a confirmed answer, that's an open risk, not a closed one.

**Fix:** identify the single highest-risk assumption in your content plan and get it confirmed in writing before your first public piece, not your tenth.

</Pitfall>

<Pitfall title="Failure Mode 4 — Success Gets Judged by the Wrong Metric">

Module 5.1's parasocial interaction research (Horton & Wohl, 1956) argued for tracking return engagement over raw view counts. If your plan's only success measure is views or reach, you'll likely abandon a habit that's actually working — or keep chasing a viral piece that, per that lesson, does less for your long-term reputation than it appears to.

**Fix:** confirm your plan's line 6 tracks *return* signal, not just reach.

</Pitfall>

<Pitfall title="Failure Mode 5 — The Plan Ignores Its Own Time Math">

Module 6.1's role-overload framing (Kahn et al., 1964) predicts that an unexamined cadence will eventually cannibalize teaching duties. If you haven't actually added up the hours in line 7 and compared them against your real protected time from Module 1, the plan is aspirational, not load-bearing.

**Fix:** do the arithmetic in Module 6.1's exercise now if you haven't already — don't let this be the first thing that breaks in week six.

</Pitfall>

## Finalize and Commit

<CodeLab title="Set Your Review Date">

Pick a date exactly 90 days out and put it on your actual calendar — not a vague intention, a specific dated event, per the same habit-formation logic from Module 2.2. On that date, review:

1. Did the cue hold, or did it need the backup?
2. Does your first-three-artifacts channel match still feel right, or did Module 2.1's analysis need revising?
3. Did you get the written boundary answers you needed?
4. What does your return-signal tracker actually show — even if it's a small or inconclusive sample?
5. Did the time budget hold, or did something else quietly lose time to it?

This review is itself evidence — your own, specific to your context — stronger than any single citation in this course for deciding what to adjust in the next 90 days.

</CodeLab>
