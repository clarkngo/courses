## The Wrong Metric to Chase

New public creators, instructors especially, often fixate on the one metric platforms make most visible: views, or reach, on a single piece of content. This lesson makes the case, from a 70-year-old piece of media research, that this is the wrong thing to optimize for if your actual goal is professional reputation and a durable relationship with an audience.

---

## Parasocial Interaction Theory

**Horton, D., & Wohl, R. R. (1956). "Mass communication and para-social interaction: Observations on intimacy at a distance." *Psychiatry*, 19(3), 215–229.**

Horton and Wohl coined the term **parasocial interaction** to describe the one-sided but psychologically real relationship audiences form with a recurring media figure — originally studied through radio and early television personalities. Their key observations, made decades before social media existed but consistently supported by later research on media audiences:

1. **The relationship is built through repeated exposure over time**, not through a single powerful appearance. Audiences come to feel they "know" a recurring figure — their mannerisms, their perspective, what to expect from them — in a way a single, even excellent, one-off performance cannot replicate.
2. **Trust and perceived intimacy accumulate gradually**, tracking the audience's cumulative history with the figure, not any single instance of contact.
3. **The relationship persists even though it's entirely one-directional** — the audience member feels a bond the media figure has no comparable awareness of — which is precisely why consistency of appearance, not intensity of any one appearance, is what sustains it.

Applied directly to a content-creating instructor: **a single viral piece of content produces a large, one-time audience with no accumulated parasocial relationship at all** — most viewers will never see anything else you make. A modest but consistent cadence, even with far smaller individual view counts, is what actually builds the repeated exposure Horton and Wohl identify as the mechanism behind audience trust.

<Pitfall title="Judging a Piece of Content by Its View Count Alone">

It's natural to treat a low-view piece as a failure and a viral piece as the model to replicate. Horton and Wohl's framework suggests the opposite read is often more accurate: a piece that gets moderate views but is watched by the *same* returning people you reached last time is doing the parasocial work that actually compounds. A one-off viral piece, disconnected from your regular audience and rarely followed by a return visit from those new viewers, may look more successful by the platform's metric while doing almost nothing for the standing, trusted reputation this course is trying to help you build.

</Pitfall>

## What to Track Instead

Given Horton and Wohl's emphasis on cumulative, repeated exposure, the more useful indicators of whether your content is building a real parasocial relationship are:

- **Return rate** — are the same names, handles, or commenters showing up across multiple pieces, not just once?
- **Depth of engagement, not volume** — a handful of substantive comments or replies from repeat viewers signals more than a large number of passive views.
- **Direct outreach over time** — invitations to speak, consult, or collaborate that reference more than one piece of your content ("I've been following your posts on X") are a strong signal of an accumulated parasocial relationship converting into a real professional connection.

None of these show up prominently on a platform's default analytics dashboard, which is itself worth noting — the metrics platforms surface most visibly (views, likes) are not the same as the metrics this research says actually matter for building trust and reputation.

<CodeLab title="Track Return Signal, Not Just Reach">

For your next three pieces of content:

1. Note total views/reads as reported by the platform, but don't treat it as the primary success metric.
2. Separately track: how many comments, replies, or messages came from someone who had engaged with a *previous* piece of yours — even a rough, non-scientific count.
3. After three pieces, compare which metric moved more consistently upward — total reach, or repeat engagement. Expect repeat engagement to grow more slowly but more reliably, per Horton and Wohl's emphasis on gradual accumulation.

</CodeLab>
