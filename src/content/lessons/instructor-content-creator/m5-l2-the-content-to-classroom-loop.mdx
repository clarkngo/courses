## Closing the Loop

Module 1 introduced a loop, not a one-way funnel: Classroom → Capture → Publish → Audience → Reputation → **Return**. This lesson covers the Return stage — what happens when consistent public content (Module 5.1) starts to feed back into your teaching, your professional opportunities, and plausibly, CityU's own visibility. It's also the lesson where this course is most careful about the difference between a well-supported mechanism and an unproven causal claim.

---

## A Real Mechanism: Mere Exposure

**Zajonc, R. B. (1968). "Attitudinal effects of mere exposure." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, Monograph Supplement*, 9(2, Pt. 2), 1–27.**

Zajonc's mere exposure effect is one of the most replicated findings in social psychology: **repeated, even passive, exposure to a stimulus — a face, a name, a phrase — reliably increases how favorably people rate it**, independent of any reasoned evaluation of its merits. It's a robust finding across decades of replication, and it supplies a real, well-evidenced mechanism for a plausible claim: a prospective student or industry contact who has repeatedly encountered your name and your explanations through public content is likely, all else equal, to view you — and by extension, the institution they know you teach for — more favorably than someone encountering you for the first time in an admissions brochure.

That's the honest limit of what Zajonc's research supports here. It's a mechanism for *familiarity producing favorability* — it is **not** a study of content marketing, enrollment funnels, or higher-education admissions specifically, and treating it as direct proof that "posting content increases enrollment" overstates what the citation actually shows.

<Pitfall title="Claiming Your Content 'Drives Enrollment' Without Your Own Data">

It is tempting — and common in less careful accounts of this model — to assert a direct causal line from public content to enrollment numbers. The honest position: Zajonc (1968) supports a plausible *mechanism* (familiarity increases favorability), and Boyer (1990, covered in Module 1) supports a legitimacy argument (this work counts as real scholarship) — but neither is a causal study of your specific content's effect on your specific institution's enrollment. Until you have your own tracked data connecting a specific piece of content to a specific inquiry or application, the correct claim is "plausibly contributes to," not "drives" or "causes."

</Pitfall>

## Building Your Own Evidence, Instead of Borrowing Someone Else's

Because the research stops short of proving a direct content-to-enrollment link for your specific case, the responsible move is to start generating your own traceable evidence, the same way Module 2's cohort-economics thinking in the sister course to this one treats platform statistics: useful as directional signal, clearly labeled, never blended with peer-reviewed citation as if it were the same kind of evidence.

Concrete, low-effort ways to start tracking your own Return-stage signal:

1. **Ask, every time.** When a new student, guest-speaking invitation, or industry contact appears, ask directly: "how did you find me?" It's the single highest-signal, lowest-cost data point available, and most instructors never collect it.
2. **Use a distinct link or contact path for public content** where your platform allows it — a dedicated bio link, a distinguishable email signature line, or a simple "mention this post" ask — so referrals from content are distinguishable from referrals through other channels.
3. **Track invitations that reference more than one piece of content** (the "return signal" from Module 5.1) separately from one-off inquiries — this is your best proxy for the compounding, parasocial-relationship effect Horton and Wohl's research (Module 5.1) predicts, versus a single lucky exposure.
4. **Report your own data honestly**, including when it's inconclusive or small-sample — a handful of real, traceable data points about your own audience is more useful to you than an untraceable industry statistic about content marketing in general.

<CodeLab title="Build Your Own Return-Stage Tracker">

Set up a simple running log — a spreadsheet is enough — with four columns:

1. Date and content piece (if known) that prompted contact
2. How the person said they found you (their own words, not your assumption)
3. What kind of contact resulted (speaking invite, student inquiry, industry connection, nothing actionable)
4. Whether this is a first-time contact or someone referencing more than one prior piece

Review it every semester. This becomes your own institution- and audience-specific evidence — stronger for your actual decisions than any external citation, including the ones in this lesson.

</CodeLab>

<div>

**Describe the plausible mechanism (repeated public exposure builds familiarity and reputation, per Zajonc's mere exposure research, and constitutes real scholarly application and teaching work, per Boyer) and separately share whatever specific, self-tracked evidence exists — inquiries that mentioned the content, invitations referencing it — while being explicit that no peer-reviewed study establishes a direct causal enrollment effect for this specific case.** Overclaiming a direct causal link the evidence doesn't support would repeat exactly the mistake this lesson's Pitfall box warns against. The stronger, more defensible answer combines a real, well-evidenced mechanism with the instructor's own tracked data, clearly distinguishing which parts of the claim are established research and which parts are this instructor's own preliminary evidence.
