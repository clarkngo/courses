## Why "When I Have Time" Fails

Almost every instructor who tries public content starts with some version of the same unstated plan: keep it flexible, post "when something good comes up" or "when I have a free evening." This plan fails for a predictable reason — it never triggers automatically, because it was never attached to a specific, repeatable cue in the first place.

---

## What Habit Formation Research Actually Shows

**Lally, P., van Jaarsveld, C. H. M., Potts, H. W. W., & Wardle, J. (2010). "How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world." *European Journal of Social Psychology*, 40(6), 998–1009.**

Lally et al. tracked 96 participants forming a new habit (a simple health behavior, tied to an existing daily cue) over 12 weeks, measuring how "automatic" the behavior became over time. Two findings matter directly for content creation:

1. **Habits form faster when tied to an existing, stable cue** — the study design itself relied on this: participants chose a habit to perform at the same daily trigger point (e.g., "after breakfast"), not at a floating, self-selected time.
2. **Automaticity took a median of 66 days to plateau, with a wide range (18 to 254 days)** across participants and behaviors — meaning a habit that feels effortful in week 2 is not a sign it isn't working; it's the expected shape of the curve. Missing a single day did not meaningfully disrupt long-run habit formation, which the authors note runs counter to popular claims that one missed day "resets" the process.

Applied to public content: a posting schedule tied to a vague intention ("when I have time") has no cue to trigger it at all — it's not a fragile habit, it's a non-habit. A schedule tied to something already fixed in your week — the same day you prep a lecture, the same evening you do office hours — has a real cue, and Lally et al.'s finding that a missed instance doesn't reset progress means an occasional skipped week won't undo months of consistency.

<Pitfall title={`"I Need to Post Daily to Build an Audience"`}>

Daily-posting advice is common in creator-economy content, but it's rarely grounded in anything beyond individual anecdote, and it directly conflicts with the realistic time budget of someone teaching full course loads (Module 6 covers this in detail). Lally et al.'s research doesn't say anything about *frequency* being the driver of value — it says **consistency tied to a stable cue** is what makes a habit stick. A well-produced piece every two weeks, tied to a fixed cue in your schedule, is more sustainable — and by the automaticity research, more likely to survive past month three — than a daily habit with no anchor that collapses in week two.

</Pitfall>

## The Minimum Viable Content Habit, Defined

For this course, the minimum viable habit has three fixed parts:

1. **A fixed cue** — an existing point in your week (after you finalize a lecture, during a specific admin block, the same evening each week) that the content task attaches to, rather than a floating intention.
2. **A fixed cadence you can sustain for a full semester without renegotiating it** — biweekly is a realistic default for most instructors; weekly only if your Module 2.1 channel choice is genuinely low-production (a short written post, not an edited video).
3. **A capped production ceiling** — a hard limit on how long any single piece of content is allowed to take, so "make it better" doesn't quietly turn a 45-minute task into a three-hour one.

<CodeLab title="Set Your Fixed Cue and Cadence">

Answer these before Module 3, in writing:

1. What existing, fixed point in your week will this attach to? (Not "evenings" — a specific point: "the hour after I finish my Thursday lecture prep.")
2. What cadence can you commit to for a full 15-week semester, not just this month?
3. What is the maximum time any single piece of content is allowed to take, start to finish?
4. If you miss one scheduled slot, what is your plan — per Lally et al. (2010), the answer should be "resume at the next cue," not "restart the whole habit."

</CodeLab>
