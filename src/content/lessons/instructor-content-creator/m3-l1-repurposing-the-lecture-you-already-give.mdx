## The Asset You're Sitting On

Every week, you already do something most industry experts never learn to do: take something you understand deeply and explain it to someone who doesn't understand it at all yet, in a way they can actually follow. That skill has a name in education research, and understanding it is the key to repurposing your teaching into public content without starting from a blank page.

---

## Pedagogical Content Knowledge

**Shulman, L. S. (1986). "Those who understand: Knowledge growth in teaching." *Educational Researcher*, 15(2), 4–14.**

Shulman argued that expert teaching draws on a distinct kind of knowledge he called **pedagogical content knowledge (PCK)** — separate from *content knowledge* (knowing the subject) and *general pedagogical knowledge* (knowing how to teach in general). PCK is the specific knowledge of **how a particular topic tends to be misunderstood, which analogies and examples reliably work for it, and how to sequence its explanation** for people encountering it for the first time.

This matters directly for content creation because of what it implies: your value as a public creator isn't primarily "I know this subject" — plenty of industry practitioners know their subject and still can't explain it clearly. It's the PCK you've built through repeated teaching: you already know which explanation of a concept lands and which one confuses people, because you've watched it happen in real time, semester after semester, in a way a first-time explainer hasn't.

<Pitfall title="Treating Your Slides as the Content">

The most common repurposing mistake is assuming the *content* is the slide deck or lecture notes, and that public content just means posting those. It's the opposite: the slides are the scaffolding you built *around* your PCK, not the PCK itself. The valuable, transferable part is the specific example that finally makes a concept click, the analogy you reach for when the textbook explanation fails, or the exact point in an explanation where you've learned to slow down because that's where confusion sets in. Public content should capture *that*, not the outline.

</Pitfall>

## Three Reliable Sources for Your First Artifact

You do not need a new idea. You need to notice which existing teaching moment already has PCK embedded in it. Three sources, in order of how ready-to-use they usually are:

1. **The question you've answered more than three times.** If multiple students across multiple semesters have asked the same question, you've already refined your answer through repetition — that refinement *is* PCK. This is usually your fastest-to-produce first piece.
2. **The analogy or example that finally lands.** Every experienced instructor has at least one explanation they know works because they've watched confusion clear on students' faces when they use it. That example, isolated from the rest of the lecture, often stands alone as a complete piece of public content.
3. **The point where you deliberately slow down.** If there's a spot in your lecture where you've learned, through experience, to pause and re-explain, that's a signal the concept is a common misconception — exactly the kind of "how this topic tends to be misunderstood" knowledge Shulman identifies as PCK's core.

<CodeLab title="Extract Your First Artifact">

Using your Module 1 inventory and Module 2 channel choice:

1. Pick one of the three sources above — a repeated question, a landing analogy, or a slow-down point.
2. Write out the explanation exactly as you'd give it in class, out loud if that helps, without editing for a "public" tone yet.
3. Strip out anything that only makes sense with course-specific context (assignment names, your specific syllabus structure) so it stands alone for a stranger.
4. Fit it to your chosen channel from Module 2 — a spoken explanation on video, or a written version if your channel is text-first.
5. Cap the production time at the ceiling you set in Module 2's second lesson.

This becomes your first public artifact — built entirely from material you already have, not invented from scratch.

</CodeLab>

<div>

**The refined analogy, not the polished slide deck.** Shulman's concept of pedagogical content knowledge locates the real teaching expertise in the accumulated, tested knowledge of what actually helps a specific audience understand a specific concept — which is exactly what six semesters of refining one analogy represents. The slide deck is closer to content knowledge plus general structure; it's necessary scaffolding, but it hasn't been tested and refined against real confusion the way the analogy has. A public audience encountering the topic for the first time benefits far more from the piece that's already been proven to work than from a comprehensive but untested overview.
