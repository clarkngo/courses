## After the Action

Modules 1 and 2 were about stopping a bad action *before* it happens — verifying identity, verifying data. Module 3 is about what happens *after* a legitimate action already went through, and someone later disputes it.

That's **Repudiation**: the R in STRIDE.

---

## What Is Repudiation?

To repudiate something is to deny it happened — or deny you're the one who caused it. **Repudiation** as a threat category is about a system's *inability to prove otherwise*. It's not primarily about attackers forging actions (that's spoofing and tampering); it's about a legitimate action happening and the system having no reliable, tamper-evident record of who did what, when, and under what exact instruction.

The defense against repudiation is usually called **non-repudiation**: a record strong enough that the person who took the action can't credibly deny it, and the system can't be accused of having taken an action no one actually requested.

---

## The FinFlow Scenario

`TradingAgent` can sell stock on a user's behalf. Here's the scenario from the talk:

> A user tells `TradingAgent` to sell a stock position. The market moves against them shortly after. They call support and deny ever giving that instruction. FinFlow's logs are vague — a line that says roughly "sell executed" with no clear record of the original wording, the exact time, or a confirmation step the user explicitly approved. There's no way to prove, one way or the other, what actually happened.

Notice this scenario doesn't require an attacker at all. It's the ordinary, foreseeable case of a user having a change of heart after a bad outcome — and a system that can't defend itself because it never built the evidence trail in the first place.

---

## Why This Is an Agent-Specific Problem

- **Natural language is inherently ambiguous.** "Sell my position" could mean all of it or some of it, right now or "when you get a good price." A vague instruction, faithfully logged as vague, doesn't protect anyone — the dispute becomes "what did I actually mean," which a log can't resolve after the fact.
- **Agents compress a conversation into an action.** A human support rep who executes a trade usually leaves a paper trail shaped by a form: this account, this quantity, this price, this signature. An agent can execute the same trade from a loosely worded chat message, with the compression step — turning "sell some Tesla" into `sell_stock("TSLA", 50)` — being exactly where the record needs to be most explicit, and often is least.
- **Autonomy raises the stakes of "who decided this."** If an agent can act without a human confirming each step, "the agent decided to sell" and "the user told the agent to sell" need to be distinguishable after the fact — otherwise disputes have no way to resolve.

---

## Mitigation Strategies

**1. Create an Immutable Audit Trail**
Every sensitive action gets a log entry that can't be quietly edited or deleted after the fact — append-only storage, write-once logging, or a hash-chained log where altering an old entry breaks the chain and is detectable. The point isn't just "we logged it," it's "we logged it in a way where tampering with the log itself would be tamperinng, and Module 2 already taught you how that gets caught."

**2. Require Explicit Confirmation**
Before executing an action with real consequences, have the agent restate exactly what it's about to do — in unambiguous terms — and require the user to explicitly approve that restatement. "Sell some Tesla" becomes "Confirm: sell 50 shares of TSLA at market price?" This does two things at once: it resolves ambiguity *before* acting, and it creates a clean, specific record of what was actually approved.

**3. Use Secure Timestamps**
A timestamp that's just whatever the local system clock said is trivially editable after the fact by anyone with write access to the log. A secure timestamp — signed at the moment of creation, or anchored to a trusted time source — makes "when did this happen" itself part of the tamper-evident record, not a detail an attacker (or a careless retention policy) can quietly adjust.

<Quiz question="TradingAgent's current log entry for a trade reads: 'Executed sell order per user request.' What's missing that would matter if the user later disputed this trade?">

<div>

Almost everything specific: *which* security, *how many* shares, the *exact wording* the user approved, a *timestamp* that can't be silently altered, and — if explicit confirmation was required — a record that the user actually confirmed a precise restatement of the action rather than a vague one.

"Executed sell order per user request" is a log entry that's technically true and practically useless in a dispute. It proves an action category happened; it proves nothing about the specifics anyone would actually be arguing over. A log built for non-repudiation should let a third party, months later, read the entry and know exactly what was requested, exactly what was confirmed, and exactly when — without needing to trust anyone's memory of the conversation.
