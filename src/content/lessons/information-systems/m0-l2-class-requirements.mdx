## Course Overview

This lesson walks through everything you need to know before the first substantive class: how the course is structured, what assignments and assessments look like, and the expectations for participation and performance.

---

## What to Capture

As you watch, note:

- **Grading weights** — what percentage each component (exams, projects, participation) contributes to your final grade
- **Key deadlines** — when major assignments and exams are scheduled
- **Attendance and participation policy** — whether and how it affects your grade
- **Required materials** — textbooks, software, or accounts you need to set up

---

## How to Succeed in an IS Course

Information Systems courses blend conceptual knowledge with applied skills. The students who do best tend to:

1. **Read before class** — IS concepts build on each other; showing up without the background makes lectures harder to follow
2. **Practice with actual tools** — network utilities, database queries, and CMS platforms are learned by doing, not by reading about them
3. **Connect concepts to organizations you know** — when you learn about OLAP, think about how Netflix or Amazon might use it

<Pitfall title="Don't Treat IS as Purely Technical">

A common mistake is approaching Information Systems as a purely technical subject. IS is fundamentally about how organizations use technology to achieve business goals. Every technical concept in this course — databases, networks, CRM — has a business purpose. Keep asking "why does an organization need this?" alongside "how does it work?"

</Pitfall>
