## The Core Distinction

The entire field of Information Systems rests on a single foundational idea: **data and information are not the same thing.**

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Data** | Raw, unprocessed facts and figures | `92, 78, 85, 61, 99` |
| **Information** | Data processed into meaningful context | "The average exam score is 83, with one student at risk" |
| **Knowledge** | Information applied to make decisions | "Students scoring below 70 should receive tutoring before the final" |

Data has no inherent meaning. A number like `37` could be a temperature, an age, a ZIP code suffix, or an inventory count. Context transforms data into information.

---

## The DIKW Pyramid

A common framework for understanding the relationship between data and information is the **DIKW hierarchy**:

- **Data** — raw symbols and signals
- **Information** — data with context and meaning
- **Knowledge** — information internalized and applied
- **Wisdom** — knowledge used to make sound judgments over time

Information Systems are primarily concerned with the first three levels: capturing data, processing it into information, and making that information available for decisions.

---

## Why Organizations Struggle with This

Most organizations have vastly more data than information. A large retailer might collect millions of transaction records daily — but if those records are siloed in different systems, inconsistently formatted, or never analyzed, they remain data. Turning data into actionable information is the core purpose of IS infrastructure.

<Pitfall title="More Data Is Not Always Better">

A common executive instinct is to collect as much data as possible. But unstructured, low-quality, or irrelevant data creates noise, not signal. The question is not "how much data do we have?" but "can we reliably extract information from it when we need it?"

</Pitfall>

---

## Attributes of Good Information

For information to be useful in organizational decision-making, it should be:

- **Accurate** — free of errors
- **Complete** — contains all relevant data points
- **Timely** — available when needed
- **Relevant** — applicable to the decision at hand
- **Consistent** — the same result across systems and reports

<div>

Raw temperature readings are **data**. To become information, they need context: Which patients? At what times? Compared to what baseline? Processed through analytics, those 10,000 readings might yield information like: "37 patients showed fever spikes between 2–4 AM, concentrated in Ward C — investigate for environmental cause." The processing step — aggregation, comparison, contextualization — is what transforms data into information.
