## What Is a CMS?

A **Content Management System (CMS)** is software that allows users to create, manage, and publish digital content — typically web content — without requiring direct knowledge of HTML, CSS, or server configuration.

A CMS separates two concerns that are otherwise intertwined:

- **Content** — the text, images, and media that conveys information
- **Presentation** — the design, layout, and formatting of that content

This separation means a marketing writer can update a product page without touching code, and a developer can redesign the entire site without touching content.

---

## Types of CMS

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Traditional (Coupled)** | CMS manages both content and front-end presentation | WordPress, Drupal, Joomla |
| **Headless** | CMS stores content; front-end is built separately and pulls content via API | Contentful, Sanity, Strapi |
| **SaaS / Hosted** | Fully managed platform including hosting | Squarespace, Wix, Shopify |

---

## How a Traditional CMS Works

1. **Database** — stores content (posts, pages, media) in structured tables
2. **Admin Interface** — a web-based editor where authors create and edit content
3. **Theme/Template Engine** — renders content using pre-designed templates
4. **Front-end** — what the visitor sees; generated dynamically or statically

When a visitor loads a page, the CMS retrieves content from the database, applies the template, and delivers the completed HTML to the browser.

---

## Why Organizations Use a CMS

Without a CMS, every content update requires a developer. This creates a bottleneck: marketing can't publish a blog post until IT deploys it. A CMS eliminates that dependency, enabling:

- **Non-technical content authoring** at scale
- **Consistent branding** enforced by templates
- **Version history and rollback** on content changes
- **Multi-user collaboration** with role-based access control

<Pitfall title="A CMS Is Not a Website Strategy">

Selecting a CMS is a tactical decision. It should follow from a content strategy — what you publish, how often, to whom, and in what formats. Organizations that choose a CMS before defining their content needs often end up with a powerful platform they use at 10% capacity, or a simple platform that can't support what they actually need.

</Pitfall>

---

## CMS in Enterprise IS

In enterprise contexts, CMS platforms often integrate with other systems:

- **CRM** — to personalize content based on customer data
- **DAM (Digital Asset Management)** — for structured storage and retrieval of images, video, and documents
- **Marketing Automation** — to trigger content delivery based on user behavior
