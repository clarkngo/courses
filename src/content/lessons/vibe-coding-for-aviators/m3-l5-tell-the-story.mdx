## 📖 Why a Story Beats a Feature List

Back at the Demo Fair, you already brushed up against this: "I built a quiz" is a fact. "I built a quiz, and someone almost missed the whole point of it until I fixed the hover state" is a story. The second one is the one people actually remember.

That's true everywhere, not just in this room. Nobody funds, hires, or clicks on a spec sheet — they respond to a story about a real problem and a real fix. Pilots know this instinct already: a safety briefing that's just a list of rules gets ignored, but the same rules wrapped in "here's what happens if you skip this" gets remembered. Selling an idea works the same way.

Below are three ways to structure that story. They're not competing — they're tools for different rooms.

---

## 🧩 Structure 1: Problem → Solution → Proof

The classic elevator pitch. Fast, credible, and built for a reader who's skimming — a resume line, a cover letter, an application form.

<CodeLab title="Problem → Solution → Proof Template">

```
Problem:  [Who has this problem, and what's annoying about it?]
Solution: [What you built, in one line]
Proof:    [Evidence it actually works]
```

**Example**, using Clear the Runway from Day 2:

> First-time flyers waiting at a gate have a few idle minutes and nothing to do but stare at the departure board. I built Clear the Runway, a 30-second clicking game where planes pop into random runway slots and you tap them before they vanish. When a classmate tested it cold, they didn't need any instructions — they started clicking within two seconds and asked to play again after their score showed up.

</CodeLab>

Notice the proof isn't a claim ("it's fun!") — it's the actual rose from your Day 3 feedback test. You already collected this evidence; this structure just puts it to work.

**Best for:** resumes, cover letters, written applications — anywhere someone reads fast and wants credibility, not a conversation.

---

## 🌱 Structure 2: Origin Story

A more personal frame: *I noticed X, so I built Y, and here's what changed.* This one centers you — what you saw, what you decided to do about it — which makes it land well when someone's trying to get a sense of who you are, not just what you shipped.

<CodeLab title="Origin Story Template">

```
I noticed [the moment or problem that started it].
So I built [what you made].
Here's what changed: [what happened once someone used it].
```

**Example**, using the Day 1 boarding pass project:

> I noticed most airline apps bury your flight details behind five taps and a login screen, right when you're rushing through security. So I built a boarding pass generator that shows your gate, seat, and boarding time on one clean screen, no login required. Once I had a working version, I asked a friend to find her gate number in it — she found it in under three seconds, which is exactly what a boarding pass should let you do.

</CodeLab>

**Best for:** interviews, "about this project" write-ups on a portfolio site — anywhere someone wants to hear your voice, not just your output.

## 🎤 Structure 3: Full Sales Pitch

Hook → Problem → Demo → Call-to-action. This is the closest to a real stage pitch — built for a live audience with limited attention, where showing beats telling.

<CodeLab title="Full Sales Pitch Template">

```
Hook:            [One line that makes someone want to know more]
Problem:         [Who has it, why it matters]
Demo:            [Show the actual thing working — don't just describe it]
Call-to-action:  [What you want the listener to do next]
```

**Example**, using Clear the Runway on an actual demo-day stage:

> Hook: "Raise your hand if you've ever been stuck at a gate with fifteen minutes and nothing to do." Problem: first-time flyers get bored and bored people get anxious before a flight. Demo: *[open the app, play ten seconds live]* — "see how fast that score climbs? That's the whole game." Call-to-action: "Try it yourself right now — it's on the screen behind me, scan the QR code."

</CodeLab>

The demo beat is what makes this structure different from the other two — you're not describing the proof, you're putting the working thing in front of people and letting them watch it work.

**Best for:** demo days, stage pitches, any live audience where you can actually show the product instead of just describing it.

<Pitfall title="Trying to Use All Three at Once">

Don't staple the origin story, the proof, and a live demo together into one five-minute ramble — that buries the point instead of making it. Pick the structure that fits the room you're actually in, and cut everything the structure doesn't call for. A tight 20-second Problem → Solution → Proof beats a rambling version of all three combined, every time.

</Pitfall>

## 🧭 Which One Do I Use?

| You're in this situation... | Reach for this structure |
|---|---|
| Writing a resume line or cover letter | Problem → Solution → Proof |
| Sitting in an interview, or writing a portfolio page | Origin Story |
| Standing in front of a live audience with the product open | Full Sales Pitch |

## ✍️ Try It

Pick your favorite project from the week and draft one version of its story, using whichever structure matches a situation you can actually picture using it in soon — a college application, a scholarship interview, a future demo day. Keep it to the template. Resist adding more.

<div>

**Problem → Solution → Proof** — it's built for a fast reader who needs credibility in very little space.

An Origin Story needs room to build a personal voice, and a Full Sales Pitch needs a live demo — neither fits a 200-character text box. Problem → Solution → Proof compresses to its essentials by design: who has the problem, what you built, and one piece of evidence it works. That's exactly what a skimming reader with a character limit needs.
