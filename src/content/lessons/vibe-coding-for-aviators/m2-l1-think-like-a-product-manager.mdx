**Who is this for?** Not "everyone" — a real person or type of person. A new classmate. A nervous first-time flyer. Yourself, six months from now.

**What's the ONE thing it has to do well?** Not five things. One. If your quiz app does one thing brilliantly — matches someone to an aircraft — and nothing else, that's a stronger build than an app that half-does five different things.

> Everything past those two answers — colors, animations, extra features — is optional polish. Nice to have, not required.

## 🔬 Why These Two Questions Actually Work

These aren't just study habits — they're backed by how both builders and brains actually work.

**"Who is this for?"** Once you've stared at your own build for twenty minutes, you can't see it fresh anymore — every button makes sense to you because you put it there. Researchers call this the *curse of knowledge*, and it's the single biggest reason builders misjudge their own apps. Naming one specific person — even a made-up one — forces you to imagine someone who doesn't already know what you know. It's also the same idea behind "Jobs to Be Done," a framework real product teams use: people don't just want an app, they're "hiring" it to do a specific job for them.

**"What's the ONE thing it must do well?"** Partly, this is about *you*. Your working memory holds roughly seven things at once — that's measured, not a discipline problem. Every extra feature you try to build at the same time competes for that same slim budget, which is exactly why yesterday's kitchen-sink pitfall produces messier code. Partly, it's about *your user*: one of the most copied rules in software history, the Unix philosophy, is one sentence — "do one thing and do it well." Software that tries to do five things adequately loses to software that does one thing brilliantly, every time.

## 💡 Why This Changes Your Prompt

Compare these two prompts for the same rough idea:

| Without the PM lens | With the PM lens |
|---|---|
| "Build a quiz about airplanes" | "Build a 5-question quiz that matches a first-time flyer to one of four aircraft personality types, based on how they'd want to travel" |
| Vague target, vague outcome | Specific audience, specific single job |

The second prompt is longer, but it's not more complicated — it's more *focused*. Gemini has a clear target to build toward, and you have a clear target to test against.

<Pitfall title="Designing for 'Everyone'">

"Everyone" is not an audience. If you try to build something for everyone, you'll end up compromising on all of it — too simple for the people who wanted depth, too complex for the people who wanted speed. Pick one real person in your head (even a made-up one) and build for them specifically. It's easier to widen a focused build later than to sharpen a vague one.

</Pitfall>

**Who specifically it's for, and the one job it must do well — before anything else.**

"Flight planning helper" could mean a dozen different apps: a checklist for student pilots, a route-distance calculator for hobbyists, a packing list for frequent flyers. None of those are wrong — but trying to build all of them at once produces something that does none of them well. Pick one person and one job, and the rest of your build gets dramatically easier.
