## 🎯 Three Habits, Not a Design Degree

You don't need to study design to build something that feels good to use. Three habits cover most of it:

**Clarity** — there's one clear main action, not six competing buttons. Someone landing on your page for the first time should know instantly what to do.

**Feedback** — something visibly happens when you click. A color change, a message, an animation. Silence after a click makes people think it's broken, even when it worked.

**Hierarchy** — size and color show what matters most. Your main button should look more important than a small footer link, because it *is* more important.

<Pitfall title="Every Button the Same Size and Color">

If every element on the page is the same size, weight, and color, nothing stands out — including the one action you actually want people to take. Pick your single most important action and make it visually louder (bigger, bolder, brighter) than everything else. That's hierarchy doing its job.

</Pitfall>

## 🔀 Choice Point

Before you start building, decide:

- **Iterate** yesterday's project — apply today's PM + UX lens to what already exists, and save it as the next version number (for example, `maria_day1_v1.html` becomes `maria_day2_v2.html`)
- **Start fresh** with a new project, applying the lens from the very beginning — version resets to v1 under a new file name

Today's build is themed: **"Which aircraft matches your flying style?"** — a short quiz that matches the person taking it to one of a few aircraft personality types. If you're iterating yesterday's project instead, that's completely fine — just make sure your PM answers (who it's for, the one thing it does well) are clear before you touch Gemini.
